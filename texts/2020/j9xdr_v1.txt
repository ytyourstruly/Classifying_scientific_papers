Benefitting from trial spacing
Abstract
26 The statistical relation between two events influences the perception of how one event
27 relates to the presence or absence of another. Interestingly, the simultaneous absence of both
28 events, just like their mutual occurrence, is relevant for describing their contingency. In three
29 experiments, we explored the relevance of co-absent events by varying the duration and
30 frequency of trials without stimuli. We used a rapid trial streaming procedure, and found that the
31 perceived association between events is enhanced with increasing frequency of co-absent events
32 unlike the duration of co-absent events which had little effect. These findings suggest ways in
33 which the benefits of trial spacing, during which both events are absent, could be obtained
34 without increasing total training time. Centrally, this can be done by frequent repeating of
35 shortened co-absent events, each marked by a trial contextual cue. We discuss four potential
36 accounts of how co-absent experience might be processed contributing to this effect: i)
37 contingency sensitivity, ii) testing effect, iii) reduced associative interference by the context, and
38 iv) reduced encoding interference.
40 Keywords: trial spacing, distributed practice, intertrial interval, contingency learning, rapid
41 streaming contingency

Introduction
43 Learning the contingency between two stimuli, or a stimulus and a response, is a
44 fundamental form of learning (Baker, Murphy, & Vall√©e-Tourangeau, 1996), involving
45 perceiving, remembering, and learning the correlation between events. Such statistical relations
46 can be positive, in which people learn that the presence of one event predicts another (bacon is a
47 useful predictor of eggs as they are positively correlated in some people‚Äôs breakfast experience),
48 but predictive relations can be negative, where the presence of one event signals the absence of
49 another (toothpaste and wine, blessedly, tend to be experiences that are negatively correlated).
50 While the simultaneous presence of events is clearly relevant for learning a positive relation, and
51 the occurrence of either event alone is relevant for negative correlations, the mutual co-absence
52 of both events is less intuitively relevant for either type of correlation. However, the absence of
53 events is not the absence of information. For example, in determining the cause of stress-related
54 headaches, the absence of stress and headaches might be informative for inferring the relation
55 between stress and headaches (Murphy, Byrom, & Msetfi, 2017). But, how do absences become
56 relevant and inform particular predictive relations? Moreover, tasks that assess human reasoning
57 find that information about absence is difficult to process and use, suggesting an underlying bias
58 against processing these type of experiences. Here, we investigate how manipulating the
59 frequency and duration of co-absent events impacts judgements of perceived association between
60 pairs of stimuli, in other words, how can experience with ‚Äúnothing‚Äù impact judgements about
61 ‚Äúsomething‚Äù.
62 To consider how contingent relations are formed, it is worth considering the two well-
63 established empirical factors for establishing the perception of a relationship between two stimuli
64 (e.g., S1 and S2) which are: (1) the temporal and spatial contiguity, and (2) the statistical

65 dependency or contingency which defines a correlative relationship. The first factor has been
66 identified to be necessary, if not sufficient, for associative learning and memory co-activation
67 (Miller & Barnet, 1993; for a review, see Savastano & Miller, 1998); a stimulus will become
68 associated with other stimuli and be able to retrieve that stimulus if presented in the same

72 positively contingent, there is a greater likelihood of observing S1 and S2 together (i.e.,
73 conjunction or co-occurrence) and absent together (i.e., co-absence). Seeing only one stimulus
74 without the other characterizes negative contingencies (i.e., disjunction). Contingency has been
75 formalised by ‚àÜp (delta p; Allan, 1980), the one-way relation between two binary dichotomous

145 The present investigation, therefore, attempted to understand the role of mutual absent
146 stimuli in contingency learning by manipulating the frequency and duration of only the trials
147 where ‚Äònothing‚Äô, but the experimental trial context, is presented. Our previous finding was
148 surprising because there is strong evidence that increasing the duration between trials in which
149 no events are present, that is, the intertrial intervals (ITIs), increases the strength of a learned
150 association (e.g., Balsam, Drew, & Gallistel, 2010; Escobar, Arcediano, & Miller, 2002; Harris
151 & Bouton, 2020; Harris, Patterson, & Gharaei, 2015; Msetfi, Murphy & Simpson, 2007; Msetfi,
152 Murphy, Simpson, & Kornbrot, 2005; Urcelay, Wheeler, & Miller, 2009). This concept of
153 spacing of training trials is a well-researched phenomenon in psychology, in which learning
154 improves by increasing the intervals between pairings of associated stimuli (e.g., Gibbon,
155 Church, & Meck, 1984), leading to our expectation that increasing either the frequency or
156 duration of D trials would enhance the perceived association.
157 It is also possible that the failure to detect any effect of frequency or duration of D trials
158 (Murphy et al., 2021) may have been due to the rapid presentation procedure itself and its impact
159 on trial salience or relevance. In the previous experiments, the temporal periods between events
160 were of the order of hundreds of milliseconds. The effect of absent events may differ on this time
161 scale. Presenting a participant with intervals in which no stimuli occur may provide a brief
162 period to reset attention (attentional blink; Ophir, Sherman, & Lamy, 2018). Presenting stimulus-
163 absent periods has been claimed to attenuate subsequent stimulus detection (Nieuwenstein,
164 Potter, Theeuwes, 2009). In addition to the temporal differences, the participant‚Äôs expectations of
165 the relevance of an absent stimulus based on the experimental instructions may also play a role.

169 The present experiments aimed to test how absent information affects judgments of
170 association between stimuli. The potential effect of increasing the frequency or duration of the
171 co-absent information can be approached from at least the following four theoretical
172 perspectives. i) Contingency Sensitivity: D trials are perceived along with the other events and
173 combined with them provide a metric of association, but with the metric biased by the lower
174 weight assigned to the low salience D trials in comparison to the other trial types (Wasserman et
175 al., 1993). In this view, ‚àÜp could either be a process or descriptive model (Cheng, 1997; White,
176 2005). ii) Testing Effect: Performance during a test of association is enhanced if information
177 acquired on earlier A, B, and C training trials is retrieved on later A, B, and C training trials,
178 respectively, provided that the immediately preceding trial of that type is no longer in short-term
179 memory (i.e., retrieval practice; Roediger & Butler, 2011); from this perspective, D trials clear
180 STM of task-relevant trials, so, on next relevant trial, information is retrieved from LTM. iii)
181 Reduced Associative Interference by the Context: In addition to the association presumed to
182 form between the two target stimuli, it is assumed that the trial context becomes associated with
183 two nominal stimuli on A, B and C trials. Theories of association assume that on an A trial for
184 instance, both S1 and S2 become associated, but they also associate with the trial context.
186 trial markers). In this manner, the S1-S2 association being acquired may be subject to
187 interference from the associations that each element has with the context. Theories of association
188 such as the Rescorla-Wagner model (Rescorla & Wagner, 1972) assume that cues compete for
Benefitting from trial spacing
189 association (e.g., Vallee-Tourangeau et al., 1998). Increasing the frequency or duration of D
190 trials would be expected to extinguish the context‚Äôs associations with S1 and S2, freeing them up
191 to associate with each other (e.g., Bouton, 1993). iv) Reduced Encoding Interference: Other
192 theories assume that memory for the pairing between any two events takes time to process, and
193 increasing the spacing between each A trial permits more time to process each new trial (Engle,
194 2002; Waugh & Norman, 1965). From this perspective, D trials provide a relatively empty
195 period that acts as a temporary protector of information active in short-term memory (STM)
196 which boosts later long-term memory (LTM) encoding or retrieval of the association (Aust,
197 Haaf, & Stahl, 2019). Notably, these four mechanisms by which the increase of context alone
198 presentation might facilitate learning and memory are not necessarily mutually exclusive.
Experiment 1
200 The objective of Experiment 1 was to assess whether frequency and duration of D trials
201 are involved in perception of association. We manipulated duration and frequency of D trials
202 (co-absent stimuli), and introduced a retention interval between stimulus presentation and
203 testing. The retention manipulation was included because there is some evidence that immediate
204 testing following training is not as sensitive to the benefit of trial spacing as testing following a
205 retention interval (e.g., Smith & Kimball, 2010). Additionally, research has found that retention
206 intervals, just like context changes, reduce retroactive interference (e.g., Rosas, Vila, Lugo, &
207 L√≥pez, 2001). Therefore, by introducing an interval between training and testing (the retention
208 interval), we may reduce interference among the various trial types in the experiment and
209 perhaps induce a D trial effect. However, it is possible that a retention interval produces an
210 increase in subjective similarity between events; hence, an overall decrease in sensitivity to
211 discriminable contingencies is also possible (King, Jones, Pearlman, Tishman, & Felix, 2002).
Benefitting from trial spacing
212 Previous work using a predictive judgment task with partial reinforcement (only A and B trials)
213 with a longer retention interval (48 hrs) found evidence supporting a primacy effect over a
214 recency effect when there was a retrieval interval, but a recency over primacy effect when there
215 was no retention interval (Stout, Amundson, & Miller, 2005). During the retention interval, we
216 introduced a secondary irrelevant cognitive task to reduce the likelihood that the retention
217 interval was not treated as relevant to the contingency task.

302 We used linear mixed models (LMM) to analyse the ratings. The Full model included

306 + (frequency + duration) * retention + cond_order + (1 + retention + frequency + duration |

General Discussion
663 These experiments explored the effect of presenting trials without stimuli on learning an
664 association between two absent target stimuli. We demonstrated the surprising result that
665 frequency of repeated absent event trials is more predictive of performance than overall duration
666 of those trials. Experiment 1 found an effect of D trial (co-absent stimuli) frequency, but not D
667 trial duration, on judgments of a single type of contingency (zero contingency: no correlation
668 between the two stimuli). Increasing D-trial frequency enhanced the perceived relation between
669 the two stimuli. This result is consistent with an account for contingency learning in which both
670 the presentation of the two target stimuli and their absence increases their perceived association
671 (Allan, 1980). The result is also consistent with an increase in response criterion as defined in
672 signal detection theory (SDT), however SDT discriminability does not describe correctly our
674 of D trial duration nor the introduction of a 3-min delay (retention interval) between learning and
675 judgments resulted in any change in the expression of the learned relationship. Experiment 2
676 extended the findings to positive and negative baseline contingencies, where the trial frequency
677 effect was replicated. Experiment 3 provided a control for overall D trial duration that corrected
678 for increases in D trial frequency potentially having a corresponding effect on overall duration.
679 Here, the D trial frequency effect was found to be independent of changes in overall D trial
680 duration. The consistent result of the three experiments is that increasing the frequency of co-
681 absent stimuli (D trials) enhances the perceived association between the two stimuli.

682 The enhanced perceived association produced by D trials may be understood as an
683 increase in contingency discriminability (d‚Äô). Applying SDT to contingency judgments results
684 (Allan, Hannah, Crump, & Siegel, 2008; Perales et al., 2005; Siegel et al., 2009), participants are
685 assumed to use experience with the trial types to detect positive or negative contingencies. In the
686 case of negative contingencies, increasing the D-trial frequency should have little effect on the
687 proportion of hits [hit / (hit + miss)], but the proportion of false alarms [false alarm / (false alarm
689 A2). The effect of this would be to decrease d‚Äô as the number of co-absent trials increases.
690 Furthermore, in positive contingencies, the proportion of hits should increase and the proportion
691 of false alarms should remain stable, which would lead to an increase in d‚Äô as the number of co-
692 absent trials increase. In summary, and contrary to our results, SDT predicts a decrease in d‚Äô as a
693 function of the number of D trials for negative contingencies (see also Mercier & Parr, 1996).
696 A1) owing to an increase in the proportion of false alarms.
697 Memory theory also explains aspects of the present results. Testing memory for single
698 items, Hintzman (1970) demonstrated that frequency of an item was more relevant for memory
699 performance than the duration of exposure to that item. Those experiments varied frequency and
700 duration of target item exposure and found that total-time affected neither recall nor recognition
701 memory performance, but the frequency of presentation did (see also Hintzman, 2010). In our
702 case, the frequency effect applied to negative, zero and positive baseline contingencies, but was
703 most robust with negative baseline contingencies. Finally, we found that increasing the
704 frequency and reducing the duration of co-absent stimulus trials (maintaining the same overall
Benefitting from trial spacing
705 duration) did not attenuate the effect of D trial frequency. Next, we describe four different
706 accounts for how more or longer absent events, so-called D trials, have been hypothesized to
707 influence contingency ratings: i) contingency sensitivity, ii) testing effect, iii) reduced
708 associative interference by the context, and iv) reduced encoding interference.
709 Contingency Sensitivity. The first account of the trial spacing effect is that the mutual
710 absence of the two stimuli (D trials) is information supportive of the two stimuli being related, as
711 indicated by ‚àÜp. This account may be referred as the process model of ‚àÜp (Cheng, 1997; White,
712 2004). In this framework, learning involves four types of trials and it is the difference in the
713 proportions of the trials in which one event occurs conditional on the other (A/[A+B]) and the
714 proportion of the trials in which one event occurs conditional on the absence of the other
715 (C/[C+D]). Increasing the D trials has the effect of reducing the latter proportion, thereby
716 increasing the objective contingency (e.g., Allan, 1980; Msefti et al., 2005).
717 Testing Effect. The second account is the testing effect which is a boost in test
718 performance as a result of retrieval from LTM on a prior test (Karpicke, & Roediger, 2008). This
719 explanation assumes that ‚Äòirrelevant‚Äô D trial experience (at least partially) clears STM of task-
720 relevant information (i.e., A, B, or C trial) so that on the next relevant trial previously acquired
721 relevant information is retrieved from LTM as opposed to its already being in STM as a result of
722 a recent prior trial. From this perspective, each D trial provide a mask for STM and forces
723 retrieval from LTM on the subsequent trial, which in turn boosts later retrieval from LTM of A-,
724 B-, and C-trial information on later A-, B-, and C-trials, respectively (Aust, Haaf, & Stahl, 2019).
725 This retrieval practice mechanism is the presumed basis of the Testing Effect (e.g., Roediger &
726 Butler, 2011) and may explain our enhanced effect, although the effect does not differentiate
727 between frequency and duration and so does not anticipate our frequency-superiority effect.
Benefitting from trial spacing
728 Associative Interference by the Context. A third account of the trial spacing effect
729 suggests that during each A trial people learn associations between the two presented stimuli,
730 and on B and C trials the stimuli are associated with the trial context, where the trial context is
731 the set of stimuli unique to the experimental situation (e.g., the rectangular frames in the present
732 research). This context becomes associated with both B and C events, and ultimately
733 presentation of the context alone may retrieve memories of the individual stimuli. One
734 consequence of these context associations is that representations of the two stimuli (S1 and S2)
735 in each trial may be retrieved by the context on A trials and interfere with further learning of the
736 target association (Bouton, 1993; Miller & Escobar, 2002). However, to the extent that the
737 context is extinguished during D trials, these associations might weaken and consequently
738 interfere less with A-trial learning. Therefore, increasing the frequency or duration of D trials
739 would be expected to reduce the effect of interference arising from context learning.
740 Reduced Encoding Interference. A fourth account is that including ‚Äòempty‚Äô D events in
741 each training condition increases the opportunity to further process the information from the
742 preceding A, B or C trial before the next one of these trials begins, thereby providing more
743 available cognitive resources and allowing better processing of the content of each trial (Waugh
744 & Norman, 1965). Our rapidly presented stimuli are presumably processed in STM which has
745 limited capacity (Cowan, 2008). Thus, the faster a continuing stream of information enters STM,
746 the less time is available to process each new stimulus event. This is apt to decrease attentional
747 control and hence increase proactive interference (Engle, 2002). The view here is that slowing
748 down the input of information allows participants more time to consolidate the content of each
749 successive trial into LTM (McGaugh, 1966).

776 Probabilities versus rates
777 It was surprising to us that we did not find an effect of duration on judgments of
778 association given that duration is well known to play a fundamental role in learning (Balsam &
779 Gallistel, 2009; Miller & Barnet, 1993). We briefly summarize three families of learning models
780 which treat time in different ways (for a more detailed review of the first two, see Bonardi,
781 Cheung, Mondrag√≥n, & Tam, 2016). First, there are time-accumulation models (e.g., Gallistel &
782 Gibbon, 2000; Gibbon & Balsam, 1981), in which events are accumulated in a period of times
783 (i.e., rates). Second, there are trial-based models (e.g., Rescorla & Wagner, 1972) which do not
784 explicitly code the duration of events; this appears problematic given the often-reported
785 relevance of temporal information as a learning factor in both humans and animals (e.g., Buritic√°
786 & Alcal√°, 2019; Nasser & Delamater, 2016). However, a number of attempts to integrate timing
787 into the classic trial-based models have been suggested (e.g., Delamater, Desouza, Rivkin, &
788 Derman, 2014; Donahoe, Burgos, & Palmer, 1993; Gershman, 2015; Ghirlanda, Lind, & Enquist,
789 2020; Luzardo, Alonso, & Mondragon, 2017). Third, there are behavioural timing models (e.g.,
790 Killeen & Fetterman, 1988; Machado, 1997), in which an agent‚Äôs internal states (behavioural
791 states) serve as events that are associated with moments in the environment at a particular time.
792 Our results support a trial-based approach of learning where trial duration has no clear effect and
793 the critical factor is how many times an event is presented. The difference between multiple trials
794 and a long duration is the repeated change of the state of the cues, from off to on or on to off
795 which may itself support learning. There is evidence that transitions are more informative than
Benefitting from trial spacing
796 steady states (Hintzman, 1970), and that transitions are themselves cues for learning (Murphy,
797 Mondragon & Murphy, 2009).

799 Comparisons between human contingency learning and animal conditioning have been
800 previously suggested to reflect a similar underlying learning process (Baker & Mackintosh,

813 An important point concerning D trials, consistent with Murphy et al.‚Äôs (2021) findings
814 concerning A, B, and C trials, is that we found enhanced association ratings even when the
815 increased frequency of D trials was accompanied by a proportionately shorter duration of the D
816 trials. It remains unclear whether this effect would also occur in paradigms that are not built on
817 an explicit frequency basis, as was the case in the present streaming task (Allan et al., 2008). For
818 instance, as previously mentioned, the ‚àÜp formulation has been criticised because of the view

819 that counting of non-occurring events may be problematic given that it is not obvious how

821 Shahan, 2014), other than by laboratory strategies such as we used here. As an adapted example
822 taken from Gallistel et al. (2014), it is difficult to imagine how many absences of earthquakes in

824 contingency analyses based on information theory exist in which, instead of counting events,
825 participants use the entropy of the distribution of the intervals between cues and outcomes

832 opportunity for interference from competing trials. At least with contingency learning, increasing
833 trial duration was less effective than providing more frequent, shorter trial bursts.

840 Implications
841 One of the most robust phenomena in the learning literature is the trial spacing effect:
842 greater spacing of training trials results in superior performance on test trials (Vlach, &
843 Sandhofer, 2021). In applied situations such as educational and psychotherapeutic settings, one
844 challenge to the spacing of training trials is that spacing training ordinarily increases the total
845 time needed for training. The present findings suggest that frequency of D trials rather than D
846 trial duration is critical for an enhanced perception of association. If we believe that there is a
847 fundamental similarity between trial spacing and co-absent events, this would suggest that the
848 benefit of trial spacing is not due to time per se between training trials, but the number of co-
849 absent events that agent‚Äôs perceive between training trials. Hence, more intertrial events (i.e., D
850 trials) can be added without an extra cost in training time by making these events shorter.
851 However, this generalization should be entertained with caution. Given that trial spacing in many
852 other situations is seemingly time dependent and our findings suggest that duration does not
853 plays a critical role, trial spacing and number of co-absent events may act through fundamentally
854 different underlying processes. If this were the case, generalizing our findings to other
855 preparations would be premature.
856 Conclusions
857 Our results support the view that humans use the mutual absence of stimuli (D trials) to
858 guide their judgments of association (i.e., contingency). Furthermore, it is possible to enhance
859 perceived contingencies between two stimuli by increasing the frequency (but not the duration)
860 of D trials, even when these trials have a short duration, a frequency-superiority effect. The
861 results support accounts of the benefit of co-absence events based on (a) participants tracking the
862 Œîp contingency, and (b) reduced associative interference with information concerning A trials by
Benefitting from trial spacing
863 the context. These findings have potential applications for boosting learning without additional
864 explicit pairing of stimuli or lengthening of training session.
865 Context
866 One of the most ubiquitous phenomena in Psychology is the trial spacing effect; spaced
867 training trials result in better learning and retention. However, the cost of spacing training trials
868 is that training sessions must be longer or there have to be more training sessions. In contingency
869 learning sessions, trials with no cues or outcomes (D trials), are similar to standard intertrial
870 intervals (ITIs). Different theories have been proposed to explain why human and non-human
871 animals learn better with longer intervals between trials. Although the very term 'intertrial
872 interval' focuses on the duration of the interval, typically the duration of the interval is
873 confounded with seemingly irrelevant events that occur within the ITIs. In the present research,
874 we dissociated ITI duration from inter trial frequency (the ITF), and found that frequency of D
875 trials rather than duration of D trials is what enhances perceived association, at least in
876 contingency learning. Thus, stronger associative learning can be obtained by increasing D trials
877 while making the D trials proportionally shorter.
