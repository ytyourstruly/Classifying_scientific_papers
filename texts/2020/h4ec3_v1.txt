RUNNING HEAD: A dynamic adjustment account of word skipping
Abstract
Prevalent models of eye movement control in reading of word-spaced orthographies assume a
discrete control account to explain word skipping. Accordingly, word skipping results from
advanced parafoveal word activation during preview, leading to the selection of word n+2 as a
saccade target. Consequently, visual and linguistic content of the parafoveal word affect which
word is selected as a saccade target. In contrast, according to dynamic adjustment view, saccade
lengths in reading are affected by current and next word properties in a continuous manner. First,
these predictions were confirmed via simulations for representative models of discrete and
dynamic control. Then results of three gaze-contingent invisible boundary experiments studying
the visuo-attentional mechanism of skipping are being reported. The results support the dynamic
adjustment view by showing a unimodal landing position distribution across a candidate word for
skipping and its subsequent word, and that the content of parafoveal word modulates the saccade
length only slightly and in a continuous manner. In addition, the location of a visual anomaly
within a word had no effect on saccade length, suggesting that parafoveal vision of next word is
subject of attention enhancement early enough to affect saccade length computation.
Keywords: word skipping, parafoveal preview, eye movement control in reading

1.1 Discrete or dynamic adjustment control of saccade lengths?
Despite of considerable differences, prevalent models of eye movement control in reading
of word-spaced orthographies (Reichle et al., 1999; Reichle & Sheridan, 2015, Engbert et al.,
2005; Risse et al., 2014; Reilly & Radach, 2006; Snell et al., 2018b; see also Brysbaert & Vitu,
1998) share the assumption of a two-stage process concerning the computation of progressive
inter-word saccade length: First, lexical processes related to current and upcoming words
determine which word will be selected as a target word for a saccade. The saccade is then targeted
to the center of the selected word, as the center provides an optimal viewing position for parallel
letter processing during word recognition (McConkie et al., 1988). This intended saccade length is
affected by a systematic error towards preferred saccade length of seven letters (McConkie et al.,
1988). The resulting saccade range effect (or systematic error) has been implemented into most
computational models, including EZ Reader (Reichle et al., 1999; Reichle & Sheridan, 2015),
SWIFT (Engbert et al., 2005; Risse et al., 2014), Glenmore (Reilly & Radach, 2006), and OB1
(Snell et al., 2018b). Finally, random oculomotor error proportional to the intended saccade
length is added. Due to the initial target word selection -stage, the above depicted traditional view
is labelled hereafter as discrete control account of saccade length.
However, selecting saccade target word on the basis of lexical information is difficult in
comparison to planning saccades on the basis of readily available lower-level information, such as
word spaces. First of all, the vision gradient (i.e. combination of visual acuity and visual crowding
[Bouma, 1970] influences), is the main determinant of saccade lengths as indexed by the effect of
word‚Äôs spatial width on relative landing position, skipping and refixation probabilities (Hautala et
al., 2011; Hautala & Loberg, 2015; McDonald, 2006; Hermena et al., 2017). The somewhat blurry
parafoveal preview may nevertheless activate representations at multiple levels of the cognitive

hierarchy, from the visual to the semantic (Schotter et al., 2012). However, the eyes seem not to
wait for the possible activation of high-level representations of previewed word but launch the
next saccade primarily contingent on the recognition of the foveal word (for a review, see Schotter
et al., 2012; Brothers et al., 2017). This induces a great time pressure for parafoveal activations to
affect saccade programming, possibly impeding the readers‚Äô ability to make routinely lexical-level
skipping decisions.
A logical alternative to the discrete control is a dynamic control of saccade length,
suggesting that saccade length is dynamically adjusted according to the properties of current and
upcoming words. The foundation of a dynamic control lies in the critique of the saccadic range
effect (Nuthmann et al., 2016; Cutter et al., 2017). It has been criticized that McConkie et al.
(1988) studied only interword saccades from word n to word n+1, therefore neglecting the
influence of refixations and skipping saccades on the saccade length computation (see Yao-N'Dr√©
et al., 2014). Vitu and colleagues have thoroughly analyzed saccade control in reading including
also refixation and skipping saccades by predicting saccade length or landing position with launch
distance to next word and its length (Albrengues et al., 2019; Yang & Vitu, 2007; Yao-N'Dr√© et
al., 2014). The conclusion that can be drawn from these studies is that readers tend to proceed with
much more uniform saccade lengths than what would be expected on the basis of interword
saccades only. Moreover, Vitu has argued that saccade lengths are dynamically adjusted, that is, in
a continuous manner without the involvement of a target word selection process, even in word-
spaced orthographies (Yang & Vitu, 2007). In fact, there is also direct empirical evidence that
linguistic properties of upcoming word are used at very least to fine-tune interword saccade
lengths also in alphabetic orthographies (Hy√∂n√§ et al., 2018; Radach et al., 2004; Albrengues et
al., 2019). Importantly, it should be noted that the dynamic adjustment models already exist for
non-spaced orthographies, such as Chinese (for a computational model, see: Liu et al., 2017a-b).

1.4. Determinants of word skipping.
Considerable amount of research has investigated to which extent skipping depends on the
parafoveal processing of next word. To avoid possibly detrimental effects of odd textual
information for overall eye movement dynamics, most of these studies have utilized a gaze-
contingent boundary paradigm, in which target words are shown with a preview mask
disappearing during a saccade that crosses the invisible boundary located at the end of the

preceding word (Choi & Gordon, 2014; Drieghe, 2008; Gordon et al., 2013; Reingold & Rayner,
2006; Plummer & Rayner, 2012).
First, it is well known that the majority of word skipping result from the general
oculomotor mechanisms discussed above, pushing the eyes to land just over a short word when
launched from nearby (since McConkie et al., 1988, 1989). In addition, skipping is also affected
by visual properties of the next word. Depending on their relevancy to the reading task, visually
highly anomalous word-objects may be skipped with a very high (Reingold et al., 2016) or low
probability (Drieghe, 2008; Gagl et al., 2014). These strong effects provide evidence that at least
low-frequency visuospatial information available first in the parafovea, can be used to select
saccade targets in reading-like tasks.
Extraction of high-frequency visuospatial information from parafoveal word is
compromised due to poor visual acuity. Concerning word skipping, ~5 % effect size has been
observed for faint font manipulation (Drieghe, 2008; Warrington et al., 2018). In addition, faint
font preview did not affect later foveal fixation durations of young readers (Warrington et al.,
2018), suggesting that the effects of subtle visual previews may be restricted on the early stages of
word recognition (see Reingold & Rayner, 2006).
Concerning linguistic determinants of skipping, according to the review by Brysbaert and
Vitu (1998), word frequency affects skipping rates by approximately 5% (e.g., Angele & Rayner,
2013; Angele et al., 2014) and predictability by approximately 8% (Fitzsimmons & Drieghe,
2013; Rayner et al., 2004; Slattery & Yates, 2018). Obstructing parafoveal lexical recognition
whatsoever by nonword presentation preserving overall word shape reduces skipping rates
approximately 10% (Choi & Gordon, 2014; Drieghe, 2008; Gordon et al., 2013; Plummer &
Rayner, 2012). Yet, nonword previews do disturb lexical processing initiated by the parafoveal
preview, as evidenced by inflated next fixation durations and regression rates on the target word

(Choi & Gordon, 2014; Drieghe, 2008; Gordon et al., 2013; Reingold & Rayner, 2006; Plummer

1.4. Attentional mechanisms underlying skipping.
A possible yet an unlikely scenario is that skipping is predominantly driven by vision
gradient, which mechanism should stress the importance of word beginning information in
saccade length modulation. On the other hand, it is well known that covert shift of presaccadic
attention provide temporary reduction in the crowding of the target area (for a review, see Li et al.,
2016), which explains a large part the preview benefit observed in reading (Briihl & Inhoff, 1995;
Johnson et al., 2007; see Schotter et al., 2012). However, presaccadic attention occurs too late to
modulate saccade lengths. A form of attention that is more potent to contribute to saccade-length
modulation is the early parallel processing of words (Inhoff & Radach, 2014; Inhoff et al., 2005;
Snell et al., 2018a, 2019; Wang et al., 2009). This process may explain why skipping is slightly
affected by the holistic linguistic properties of words, such as word frequency.
Few studies utilizing invisible boundary paradigm seem particularly informative about the
attentional mechanisms involved in skipping. Gagl et al. (2014) found that skipping was inhibited
similarly by previews, in which either the beginning or end of a word was subject of clear visual
degradation. The manipulation was also associated with inflated foveal fixation durations,

suggesting that the degradation also interfered with higher-level orthographic and lexical
processes. Hermena et al. (2016) reported tentative evidence that skipping is insensitive to subtle
diacritical marks of the upcoming word in reading Arabic, while the same diacritics resulted in
clear preview benefits when the words were later fixated. This finding suggests that the visual
information that is available for saccadic modulation is functionally poorer than that later
extracted via presaccadic attention. However, skipping rates are generally low in Arabic and also
insensitive to parafoveal word frequency, making this complex orthography not an ideal testbed
for studying skipping (Hermena et al., 2018).
1.5. The present study
To study whether word skipping is better explained either by the discrete or the dynamic
control view or perhaps by the vision gradient, series of gaze-contingent sentence reading
experiments with invisible boundary manipulation were run. Skipping was maximized by
presenting spatially narrow 3-5 letter (yet unpredictable) target words (Hautala et al., 2011;
Hautala & Loberg, 2015; Hermena et al., 2017) surrounded by substantially longer words. This
configuration enable testing whether there is a bimodal landing position across word n+1 and n+2,
as predicted by the discrete control, but not by the dynamic adjustment account.
To study which type of parafoveal word information govern the modulation of saccade
length leading to word skipping, target words in sentences were previewed either as identical,
nonwords or containing subtle visual extra dots located either beginning or end of the item. Here
the dynamic and discrete control views differ how they assume information affecting saccades:
According to discrete control view, anomalous previews reduce the latter peak of the bimodal
landing position distribution. On the basis of previous empirical results, dynamic adjustment
should result in leftward shift of the unimodal landing position distribution due to anomalous
previews.

Concerning the involvement of attentional mechanisms in skipping, following alternative
hypotheses could be stated: If the preview information utilized in saccade length computation is
predominantly constrained by the vision gradient, the visual dot located in the beginning part of a
word should have a stronger influence on saccade length compared to one in the end part.
However, if the early perception of candidate words for skipping is already enhanced by attention,
the location of the visual anomaly should not have any effect (Gagl et al., 2014).
Finally, the dynamics of saccade control was studied in a post-hoc analysis. According to
the discrete control, the attentional processing of the next word or decision to skip it should be
reflected in previous fixation duration especially at short launch distances, because the processing
of next word has more likely proceeded to the level of affecting saccade target selection (Reichle
& Drieghe, 2013; Schad & Engbert, 2012). If no such interactions are observed, preview exerts an
independent influence on saccadic programming, presumably via parallel attention gradient (e.g.
Snell et al., 2019). This issue is studied in a post-hoc manner for collapsed data across
experiments.

2.6. Procedure
Eye dominance was determined by the distance hole-in-the-paper test (see Rice et al.,
2008), and only the dominant eye was tracked. There were no effects of the tracked eye on the
landing position on the target words over the course of the three experiments. Participants whose
right eye was tracked launched their saccade toward the target word an average of four pixels
farther than the participants whose left eye was tracked. The tracked eye had no interaction with
the experimental conditions. Text was shown centered on the screen with proportional Arial 16
point font. The horizontal width of one letter was by average 3.1 millimeters, which corresponds
to 0.291 visual degrees and 11.7 pixels at the middle of the screen. The width of a word space was
3 mm. Although some sentences covered almost the entire screen width, the target words were
always located within the central 30 degrees of the screen.
To prevent sentence and word properties from producing effects between the four
experimental conditions, each target word received every type of parafoveal preview according to
a Latin-square rotation of the preview conditions, with the restriction that the same type of
preview was not presented for both the first and the second target word in a sentence. In

2.7.1. Independent and dependent measures.
For all of the measures, only cases including a first-pass progression from T-1 to T or to
T+1 were included in the analyses. First-pass skip is a case when a previous fixation was located
at T-1 and the next fixation was located at T+1, prior to the fixating of later words in the sentence.
Total skip is a case when a target word was not fixated at all. Previous fixation duration is the
duration of a fixation located at T-1 immediately preceding a first-pass skip or a fixation landing
on the target word. Launch distance is the difference (in pixels) between the previous fixation
position and the beginning of a word space preceding the target word, which was also the location
of the invisible boundary. Saccade length is the difference between the preceding and subsequent
fixations in angular degrees. In the relative landing position to the target word, values below 1
corresponded to T, and values above 1 to T+1, indicating how far beyond the target word the
skipping saccade landed. Next fixation duration is the duration of the first fixation on T or T+1.
This measure of preview benefit was selected instead of a more commonly used first fixation
duration on T, for which high skipping rate lead to low number of observations. Additional
fixation on the target word is a combined measure of a refixation on the target word and an
immediate regression to the target word after skipping. This measure was used because separate
analyses of post-skipping refixation and regression measures would have suffered from reduced
power in relation to the skipping probability analyses.

4. Discussion
RUNNING HEAD: A dynamic adjustment account of word skipping
Word skipping has been widely considered as a central phenomenon for the theoretical
understanding of eye-movement control in reading (Reichle & Drieghe, 2013). To explain
linguistic level effects on word skipping, prevalent models have relied on the discrete control
assumption, i.e. the selection of a saccade target word on the basis of parafoveal lexical processing
(Engbert et al., 2005; Reichle et al., 1999; Reilly & Radach, 2006; Snell et al., 2018b). In contrast,
according to the dynamic adjustment views (Cutter et al., 2017, 2018; Liu et al., 2017a‚Äìb),
saccade length is computed in a continuous manner as a function of current and next word
properties. Here, it was shown by simulations that a prominent discrete control model, EZ Reader,
predict bimodal landing position across a candidate word for skipping and its subsequent word,
and that linguistic properties of the candidate word affect which one of these words will become
the saccade target. Instead, a dynamic adjustment model (CBSL) predicted unimodal landing
position distribution, and previous empirical results provided basis to predict small continuous
influence of upcoming word on landing position. Finally, the general prediction of attentional
involvement in skipping was studied by testing whether the vision gradient over candidate words
for skipping is subject of early attentional enhancement capable to affect saccade planning. These
predictions were studied in three boundary experiments, in which likely-to-be skipped short target
words were shown with varying previews, being either intact, nonword neighbors to target words,
or containing an extra visual dot located either in the beginning part or end part of the word. As
will be discussed, the results generally favored the dynamic adjustment view over the discrete
control explanation.
4.2. Dynamic or Discrete control of Saccades?
Consistent with the CBSL, but not with the EZ Reader prediction, a unimodal landing
position distribution was found across target and its subsequent word. This result is in line with
previous empirical findings studying landing positions across word spaces (Albrengues et al.,
2019) and the well-known property of landing position distributions being truncated at word
RUNNING HEAD: A dynamic adjustment account of word skipping
boundaries (since McConkie et al., 1988, 1989). Neither other signs of discrete control (Engbert et
al., 2005; Reichle et al., 2017; Reilly & Radach, 2006; Snell et al., 2018b) were observed: The
anomalous previews did not reduce the probability to select word n+2 as a saccade target, and the
preview effects were not conditional on near launch distance or long previous fixation duration
(Eskenazi & Folk, 2015; Rayner et al., 2011; White, 2007). Note that the serial EZ Reader model
(Reichle et al., 1999) predict inflated fixation durations prior skipping due to saccadic
preplanning, whereas parallel attention models predict that processing of next word may affect
fixation duration prior skipping (Engbert et al., 2005; Reilly & Radach, 2006; Snell et al., 2018b).
Instead, the present results favor the view that the parafoveal perception induce a time-
independent influence on saccade lengths (Schotter et al., 2012; Brothers et al., 2017). However,
this aspect of our result should be taken with caution due to reliance of post-hoc data selection
procedure of effective and non-effective preview conditions. Taken together, the present results
provide support that dynamic adjustment of saccade length is a potent mechanism to explain also
word skipping in reading, previously thought to require discrete selection of a target word. In other
words, skipping saccades seem not to be categorically different from other progressive saccades.
4.1. The Nature of Parafoveal Preprocessing in Reading.
The possible involvement of attention in saccade length modulation was studied by
comparing the effect of location of the visual dot within the preview of the target word; an early
attentional mechanism should compensate the degrading vision gradient towards larger
eccentricity so that location of the visual dot should not have an effect (see Snell et al., 2019). This
hypothesis was clearly confirmed by the results, suggesting that the parafoveal vision is subject to
attentional enhancement early enough to affect saccade programming (Inhoff & Radach, 2014;
Inhoff et al., 2005; Snell et al., 2018a).
RUNNING HEAD: A dynamic adjustment account of word skipping
To study which type of parafoveal information is used in saccade length modulation
governing skipping, the effects of subtle nonword vs. visual dot previews was compared. Both
discrete and dynamics control views predicted that skipping is affected by both types of previews,
yet with different targeting mechanism: Dynamic adjustment would lead leftward shift in landing
position for anomalous previews, whereas EZ Reader predict reduced probability to target word
n+2. The results confirmed equal small effect of preview types on saccade length modulation
(Choi & Gordon, 2014; Gordon et al., 2013; Plummer & Rayner, 2012; Drieghe, 2008;
Warrington et al., 2018). After the already discovered unimodal landing position distribution, it
was not surprise that the anomalous previews affected saccade length through the dynamic
adjustment mechanism. However, the visual anomalies had to be of sufficient size for being able
to affect saccadic length, which seems to parallel Hermena et al. (2018) finding no skipping
effects for the absence of small diacritic marks in the reading of Arabic. Together, the available
evidence suggests that any detectable information in blurred parafoveal vision is used to guide
saccades in reading. Tentatively, according to predictive coding framework (for a review, see
Huang & Rao, 2011), a preview may first induce a visual-orthographic prediction, which need to
be yet confirmed on the basis of more acuity vision (Friston et al., 2012). Any detected oddity in
preview may intervene the prediction generation and reduce the saccade length to some extent.
Instead, highly familiar preview induces fast and reliable predictions, and lower quality visual
sample may be sufficient to confirm their accuracy.
4.1. The Cognitive Mechanism Underlying Word Skipping in Reading.
Combining the present findings with the previous knowledge of the importance of spatial
width of current (Cutter et al., 2017; 2018) and next word (Hautala et al., 2011; Hautala & Loberg,
2015; Hermena et al., 2017) on saccadic measures, a following dynamic adjustment mechanism
for skipping can be drafted: First, as explicated by the CBSL account (Cutter et al., 2017, 2018;

between center of words n+1 and n+2. Then, this preliminary program is corrected to some extent
by the actual launch distance from word n+1 to word n+2. The results of the present study then
add to this knowledge by showing that the saccade length if also fine-tuned by the preview
processing of the next word. This preview processing seems to be enhanced by parallel attention
gradient, instead of being fully determined by vision gradient (Gagl et al., 2014). If this preview
processing does not encounter anything highly anomalous content (Reingold et al., 2016), its
influence on saccade length is rather small, yet sufficient to produce the small visual and linguistic
level effects on word skipping. Together these mechanisms produce uniform landing position
distribution across words n+1 and n+2.

4.3. Preview Effects on Later Foveal Processing.
After fixing the saccadic program, the processing of a parafoveal word continues, and its
visual perception is further enhanced by the presaccadic attention mechanism (Li et al., 2016;
Rolfs & Carrasco, 2012). In line with previous studies utilizing faint font manipulation (Glaholt et
al., 2014; Reingold & Rayner, 2006; Warrington et al., 2018; White & Staub, 2012), but in
contrast to partial visual degradation manipulations (Gagl et al., 2014), our subtle visual dot

preview was not associated with foveal processing difficulties. Thus, the task-irrelevant visual
dots affected only early level of lexical processing of words, which was overcome rapidly during
later foveal processing (Warrington et al., 2018).
As expected, orthographically illegal previews led to later foveal processing difficulties,
which has been observed in several previous studies (Choi & Gordon, 2014; Gordon et al., 2013;
Hohenstein & Kliegl, 2014; Schotter et al., 2018, 2019; Plummer & Rayner, 2012; Reingold &
Rayner, 2006). Surprisingly, the effect of nonword preview did not yet manifest in the next
fixation duration (on T or including also T+1 word), but appeared first in the increased probability
to make an immediate additional fixation and in later regressions to the target word. A
contributing factor here was the use of visually similar replacement letters, which are known
reduce the effect of nonword preview on foveal measures (Marcet & Perea, 2018 Also, the

present study was not optimized for studying foveal effects due to high skipping rate, which
reduced the number of first-pass fixations. In line with this explanation, there was a trend towards
expected direction in the first fixation duration of target words in experiment 2, where the
skipping rate was lowest.
4.4. Limitations.
It is possible that the discrete control disputed herein depends largely on the distinctiveness
of preview objects controlling the speed and the certainty of parafoveal perception. Supporting
evidence for this view is provided by Reingold and colleagues (2016), who showed that symbol
strings unrelated to the text are highly likely to be skipped during reading. Thus, the caveat with
visual manipulations is that in some circumstances, they may become saccade targets (or non-
targets) themselves. We did not find such an effect; yet, in Experiment 2, there was a trend toward
longer saccade lengths when the anomalous dot was in the end rather than in the beginning part of
a word. Given that the nonword effect was also absent from Experiment 2, it is possible that when

almost every sentence contains something weird, readers start to invest their attentional resources
in detecting these anomalies by trying to fixate every word (see Wotschack, & Kliegl, 2013, for
the strategic control of skipping). Thus, it is recommended that the number of intact and visually
anomalous trials should be balanced in this type of research.
Importantly, the present findings may be rather specific to short words and therefore likely
to represent skipping driven by the visuo-oculomotor mechanism. Instead, the skipping of longer
words is likely to be driven more by higher-level cognitive processes, perhaps by a genuine
discrete control mechanism taking place in rare occasions. In fact, there is evidence that word
predictability has an equal influence on word skipping in short and long words (Rayner et al.,
2011), meaning that the effect is relatively greater in long than short words. Moreover, readers can
also strategically adjust their reading, e.g., by skimming parts of a text or skipping redundant
words or entire parts of sentences.
Methodological particularities of the present study include insertion of two target words
into a single sentence, which lead to long carrier sentences. Despite of high calibration accuracy
values, the long sentences may have induced some extra head movements. The comprehension
questions tried to guide attention towards target words, being therefore often concerned on minor
details. Consequently, few participants scored low in these questions, making it somewhat unsure
if they were fully engaged with reading all of the time. However, their data was kept in the
analyses because exclusion of any participant would had produced a bias in the latin-square
rotation of conditions.
4.5. Conclusions.
The present findings provide clear support for the dynamic adjustment view of saccade
length also in word-spaced orthographies (Cutter et al., 2017, 2018; Anonymous, Preprint), as
previously studied in non-spaced orthography (Liu et al., 2017a‚Äìb, 2018). The dynamic center-

based saccade length model (Cutter et al., 2017, 2018; Anonymous, Preprint) supplemented with
empirical knowledge, was found to provide a highly tenable and parsimonious account of eye
movement control underlying word skipping. Boundary manipulations provided also novel
evidence that attentional enhancement of vision over short next word takes place early enough to
affect fine-tuning of saccadic length. It can be concluded that dynamic adjustment control
provides a viable alternative to discrete control family of models, which assume that saccade
target word selection is driven by lexical processes.
