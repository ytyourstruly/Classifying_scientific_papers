Abstract
This pre-registered meta-analysis examines the discriminant validity of Disintegration (D),
the trait-like conceptualization of psychotic-like experiences and behaviors (PLEBs), in
relation to the HEXACO personality traits. The main finding is that D shows small to
moderate intercorrelations (i.e., range = |.01| - |.31|) ranging within the HEXACO
intercorrelations. The main implication is that D as a most recent operationalization of PLEBs
is not covered by existing taxonomies, but rather suggests its addition, which may allow for
improved predictions of personality outcomes by covering previously neglected variance, or
by capturing new phenomena. Limitations of the primary studies aggregated and the extended
HEXACO plus D model are discussed.
Keywords: HEXACO, Disintegration, HEXACOD, psychotic-like experiences and
behaviors, psychosis-proneness, meta-analysis

Introduction
For several decades, various scholars have proposed the idea of conceptualizing the
dispositional proneness to psychotic-like experiences and behaviors (PLEBs) â€“ otherwise
known as Psychoticism (Eysenck & Eysenck, 1976), Psychosis-Proneness (e.g., Chapman et
al., 1994), Oddity (Watson et al., 2008), Dissociation (Ashton & Lee, 2012; MomiroviÄ‡ et al.,
1993), or Schizotypy (Claridge, 1997; Meehl, 1962, 1990) - as a trait-like dimension of
interindividual differences. Since then, there has been an ongoing debate about the
relationship of PLEBs with existing personality frameworks such as the Big Five (Goldberg,
1990) or HEXACO (Ashton & Lee, 2007; e.g., Ashton & Lee, 2012, 2019; DeYoung et al.,
2012; KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2017; Krueger et al., 2012; Watson et al., 2008). Roughly, three
positions have been proposed:
First, some researchers do not place PLEBs on a shared continuum with established
traits but rather place PLEBs entirely in the space of maladaptive personality (e.g., Krueger et
al., 2012). This separation of PLEBs from the space of normal personality is reflected in
Ashton and Lee's (2019) recommendation that a more comprehensive assessment of
personality should add proneness to PLEBs (such as Psychoticism) if one wish to account not
only for normal variations but also for the maladaptive ones.
Second, based on the finding that four of the five dimensions of the models of
maladaptive personality traits (such as DSM-5 Alternative Model of Personality Disorders,
AMPD) (Krueger & Markon, 2014; Widiger & Simonsen, 2005) correspond to the Big Five
dimensions, some researchers propose that PLEBs should be located within the dimension of
Openness, as its extreme maladaptive pole (e.g., Chmielewski et al., 2014; DeYoung et al.,
2012; ). Thus, this position negates the need for a dimension beyond existing personality
frameworks to explain PLEBs.

The empirical evidence justifying the trait-like character of D, separate from any other
established personality traits, appears contradictory. On the one hand, there is accumulating
evidence that D exhibits many of the characteristics of lexically derived traits. First, there is
support for the dimensionality of D (e.g., Kwapil & Barrantes-Vidal, 2014). Accordingly, D
ranges from non-pathological psychotic-like experiences to clinically relevant psychosis.
Similarly, D is normally distributed in the general population (Barret & Etheridge, 1992;
Hanssen et al., 2006; Johns & van Os, 2001). Thus, D is more common than might be
expected and, more importantly, is not restricted to the domain of psychopathology.
Furthermore, replicability is supported by evidence of both temporal stability (Sanislow et al.,
2009) and cross-cultural stability (Fonseca-Pedrero et al., 2018; KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2022a).
Similarly, D incrementally predicts phenomena such as (non-)psychopathology (RÃ¶ssler et
al., 2011), socio-political attitudes and thinking styles (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ & Keller, 2023), and health-
related behaviors (LazareviÄ‡ et al., 2021).
On the other hand, D has not been found in previous lexical studies (e.g., Ashton et al.,
2004; Lee & Ashton, 2008). Apparently, only the Big Five or HEXACO have been
repeatedly extracted and replicated across cultures and languages (McCrae et al., 2005;
Schmitt et al., 2007; Thielman et al., 2020). The non-appearance of D as a separate factor in

many lexical studies might be the result of decisions made by researchers in terms of the
valence of the adjectives deemed eligible for further analysis. Specifically, most lexical
studies favor descriptive adjectives as personality indicators and do largely neglect adjectives
which may have an evaluative (i.e., negatively valenced) connotation (Ashton & Lee, 2005).
When other potential personality indicators, such as negatively valenced adjectives and type
nouns, are selected, seven or more personality factors that partially correspond to the existing
traits can be extracted (Saucier, 2003; Vader & Saucier, 2024). Among these additional
factors are elements of D. Similarly, Saucier (2019) shows that morality-related terms were
unsystematically labeled as evaluative or neutral in the original personality indicator sets
(e.g., Goldberg, 1990, 1992), thus artificially inhibiting the extraction of an Honesty-
Humility (H) factor. Accordingly, understanding D as a purely pathological phenomenon
leads to classifying D-related terms such as â€œweird," "paranoid," or "crazy" as evaluative,
leading to their rejection. Thus, D could not be found because all terms describing it were
excluded a priori. Other researchers have argued that D-related terms may be too complex to
be encoded in a single term (Watson et al., 2008), or that terms describing D are phenomena
that have only recently become a focus of public awareness to be sufficiently encoded in
natural language (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2016). The latter argument is based on the observation that
the emergence of traits follows socio-cultural developments (Piedmont & Aycock, 2007).
Related to novelty is the proposition that laypersons rarely encounter or experience D-like
phenomena, leading to less frequent linguistic representation and non-emergence when
frequency of use criteria are applied (Ashton & Lee, 2005, 2019).

data structure (i.e., neglecting correlated bivariate trait intercorrelations from primary studies)

Objectives
This meta-analysis examined whether D measured by the DELTA inventory (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡
et al., 2017), qualifies as a separate personality trait dimension beyond the HEXACO,
assessed by the HEXACO-PI-R (Ashton & Lee, 2009; Lee & Ashton, 2018) or whether it is
absorbed. The aim of this study was to summarize the available primary study evidence on
the relationships between HEXACO and D. We assessed the discriminant validity of D,
based on the aggregated domain-level intercorrelations. We also tested whether and to what
extent the discriminant validity is challenged by the proportion of patients, students, and the
language version used which might lead to biased inferences when overlooked. In addition,
we evaluated the robustness of the discriminant validity against methodological fallacies of

primary studies induced by using outdated DELTA versions which include the later rejected
facet of Social Anhedonia , (un)reliability of the measures, reporting of outlier
intercorrelations or dependent intercorrelation matrices, and publication status.
Hypotheses
Hypothesis 1: Overall, we expected that D is distinct from any HEXACO trait. This
will be reflected by the HEXACO-D intercorrelations not exceeding the range of HEXACO
intercorrelations, i.e., we expected each of the six bivariate HEXACO-D intercorrelations
being equal or smaller than the largest absolute bivariate intercorrelation among the
HEXACO traits (H1).
As stated, D could not be adequately integrated into existing personality models (e.g.,
KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2016, 2019; LazareviÄ‡ et al., 2016). Thus, D is unlikely to be absorbed by
other traits as it is a unique dimension. In addition to the primary findings in favor of D also
separating from the HEXACO (e.g., Ashton & Lee, 2012, 2019; KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2022a), we
do expect the same on the meta-analytic level across studies.

intercorrelations, emphasizing the need for additional moderator analyses (H2.X).

Method
This study was designed according to the PRISMA guidelines (Page et al., 2021).
Eligibility Criteria
Eligible documents and data had to meet the following criteria, which we created using
PICO-S (Amir-Behghadami & Janati, 2020), but we omitted Intervention and Comparison
due to the correlational design of this study. Population: There were no restrictions regarding
age, gender, or other sociodemographic characteristics. Outcome: Relevant studies reported
domain-level product-moment intercorrelations between any of the HEXACO traits and D,
based on total scores of the seven scales. Studies reporting only latent factor intercorrelations
were excluded. In cases where adequate intercorrelations could not be extracted, eligible
studies must at least report raw total scores per participant that allow the calculation of the
missing intercorrelations. Eligible studies measured HEXACO traits using either the
HEXACO-PI-R-60 or -100 (Ashton & Lee, 2009; Lee & Ashton, 2018), and D had to be
measured using any version of the DELTA inventory (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2017). Other
conceptualizations of D were rejected because DELTA is the only index that captures the full
breadth of the domain, allowing for a more rigorous test of discriminant validity. Study
Design: There were no restrictions on publication language or publication type, including

Data Extraction and Coding Procedure
The coding scheme was developed based on the PsychOpen CAMA template (Burgard
et al., 2021) to assess descriptive information on four levels in descending order regarding the
reports, the studies conducted, the samples examined, and the respective intercorrelations per
(sub)sample. Pearson product-moment intercorrelations were extracted directly or computed
as effect size measures between DELTA and HEXACO PI-R total scores, as needed.
Similarly, the sample sizes of the primary studies were required to account for the precision
of each study in the calculation of the aggregated intercorrelation matrix. The available
information was coded as fine-grained as possible, for example if a study reported three
separate intercorrelation matrices for three different samples (e.g., KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2022a),
the data were coded at the sub-sample/group level. The proportion of patients, the combined
proportion of individuals with completed and ongoing higher education (proportion of
students, i.e., individuals with > 12 years of education), and the language of the questionnaire
were the hypothesized moderator variables that may affect the discriminant validity of D. The
first two moderators were coded as continuous variables so that studies with mixed samples
could be included in the moderator analysis which otherwise had to be excluded. The greatest

multivariate analysis, which also addressed the issue of the same study reporting multiple
dependent effect sizes (i.e., up to 21 HEXACO-D intercorrelations), we performed the first

step of the two-stage structural equation modeling approach (TSSEM; Cheung, 2014), which
is a form of meta-analytic structural equation modeling (MASEM, Cheung & Chan, 2005).
Accordingly, the intercorrelation matrices are pooled meta-analytically using fixed or
random-effects models (Hedges & Vevea, 1998) and applying maximum likelihood
estimation. The two available models differ in their assumptions about the aggregated effects.
The fixed effects model assumes that there is only one true effect and that all observed
variation in the effects is the result of sampling error. The random effects model (Borenstein
et al., 2009; Hedges, 2016), on the other hand, assumes that each study effect has its own true
population effect, which in turn is part of a supra-population of true effects. The random
effects model seemed appropriate in our case because the scales were used in different target
populations, such as clinical or general population samples.
Jak and Cheung (2020) highlighted the possibility of assessing the heterogeneity of the
aggregated intercorrelations from the first stage of TSSEM through multivariate meta-
regressions. Univariate meta-regression examines the influence of one or more moderators on
a single criterion variable. In the case of multiple criterion variables, the interdependence of
these variables must be considered. Accordingly, the intercorrelations of the primary studies
were nested in samples. In this way, the effect of moderator variables on multiple
intercorrelations can be assessed simultaneously. We modeled three separate multivariate
meta-regression models for each moderator variable, assessing the influence of the latter on
the former. A combined analysis of all moderators in a single model would have suffered
from information loss, as only cases with complete information on all three variables could
be included, artificially limiting the available evidence. Statistical analyses were performed

Sensitivity Analyses
To validate the robustness of the aggregated HEXACO-D intercorrelations to the risk
of bias, we conducted several additional analyses. As the inclusion of the Social Anhedonia
facets in DELTA-10 versions might inflate the X-D intercorrelation, we repeated the main
analysis in the DELTA-9 (i.e., D without Social Anhedonia) and DELTA-10 subgroups. To
assess whether the inclusion of Social Anhedonia leads to different intercorrelation patterns,
we performed a multivariate meta-regression with the DELTA version as the only moderator.
Another source of potential bias was outlier intercorrelation matrices that were only
loosely related to most other matrices. Statistical outliers were identified by multivariate
internally standardized residuals (Viechtbauer & Cheung, 2010) with adjusted p-values
(Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995) due to alpha error inflation. Influential matrices were detected
by Cook's distance values (1977) that exceeded three standard deviations of the mean
distance score. Reports fulfilling both criteria were treated as outliers, and they were
excluded from a repeated analysis.
There are no established methods for assessing publication bias in MASEM (Gnambs
& Schroeders, 2024). One indicator of publication bias is publication status, so it is
recommended to separate the data into peer-reviewed and unpublished. We first compared
the significance tests of the difference scores for both subsets. Secondly, publication status
was included as a moderator variable in a multivariate meta-regression model to assess
whether and to what extent underlying reporting bias, such as reporting overall higher or
lower intercorrelations, might challenge the discriminant validity of D.
We performed a fourth sensitivity analysis assessing the impact of dependent effect
sizes. MASEM assumes independent intercorrelation matrices with the usual

recommendation being to limit each study to contributing only a single matrix (Jak &
Cheung, 2018, 2020). Multiple reported intercorrelation matrices may lead to biased
parameter estimates and standard errors, potentially affecting the assessment of discriminant
validity. Accordingly, we averaged the information of highly intercorrelated intercorrelation
matrices that were based on the same sample and reconducted the analyses with the smaller
dataset.

Discussion
This pre-registered meta-analysis is the first to assess the empirical evidence on
whether the trait-like dimension of PLEBs, conceptualized as D, has discriminant validity
beyond the HEXACO at the meta-analytic level. The main finding of this study is that D
indeed shows small to moderate intercorrelations (i.e., range = |.01| - |.31) that are within the
D from the HEXACO. Furthermore, our findings were robust to methodological flaws of
primary studies such as the inclusion of Social Anhedonia in D measures, outliers, data
moderator effects of sample homogeneity due to patients or students, nor conclusive
translation effects that would challenge the discriminant validity of D. We observed a weak
convergence trend for C-D and X-D which exhibited the largest absolute intercorrelations,
but the difference was insignificant and small compared to the benchmark (âˆ† = |.02|),
max
hardly calling into question the discriminant validity of D. From a theoretical perspective, the
former could be explained by a general impairment of executive control in highly
DOES DISINTEGRATION QUALIFY AS A SEPARATE TRAIT EXTENDING THE HEXACO MODEL? 31
disintegrative individuals, related to low C. Similarly, high levels of D might lead to peer
rejection due to exhibiting strange thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, or to active avoidance of
social interactions due to paranoia or rapid emotional changes, captured by the latter. Similar
patterns have been found empirically, as Malouff et al. (2005) observed extreme levels of D
and Big Five N, and low levels of Big Five Extraversion, A, and C in clinical groups.
To put the current findings into perspective, it is worth bearing in mind that it is not
uncommon to find absolute intercorrelations of up to .40 in meta-analyses of basic
personality traits. For example, a meta-analysis assessing the discriminant validity of D from
the Big Five based their benchmark of .40 on meta-analytically averaged Big Five
intercorrelations from different meta-analyses (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2016). Similarly, several
meta-analyses show that also some HEXACO intercorrelations exceed r = .40 (e.g.,
Moshagen et al., 2019; Pletzer et al., 2020; Zettler et al., 2020). The main point here is not
that the specific value of .40 is or is not a valid benchmark, but rather that it shows that
moderate intercorrelations can be observed empirically, even between the theoretically
distinct Big Five and HEXACO traits. Thus, the observed HEXACO-D intercorrelations fit
into this picture. Moreover, another crucial element that a reader should be aware of when
evaluating the evidence for the discriminant validity of D is that both the Big Five and the
HEXACO model are top-down personality models, meaning that their upper levels (domains)
are empirically based, while their facets are conceptual constructs designed to minimize
overlap between factors. D, on the other hand, is a bottom-up construct par excellence, based
on the results of factor analyses of nearly a thousand indicators (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2017). It is
not constructed to maximize the variance of the higher-order (D) factor, but to be
comprehensive for the domain of PLEBs, without regard to possible overlap with other
personality domains. In fact, it is surprising that such a strategy did not result in more overlap

with models that aim to comprehensively capture personality variation, which is the basic
idea of models based on the lexical approach (e.g., Saucier, 1997; de Vries, 2020).
Contributions to the PLEBs-Personality Debate
Several researchers have challenged the trait-like conceptualization of D, either by
attempting to assign D to existing taxonomies (e.g., DeYoung et al., 2012) or by locating it
outside normal personality space (e.g., Ashton & Lee, 2019; Krueger et al., 2012). Since both
positions would preserve the more parsimonious personality structures of the Big Five or
HEXACO, they are worth discussing.
Several authors have argued that maladaptive and normal personality can be viewed as
opposite poles on a shared personality continuum. They proposed substantial overlap of
positive symptoms of D with extreme levels of O (DeYoung et al., 2012; Dilchert et al.,
2015; Widiger, 2011) and the possibility of conceptualizing psychotic-like phenomena as an
extreme of O and not a distinct trait per se. This assumption was based on the inverse
relationship between positive and negative symptoms of schizotypy and O (Chmielewski &
Watson, 2008; Ross et al., 2002), positive for the former and negative for the latter. Given the
current and previous findings however, we doubt that D nor its indices can be equated with
O. In fact, the O-D intercorrelation was the smallest observed HEXACO-D intercorrelation,
which was close to zero in all analyses. Similarly, we observed the largest difference for O-D
from the benchmark suggesting that D and O are indeed the farthest apart in a combined
HEXACOD framework. The same was found in the meta-analysis of the Big Five model
(KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2016). Due to the domain-level focus of this study, we were unable to
examine Chmielewski et al.'s (2014) proposal to split O into a pure Openness and an Intellect
component. Accordingly, the Intellect facet of O is expected to mask the substantial
relationship between pure O and positive symptoms of D, suggesting conceptual overlap.
However, this proposal has repeatedly failed to withstand empirical evidence in the Big Five

and HEXACO frameworks (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2017, 2022a, RistiÄ‡ et al., 2023). In joint factor
analyses, low intercorrelations are observed between pure O and the aspects of D
corresponding to the positive symptoms of schizotypy, even when controlling for the
inhibitory effects of Intellect. Thus, there is currently no convincing evidence for a fusion of
D and O.
In contrast to the trait-like conceptualization of D, Krueger and colleagues (2012)
conceptualized D as a strictly pathological phenomenon. In this vein, it is interpreted as a
maladaptive personality variation that is limited to clinical contexts. For this reason, we
modeled the proportion of patients as a moderator to investigate whether the intercorrelations
differ depending on the clinical status of the participants. Like the Big Five, the HEXACO
model covers primarily normal personality variation. Like the meta-analysis by KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et
al. (2016), the proportion of patients did not affect the estimates at all, which was robust
across all sensitivity analyses. Surprisingly, scale range restrictions, which we expected to
reduce the absolute intercorrelations depending on the number of patients, had no effect.
However, there were few studies that assessed patients and even smaller proportions of
patients within these studies, limiting the influence of such range restriction effects.
Nevertheless, most of our data consisted of representative samples of the general population
for which we found evidence of the discriminant validity of D. If PLEBs were indeed as rare
or irrelevant as claimed, it would be surprising to find a distinct D dimension indicated by
comparatively small intercorrelations in non-clinical samples. This cannot be explained by D
resulting from mere sampling bias and reflecting only psychopathology or pathological
personality variation. In this context, it is also not surprising that a recent study replicated the
extended Big Five+D model in both clinical and normal samples and showed that the 6-factor
model fit better than more parsimonious structures in which D persisted while the Big Five
factors tended to merge (RistiÄ‡ et al., 2023).

However, there is also the possibility that the distinct D dimension may be caused by
measurement artifacts. Like clinical samples, student samples run the risk of sample
homogeneity leading to reduced absolute intercorrelations, which could lead to false-positive
inferences of discriminant validity. We observed only a single robust effect like scale range
restrictions in the E-D intercorrelation. There were no other consistent patterns indicating a
general tendency for student samples to have reduced absolute intercorrelations. Similar
effects have been previously explained by the higher cognitive abilities of students to better
compensate for the negative effects of D on executive control (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2016).
Accordingly, the reduced absolute E-D intercorrelation can be explained in the same way as
the reduced absolute C-D intercorrelation. If higher cognitive abilities compensate for the
negative effects of D on executive control, then this may also be true for emotional control.
Previous research supports this interpretation, as D has been shown to be associated with
diminished emotion processing (Kerns, 2006) and relates to emotion regulation (BarthelmÃ¤s
& Keller, 2021). In this sense, the higher cognitive abilities of the students reduce these
negative relationships between D and E.
Similarly important was the assessment of bias due to translation, for which we found
no consistent evidence. In particular, the translation effects that did emerge barely scratch the
.40 benchmark by at most .01. Thus, the practical significance of the observed differences
remains questionable. This is supported by the fact that all effects disappeared when
measurement error was taken into account. Since there is evidence for the cross-cultural
replicability of the HEXACO+D model (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2022a), some different
intercorrelations should be expected due to cultural or linguistic differences, but none should
call into question the general separation of D from the HEXACO traits.

Limitations
The first major limitation of this study was the simplified structure of the extended
HEXACOD model (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2022a). Due to focusing on the domain-level
intercorrelations, we were not able to test the proposed factorial structure of the model, nor
could we make any further conclusions about the exact nature of D and the HEXACO. As D
was primarily constructed to represent the breadth of the PLEBs domain rather than to reduce
its overlap with existing traits (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2022a), it is possible that some of the
proposed subscales of D have considerable off-target loadings on other traits. Likely
candidates for the observed overlap in X-D are Apathy/Depression and Mania, which may be
related to the low and high poles of X, respectively, and the General Executive/Cognitive
Impairment facet of D, which may be substantially related to the low pole of C. Alternatively,
there is the possibility that some indicators of D are better understood as indicators of other
traits altogether, as was the case for Social Anhedonia and Big Five Extraversion (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡
et al., 2017). Likewise, the content of the D model itself may be subject to change (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡
et al., 2017; 2022a). A likely candidate for this is the affective subscales of
Apathy/Depression, Mania, and Flattened Affect. Rather than being rooted in D per se, their
common nature of emotional instability and rapid affective changes probably is.
Consequently, it is necessary to examine the hierarchical model structure and identify the
optimal measurement model and potential interstitial subscales that do not load clearly on a
single dimension. This way, the usefulness of an extended HEXACOD model might be
enhanced.
Second, and in contrast to previous meta-analytic assessments of the discriminant
validity of D, we restricted our data to the DELTA instrument. Consequently, our results are
limited to a smaller literature base. As D represents the most comprehensive bottom-up
conceptualization of the PLEBs domain, using its DELTA measure still allows for a thorough

test of whether the domain is unique from the HEXACO at all. However, future research
using a more diverse set of D measures is warranted to validate the current findings.
Third, regarding the literature base itself, we found that most reports were based on
Serbian community samples. Accordingly, there was too little data to answer conclusively
whether translated DELTA versions show systematically different intercorrelations. Future
evaluations of HEXACO-D in non-Serbian samples are warranted to test the cross-cultural
generalizability of the model. Even though translation and culture effects are likely to be
confounded, this is unproblematic for the more general question of systematic differences in
aggregated HEXACO-D intercorrelations regardless of their origin. Although the proportion
of students did not affect the discriminant validity of D overall, in many cases the primary
studies did not distinguish between individuals who were in higher education and those who
had completed it. Because graduates are not fully comparable to undergraduates, this
modification may have introduced heterogeneity within the proportion of students, limiting
the effect of scale range restriction effects. Regardless, we argue that graduate students are as
unrepresentative of the general population as undergraduate students and, on the other hand,
more like undergraduate students than individuals without higher education. In any case, we
observed an overrepresentation of academics (i.e., the proportion of undergraduate and
graduate students) in the general population samples, which limits the generalizability of our
results. The same is true for the rather small number of reports that assessed the clinical status
of their participants. This may be due to the focus of the HEXACO model, which aims to
capture normal personality variation. However, there were significant patient effects in
models that also cover abnormal personality variation (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2019; LazareviÄ‡ et al.,
2016), likely due to a higher number of patients overall. Consequently, all moderator analyses
suffered from limited information.

Conclusion
Taken together, we can confidently conclude that D, or more generally speaking
PLEBs, is indeed a distinct construct beyond the HEXACO or any other established
personality model (KneÅ¾eviÄ‡ et al., 2016, 2019; LazareviÄ‡ et al., 2016). This interpretation is
based on a substantial body of research showing the trait-like characteristics of D. Similarly,
alternative claims about the relationship between D and HEXACO were not convincing or
could not adequately explain the current or previous findings of discriminant validity.
Whether or not to extend existing personality models with a D factor should be carefully
evaluated in terms of parsimony, replicability, and effectiveness (de Vries, 2020).
Accordingly, simpler models are only sacrificed in favor of increased predictive validity (i.e.,
effectiveness), as was the case with the HEXACO that predict unique phenomena that are not
adequately captured by the more parsimonious Big Five (Ashton & Lee, 2019; Thielmann et
al., 2022). An important requirement for predictive validity is support for discriminant
validity, which we demonstrated in the current study. Thus, initial evidence that D does
indeed have incremental validity can be interpreted as first indications of the potential
usefulness of adding D to existing personality models. However, a comprehensive overview
of the predictive validity of D and systematic model comparisons is currently missing.
Accordingly, the current meta-analysis cannot conclusively answer the question of model
extension, but it adds to a growing body of research that supports this view.
