Framing Effects With Somewhat Risky Options 2
Abstract
When a certain option and a risky option are both described as potential gains, most people
choose the certain option. However, when the same two options are described as potential losses,
most people choose the risky option. This famous preference pattern is known as the risky-
choice framing effect. Despite the seeming rarity of sure things and all-or-none risks in real life,
surprisingly little research on this framing effect has involved options with intermediate levels of
risk. In this research, we identified four types of ‚Äúsomewhat risky‚Äù options, combined them with
each other and with certain and all-or-none options to create 15 risk comparisons in both gains
and losses in each of four domains (cancer, drought, investment, and wildfire), and conducted
two preregistered experiments in which we varied risk comparisons between or within
participants in both student and online samples (total N = 2,586 after exclusions). We found
significant framing effects in almost all risk comparisons, greatly expanding the range of
decisions in which framing is relevant. In addition, we used out-of-sample predictions from 13
versions of risky-choice theories to evaluate how well those theories accounted for participants‚Äô
choices. Versions of transfer-of-attention-exchange theory (TAX) generally outperformed
versions of prospect theory, fuzzy-trace theory, the priority heuristic, and other accounts,
although prospect theory without loss aversion outperformed TAX and the other accounts when
we used an alternate set of parameter values.
Keywords: decision making, risk, replication, generalizability
Public Significance Statement
Options in decisions about money, health, and other matters can often be described or
‚Äúframed‚Äù in terms of potential gains or potential losses, steering people‚Äôs preferences toward
safer or riskier options, respectively. This research shows that this well-known framing effect

applies not only to choices between riskless options and very risky options but also to choices
involving intermediate levels of risk, expanding the applicability of framing to a much wider set
of circumstances. For theories of risky choice, the results favor accounts based on the
expectations of options (i.e., weighting possible outcomes by their likelihoods) over accounts
based on simpler categorical comparisons, such as whether one, both, or neither of the options
includes the possibility of gaining (or losing) nothing.

These pearls of wisdom, one famous and one not, address two essential components of risk:

In our view, this has been the situation with research on the classic risky-choice framing

certain option (‚Äú400 people will die‚Äù).

Overview
The research reported in this article is our attempt to apply a similarly thorough and balanced
approach to assess framing effects in choices with different types of somewhat risky options.
Although we preregistered our experiments, they were predominantly exploratory. We expected
some previous results to replicate, but we did not preregister specific hypotheses. Similarly,
while we listed numerous theoretical accounts and described our primary analysis for comparing
them, we did not hypothesize a winner. To the contrary, we noted that ‚Äúwe do not have a horse in
this race.‚Äù
In the reminder of this Introduction, we describe four types of somewhat risky options;
discuss their relative riskiness; consider the various choice types or risk comparisons that can be
created by combining these options with a certain option, an all-or-none risky option, and each
other; and summarize the literature on framing effects in such choices. We also introduce 13
versions of theories and accounts for predicting framing effects, though we leave some details to
the Method section of Experiment 1. We then report two experiments in which we varied 15 risk

comparisons between participants (Experiment 1) or within participants (Experiment 2), with
two participant populations (students and online participants) in each case. For each of these four
samples, we report the magnitude of framing effects in the various risk comparisons and
compare the performance of the theories in predicting those results. We use out-of-sample
predictions so that theories with multiple parameters, PT and transfer-of-attention-exchange
theory (TAX; Birnbaum, 2008; Birnbaum & Navarrete, 1998), do not have an advantage over
those with no parameters, such as fuzzy-trace theory (FTT; Broniatowski & Reyna, 2018; Reyna,
2012; Reyna & Brainerd, 1995) and the priority heuristic (PH; Brandst√§tter et al., 2006; Pachur
et al., 2013). For PT and TAX, we use parameter values from the literature, though we later
revisit the implications of this choice. In our General Discussion, we update the literature and
summarize our theory comparisons.

Theoretical Explanations for Framing Effects
We consider several accounts of framing effects that are sufficiently specified to allow out-
of-sample predictions for the choices in our experiments. Even with this restriction, our
consideration set is necessarily incomplete.
Expectation-Based Theories
PT and TAX are both expectation-based theories, meaning that the overall evaluation of an
option is a weighted sum of the subjective values of the possible outcomes, with the weights
being a function of the probabilities of the outcomes.
Prospect Theory. PT holds that the wording of the options leads decision makers to adopt
different reference points in the gain and loss frames. C vs. R framing effects can then be
explained by the theory‚Äôs S-shaped value function (a two-part power function with exponents ùõº
in gains and ùõΩ in losses), its inverse-S-shaped weighting function (with parameters ùõæ in gains
and ùõø in losses), or their combination (for details, see DeKay & Garge, 2023). PT‚Äôs other
trademark feature, loss aversion (with parameter ùúÜ), cannot generate framing effects on its own.

Gist-Based and Heuristic Theories and Accounts
Fuzzy-Trace Theory. FTT uses the categorical gist of the options to explain framing effects.
In gains, options that include a zero outcome (e.g., no people will be saved) are categorically
worse than those to that do not. In losses, options that include a zero outcome (e.g., no people
will die) are categorically better than those that do not. These comparisons imply the usual
framing effect in C vs. R choices. FTT also includes ordinal gist comparisons and interval
‚Äúverbatim‚Äù comparisons, but those do not contribute to preferences in any of the choices in this
article.
The Explicated Valence Account. In the explicated valence account (EVA; Tombu &
Mandel, 2015), which followed FTT by over 20 years, preferences are based on categorical
comparisons of the valences of the option descriptions. In the gain frame of standard C vs. R
choices, the positive valence of the certain option is preferred to the mixed valence of the risky
option. In losses, the mixed valence of the risky option is preferred to the negative valence of the
certain option. For the choices considered in this article, EVA makes the same predictions as

The Priority Heuristic. PH is a ‚Äúfast and frugal‚Äù heuristic in which the options‚Äô aspects are
compared in a specific order until a difference on one aspect is large enough to justify a choice.
When there are two options with only one or two possible outcomes each, the steps in the gain
frame (for example) are as follows: Compare the options‚Äô minimum gains. If they differ by at
least 10% of the maximum gain, rounded the nearest prominent number (e.g., 100, 200, 500,
1000, 2000, 5000), choose the option with the larger minimum gain. If not, compare the
probabilities of the minimum gains. If they differ by at least .10, choose the option with the
lower probability. If the difference is insufficient, choose the option with the larger maximum
gain regardless of the difference. In standard C vs. R choices without extreme probabilities
(DeKay & Garge, 2023), the certain option is preferred in gains because it has a larger minimum
gain, and the risky option is preferred in losses because it has a smaller minimum loss. Pachur et
al. (2013) reviewed the evidence regarding PH and tested its process predictions relative to those

recruited 1,024 participants from Amazon Mechanical Turk via CloudResearch (CR) for an
incentive of $0.50. For the latter sample, we used only CR-approved participants from the United

selected settings to block duplicate IP addresses, block suspicious geocode locations, and verify

representative sample well-matched to U.S. Census data (DeKay & Dou, 2024b).
Our target sample size of 900 in each sample (30 participants in each of 30 conditions) was
chosen to yield about 60 participants and 240 choices in each of 15 framing-effect comparisons.
We did not conduct formal power analyses because (a) different theories predict different
framing effects in different conditions, including no effect in some conditions, and (b) each of
these separate comparisons would involve only 7% of our data. Other studies using stimuli very
similar to ours (DeKay & Dou, 2024a, 2024b; DeKay & Garge, 2023; DeKay et al., 2022) have
been able to demonstrate numerous variations in framing effects using comparable total sample

Procedure
In an anonymous survey, participants considered four different scenarios in random order, in
either the gain or loss frame for all scenarios. In each scenario, participants chose between two
programs (A and B) that differed in riskiness but had equal EVs. Participants also indicated their
preference on a 7-point scale with endpoints labeled strong preference for Program A [B] and
the midpoint labeled no preference at all. Which program was called Program A and listed first
was randomly determined in each scenario for each participant. At the end of the survey,
participants answered a few demographic and debriefing questions.

Experiment 2
Experiment 2 was designed to assess whether the above results would generalize to a
different experimental design in which each participant considered all 15 risk comparisons. As in
Experiment 1, we did not preregister specific hypotheses.
Method
Participants
In fall 2020 and winter 2021, we recruited 476 undergraduates from the Department of
Psychology at The Ohio State University. They received course credit for their participation.
Also in winter 2021, we recruited 483 participants from Amazon Mechanical Turk via CR for an
incentive of $1.00, using the same data-quality settings as before.
Our target sample size was 400 in each sample (50 participants in each of eight conditions).
In terms of the total number of observations, our target was higher than in Experiment 1 (400
participants ¬¥ 15 choices each = 6,000 choices per sample, compared to 900 participants ¬¥ 4
choices each = 3,600 choices per sample). We did not conduct a formal power analysis, but we
assumed power would be higher in each of the 15 framing-effect comparisons because about 400
participants would contribute to each, compared to about 60 participants per comparison in
Experiment 1.

In order, we excluded participants who answered no questions (11 and 14 in the student and
CR samples, respectively); were flagged by Qualtrics as duplicates (13 and 1), potential frauds (6
and 6), or bots (3 and 1); finished the task too quickly (19 and 30) or too slowly (5 and 7);
skipped more than one of the 30 choice and rating questions (4 and 8); answered more than one
pair of choice and rating questions inconsistently (20 and 15); or whose answers to a debriefing
question indicated familiarity with framing effects (0 and 0). After exclusions, 395 and 401
participants remained in the two samples, respectively. Participants in the student sample were
mostly from Ohio (84%) and were 18‚Äì37 years old (M = 19.2, SD = 2.1); those in the CR sample
were from 45 states (plus Washington, DC) and were 18‚Äì78 years old (M = 40.5, SD = 13.1).
Among student participants (and CR participants in parentheses), 54% (50%) identified as

Each participant considered 15 risk comparisons in random order, in one of the four domains

Design

Comparisons of Theories
We took two approaches to comparing the performance of several theoretical accounts. The
first was to note theories‚Äô relatively clear prediction errors, such as predicting no framing effect
when the data indicate a significant effect. This approach tended to favor versions of the
expectation-based theories, PT and TAX, though notably not PT . Gist-based and heuristic
full
accounts typically fared slightly worse (FTT, FTT+, and SA) or much worse (PH and especially
GBC). This approach was useful for seeing where theories made their worst errors, but it had its
shortcomings. For example, we aggregated the data over the four domains (see Figures 2 and 4)
and over the two samples in each experiment to reduce the variability for each risk comparison.
Perhaps more importantly, the approach focused mostly on categorical errors rather than errors
of degree (e.g., predicting that a framing effect would be 30% as large as that for C vs. R choices

when it was actually 90% as large). Our threshold for counting a continuous prediction as being
a prediction of ‚Äúno framing effect‚Äù (less than 15% as large as the predicted effect in C vs. R

would have increased the error counts for PT and TAX.

TAX as a Theory of Framing Effects
To our knowledge, TAX had not been applied to framing effects prior to DeKay and Garge‚Äôs
(2023) article. In that investigation, framing effects in C vs. R choices and SR vs. R choices
(but not in C vs. SR choices) were smaller for extreme probabilities (e.g., .01, .99) than for
moderate probabilities, but framing effects remained substantial for extreme probabilities. TAX
(called TAX here) outperformed PT (called PT here), FTT, a modified version of FTT in
full full
which extreme probabilities were rounded to 0 or 1, and PH in accounting for the results.
Moreover, both of TAX‚Äôs mechanisms for generating framing effects were required to predict
the observed pattern: The S-shaped value function predicted the inverted-U pattern, and the
weight-transfer function predicted nonzero framing effects at all probabilities, including the
extremes.
The present results, together with those of DeKay and Garge (2023), suggest that TAX
should be considered a leading theory for explaining framing effects. Note, however, that our
application of TAX assumes that the wording of the options leads people to adopt different
reference points in the two frames, as in the original PT explanation for framing effects. In
addition, the S-shaped value function that contributed to TAX‚Äôs performance here and in DeKay
and Garge‚Äôs experiment is the same as that in PT, but without loss aversion. While TAX clearly
allows for such a function, a linear function often used to show that many of TAX‚Äôs predictions
do not depend on such curvature (Birnbaum, 2008). Our application of TAX was closer to PT in
these regards.
Despite TAX‚Äôs promising performance, it does not account for all results involving risky-
Framing Effects With Somewhat Risky Options 57
choice framing effects. Numerous studies of C vs. R framing effects have involved complete
descriptions of the certain option (e.g., ‚Äú200 people will be saved and 400 people will not be
saved‚Äù in the gain frame), incomplete descriptions of the risky option, or both (Broniatowski &
Reyna, 2018; DeKay & Dou, 2024a, 2024b). PT cannot explain the observed variation in the
direction and magnitude of framing effects across such problems, for reasons that also apply to
TAX. For example, it is not clear how to apply PT‚Äôs (or TAX‚Äôs) functions to negations like ‚Äú400
people will not be saved‚Äù (K√ºhberger & Tanner, 2010; Mandel, 2001). Categorical accounts,
including versions of EVA and FTT, can explain such variation, though a residual framing effect
may remain even when those accounts predict no effect (DeKay & Dou, 2024a, 2024b).
Critical Tests of FTT versus PT (and Other Expectation-Based Theories)
Versions of framing problems in which participants choose between the usual certain option
and an incomplete or truncated risky option have been especially popular as critical tests of FTT
versus PT and other expectation-based theories (Broniatowski & Reyna, 2018; Chick et al.,
2016; K√ºhberger & Tanner, 2010; Reyna, 2012; Reyna & Brainerd, 1995; Reyna et al., 2014,
2021, 2023). When the nonzero component of the risky option is omitted so that only the zero

Constraints on Generality
We assessed framing effects and the ability of several theories to predict those effects across
15 risk comparisons, four scenarios, two participant populations, two experimental designs, and
several variations on our primary analyses. With a few notable exceptions (e.g., larger framing
Framing Effects With Somewhat Risky Options 61
effects for SR vs. R choices in Experiment 1 but not Experiment 2), the results were very
consistent.
Given the similarity between the results for our student and CR samples, we would expect
the results to generalize to a more representative sample of adults in the United States. In related
research on framing effects, DeKay and Dou (2024b) recently replicated results from student and
CR samples (DeKay & Dou, 2024a) almost exactly in a more representative sample well-
matched to U.S. Census data on age, gender, race, ethnicity, region, and personal income. More
broadly, Coppock et al. (2018) reported a ‚Äústrong correspondence‚Äù (p. 12441) between the
results from online convenience samples and those from nationally representative samples for 27
effects in ‚Äúthe sorts of persuasion, attitude-formation, and decision-making studies that are
common in social-science experiments‚Äù (p. 12442). We are less sure about generalization to
other countries or cultures, though we note that framing effects in a version of the original
disease problem have been observed worldwide (Im & Chen, 2022).
We advise against generalizing results from between-participants comparisons (e.g., for
framing effects in our experiments) to individual participants. Even when between-participants
effects are large and significant, it is possible that a minority of participants would exhibit the
effect in a within-participants design, as has been demonstrated for the reflection effect (Hershey
& Shoemaker, 1980), certainty and possibility effects (DeKay et al., 2016), and various other
effects involving risky choices (Regenwetter et al., 2022; Regenwetter & Robinson, 2017).
we note that framing effects were most reliable in C vs. R and SR vs. R choices, followed by C
vs. SR choices. We would expect to find similar effects in choices between other options that
clearly differ in riskiness. We are less confident that the results for SR vs. SR choices would

especially if they are able to make comparisons based on the numerical properties of options in
addition to the textual elements that differentiate the gain and loss frames.
Conclusion
This research represents a major contribution to the literature on framing effects in choices
with somewhat risky options, nearly doubling the number of participants in the relevant
conditions. We have demonstrated framing effects for three apparently novel choice types and
either corroborated evidence for framing effects or helped resolve mixed results for several other
choice types. There now is strong evidence for framing effects in most of these risk comparisons.
As a result, financial advisors, healthcare practitioners, and other risk communicators (for
example) can be more confident that the implications of the classic framing effect also apply in
situations involving intermediate levels of risk.
Overall, these results favor versions of expectation-based theories‚Äîespecially TAX but also
PT without loss aversion‚Äîover gist-based or heuristic accounts like FTT and PH. One reason for
this performance difference was that the categorical predictions of the latter accounts proved
costly when they were wrong (e.g., FTT‚Äôs predictions of no framing effects in some C vs. SR
choices).
As with other recent work on framing effects (DeKay & Dou, 2024a, 2024b; DeKay &
Framing Effects With Somewhat Risky Options 63
Garge, 2023; DeKay et al., 2022), this research highlights the benefits of exploring variation in
the aspects of stimulus materials thoroughly and systematically. That this approach continues to
uncover new results and informative theoretical comparisons for a phenomenon as well known
as the risky-choice framing effect is a testament to its merit.
