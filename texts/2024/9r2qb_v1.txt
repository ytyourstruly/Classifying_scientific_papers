Abstract
Cooperation underlies the ability of groups to realize collective benefits (e.g., creation of
public goods). Yet, cooperation can be difficult to achieve when people face situations with
conflicting interests between what is best for individuals versus the collective (i.e., social
dilemmas). To address this challenge, groups can implement rules about structural changes in
a situation. But what institutional rules can best facilitate cooperation? Theoretically, rules
can be made to affect structural features of a social dilemma, such as the possible actions,
outcomes, and people involved. We derived 13 pre-registered hypotheses from existing work
and collected six decades of empirical research to test how nine structural features influence
cooperation within prisoner‚Äôs dilemmas and public goods dilemmas. We do this by meta-
analyzing mean levels of cooperation across studies (Study 1, k = 2,340, N = 229,528), and
also examining how manipulations of these structural features in social dilemmas affect
cooperation within studies (Study 2, k = 909). Results indicated that lower conflict of
interests was associated with higher cooperation, and that (1) the implementation of sanctions
(i.e., reward and punishment of behaviors) and (2) allowing for communication most strongly
enhanced cooperation. However, we found inconsistent support for the hypotheses that group
size and matching design affect cooperation. Other structural features (e.g., symmetry of
dilemmas, sequential decision making, payment) were not associated with cooperation.
Overall, these findings inform institutions that can (or not) facilitate cooperation.
Keywords: Institutions, Rules, Cooperation, Social dilemma, Meta-analysis

Institutions and Cooperation:
A Meta-Analysis of Structural Features in Social Dilemmas
Humans are an ultra-social, cooperative species. People cooperate to realize a wealth
of benefits in different activities in daily life, such as trade, conserving resources, and work
within organizations. Cooperation involves individuals making decisions that benefit a
collective at a personal cost. Although cooperation can often occur in situations in which
people have corresponding interests (Columbus et al., 2021), not all situations are so
conducive to cooperation. Cooperation can be difficult to achieve as there exists a conflict
between individual and collective interests (i.e., social dilemmas; Dawes, 1980). Social
scientists have long claimed that people will fail to cooperate when it is not in their self-
interest to do so (Hardin, 1968; Olson, 1965). The temptation to maximize self-interest in
social dilemmas can lead people to defect and thereby result in detrimental collective
outcomes.
Over the past few decades, researchers across disciplines have been seeking solutions
to solve social dilemmas (Chaudhuri, 2011; Kollock, 1998; Ostrom, 2005; Ostrom et al.,
1994; Simpson & Willer, 2015). Prominent theories in social and behavioral sciences, such as
game theory (Colman, 1995) and interdependence theory (Kelley et al., 2003), have
emphasized how the structure of situations can be altered to reduce the conflict of interests
people experience in social dilemmas. Thus, an appeal to self-interest can actually be a key
solution to solving social dilemmas. Indeed, an extensive history of research has studied how
imposing rules that create structural changes to social interactions, namely, institutional
solutions, can reduce the conflict of interests people experience in social dilemmas (Kollock,
1998; Messick et al., 1983; Samuelson & Messick, 1986).

In doing so, researchers have adopted a rigorous and standardized experimental
approach to study cooperation, via social dilemma paradigms that can model the complexity
of daily life interdependent situations (Murnighan & Wang, 2016; Spadaro, Tiddi, et al.,
2022). There are at least seven aspects of a social dilemma in which structural changes can be
applied to fundamentally alter the dilemma and affect cooperation (Ostrom, 2005). Each of
these aspects can include one or more structural features that can vary across studies. To
illustrate, while designing the social dilemmas, researchers can determine the set of
participants (e.g., group size), the specific positions (roles) of participants (e.g., sequentiality
of decision making), the set of allowable actions (e.g., whether communication is possible),
the potential outcomes (e.g., participant payment), the level of control (e.g., asymmetry of
dependence), the costs and benefits assigned to different actions (e.g., punishment, rewards),
and whether the situation occurred once or a number of times for the same set of participants
(e.g., stranger versus partner matching). Thus, how researchers have constructed social
dilemmas in past studies has created variation in these structural features of social dilemmas,
which can be used to test theory about the institutional configurations that affect cooperation.
The present research, reports two meta-analyses that consider how nine structural
features of social dilemmas can shape cooperation in the most commonly studied social
dilemmas (Prisoner‚Äôs Dilemma, Public Goods Dilemma). In Study 1, we meta-analyzed how
variation in structural features of social dilemmas across studies covaried with the level of
cooperation between studies. In Study 2, we meta-analyzed effect sizes obtained from within-
study manipulations of these structural features. We conduct these analyses to achieve four
goals: (1) test hypotheses about how structural features of social dilemmas affect cooperation;
Over the past six decades, most research has studied cooperative behavior in three prototypical social
dilemmas: prisoner‚Äôs dilemmas (PD; Rapoport et al., 1965), public goods dilemmas (PGD; Ledyard, 1995) and
resource dilemmas (RD; Kopelman et al., 2002). This research tradition began in the 1950‚Äôs with a focus on
behavior in the PD, later expanded to include the PGD and RD, and has experienced an exponential rise in
studies in recent years. More information about how cooperation research changes over time can be found in the
living reviews from the Cooperation Databank (Balliet et al., 2023, May 24).

Social Dilemmas and the Hurdle of Conflicting Interests
Many social situations can involve a conflict of interests ‚Äì that is, the best outcome
for one person is not the best outcome for others. These situations present to individuals a
social dilemma (Dawes, 1980; Van Lange et al., 2014), in which individuals always receive a
greater outcome by defection regardless of their partner‚Äôs behavior. However, if each
individual pursues their self-interest and chose not to cooperate, the outcome for the entire
collective will be worse than if everyone chose to cooperate. Different social dilemma
paradigms have been developed to model everyday life social situations in which people face
conflicting interests between themselves and others, including the prisoner‚Äôs dilemma (PD)
and the public goods dilemma (PGD).
A prototypical PD involves two players, each of whom can decide independently
whether to cooperate or defect (Rapoport et al., 1965). A classic example of the PD is an

arms race between two countries (e.g., the Cold War). A PGD usually involves multiple
players, each of whom possesses a resource that can be contributed to a ‚Äúpublic good‚Äù that
benefits all group members (Ledyard, 1995). Common public goods in society include roads,
educational institutions, and charities (G√§chter & Herrmann, 2009). The two paradigms both
offer choices to cooperate or defect (with the latter representing the rational strategy, yet
producing inefficient collective outcomes, Gangadharan & Nikiforakis, 2009), and share
comparable payoff structures. In fact, making a completely cooperative or defective decision
in a binary choice set can be regarded as equivalent to contributing the maximum or
minimum possible amount to the public good. Moreover, a prototypical PD can be extended
to involve multiple players (Fox & Guyer, 1978; Hamburger, 1973) and continuous choices
representing different levels of cooperation (Van Lange, Klapwijk, et al., 2011), which is
congruent to a linear PGD.

Institutions, Rules, and Structural Features that Affect Cooperation
Institutions are considered the ‚Äúrules of the game‚Äù which can shape how interactions
unfold in everyday life (Hodgson, 2006; North, 1990). More specifically, institutions are the
‚Äúrules, norms, and strategies adopted by individuals‚Äù within a collective (Crawford & Ostrom,
2005, p. 137). At the societal level, institutions have been established to coordinate collective
efforts and foster cooperation (Hindriks & Guala, 2021; Ostrom, 1990). Within research on
social dilemmas, researchers manipulate various structural features to craft these situations,
and thereby artificially design different rules of the game to study cooperation. For example,
researchers can decide to limit group size of the social dilemma to three or six, (dis)allow
communication within the group, and enable either single or repeated interactions among the
same group members. By creating rules that define roles and outcomes, constrain and enable
certain behaviors, the researchers can change the structure of the game in ways that
(potentially) facilitate cooperation (Hindriks & Guala, 2021; Ostrom, 2005).

the key features of social dilemmas that groups, policymakers, and managers within

Set of Participants: Group Size
Social dilemmas can be experienced in groups of different sizes: students can work in
dyads or small groups to complete an assignment, citizens can pay taxes to maintain public
services, and nations can set policies to prevent climate change. Rules about participants can
determine the group size for specific social dilemmas, such as altering the size of work teams
in organizations, or setting boundaries on how many people can use a common resource in a
community.
There are several reasons why group size could affect cooperation in social dilemmas.
Group size can covary with the incentive for cooperation, that is the individual and group
payoffs for contributing to the public good (Bonacich et al., 1976; Wu et al., 2020). In larger
groups, there is often a lower individual payoff of cooperation, and a higher temptation to
free-ride on the group. This could produce a decline in cooperation with increasing group size.
Moreover, increases in group size has been found to reduce self-efficacy (Kerr, 1989), make
people feel less responsible for group performance (Forsyth et al., 2002), and perceive greater
anonymity (Van Vugt & Samuelson, 1999) ‚Äì each of which could also explain a reduction in
cooperation within larger group sizes. For all of these reasons, group size is expected to be
negatively associated with cooperation (Hypothesis 2).
Interestingly, previous research has found mixed results on how group size relates to
cooperation, with some studies reporting that cooperation declined in larger groups (e.g.,
Brewer & Kramer, 1986; Komorita & Lapworth, 1982), some studies observing an increase
in cooperation in larger groups (e.g., Isaac et al., 1994; Nosenzo et al., 2015), and others
studies showing no relation between group size and cooperation (e.g., Kerr, 1989). These
mixed findings may be reconciled by considering the individual gains relative to the costs of
cooperation in different game contexts. Barcelo and Capraro (2015) propose that larger
groups are more inclined to cooperate when the individual benefit for cooperation increases

when the individual benefit for cooperation remains constant. In the present study, we are
able to test the relation between group size and cooperation while controlling for the degree

cooperation.
Position of Participants: Sequential Decisions
Social dilemmas can involve people making decisions simultaneously (i.e., at the
same time without knowledge of the other person‚Äôs choice) or sequentially (i.e., decisions are
made in a sequential order, often with knowledge of the earlier persons‚Äô choice). Rules can be
established to specify the (relative) positions people have when facing social dilemmas. For
example, people in a work group can decide whether to simultaneously work on a project or
sequentially make contributions, and a charity can organize fundraising activities to either
allow for simultaneous donations or sequential donations disclosing the previous donations.
Abele and Ehrhart (2005) argued for differences in cooperation between group
members who move sequentially and simultaneously. In a sequential decision making
protocol, people make their decision in a temporal order, which allows people to exploit the
first mover. As a consequence, a first mover may expect this response and choose to defect
preemptively. Therefore, simultaneous choices in social dilemmas may more likely result in
mutual cooperation, compared to sequential decision making. Simultaneous decision making
also has the potential to enhance the sense of shared fate, perceived entitativity, cohesion
among people, and/or quasi-magical thinking (e.g., Abele & Ehrhart, 2005; Brewer &
Kramer, 1986; De Cremer & Van Vugt, 1999; Shafir & Tversky, 1992), which may thereby
increase cooperation. Previous research had participants move simultaneously or pseudo-

Possible Behaviors: Communication
Social dilemma can involve people interacting with specific behaviors, such as
communication, permitted or constrained. Rules can determine whether and how
communication occurs within interactions. For example, community meetings can be held
regularly to allow people to exchange ideas in solving local collective action problems; the
team leader can send unidirectional information to members or involve team members in
bidirectional communication to discuss tasks allocation.
Communication has long been recognized as one of the most effective solutions to
promote cooperation in social dilemmas (Balliet, 2010; Ostrom et al., 1994; Sally, 1995).
The very act of discussing the dilemma can increase cooperation, relative to the absence of
communication (e.g., Bouas & Komorita, 1996; Dawes et al., 1977; Kerr & Kaufman-
Gilliland, 1994; Orbell et al., 1988; Ostrom & Walker, 1991), or even imagining to
communicate (Meleady et al., 2013). Further, this positive impact of communication on
cooperation occurs regardless of whether communication is face-to-face, or by sending
messages online (Chen & Komorita, 1994). There exist several possible explanations for the
effect of communication on cooperation, including increasing expectations of others‚Äô
cooperation (i.e., trust), promoting a group identity, and establishing norms of cooperation
(Balliet, 2010; Kerr et al., 1997). Past research suggested that communication about the
dilemma can afford prosocial norms, particularly the norm of promise keeping, which is
conducive to cooperation (Bicchieri, 2002; Bicchieri & Lev-on, 2007). Therefore, we expect

more cooperation when there is communication compared to no communication (Hypothesis
4a).
However, contrary to bidirectional communication, unidirectional communication
does not allow for immediate, direct responses to others‚Äô remarks, which may produce
misunderstanding and restrict the emergence of norms of behavior. Therefore, cooperation
may be higher under settings that allow for bidirectional communication than in settings with
only unidirectional communication (Hypothesis 4b). Additionally, communication may have
greater impact on cooperation within situations that would more likely elicit competition,
such as situations with stronger conflict of interests and more temptation to free-ride. We
predict that the positive impact of communication on cooperation will be stronger when there
is a larger, compared to smaller, degrees of conflict in the social dilemma (Hypothesis 4c).
Potential Outcomes: Payment
Social dilemmas can vary in what kind of outcomes participants experience. Rules can
be applied to affect how participants are paid for their participation and decisions made in the
study. For example, participants in studies have been compensated through a flat fee for their
participation in the study (i.e., show-up fee) and/or a payment based on their performance in
the game. Monetary payment are widely used in experimental studies in the social sciences,
while there also exits other types of incentives such as course credit, gifts and vouchers.
Payment can sufficiently motivate participation (Bonner et al., 2000; Friedman et al.,
1994), and also enhance the internal validity of studies (Hertwig & Ortmann, 2001). However,
rules that determine each type of payment may affect cooperation within studies. It has been
argued that offering show-up fees could lead to biased samples because volunteer participants
might be inherently more cooperative (Thielmann et al., 2020; Van Lange et al., 2007).
Indeed, research has found that individuals who assign more weight to other‚Äôs outcomes are
more likely to volunteer for studies (Van Lange, Schippers, et al., 2011). Therefore, we
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 14
predict higher cooperation in studies where participants receive no show-up fee, compared to
when they receive compensation for participation (Hypothesis 5a).
Performance-based payment related to the outcomes of the social dilemma may be
negatively related to cooperation, because cooperative behavior is more costly in situations
with real versus hypothetical payoffs. Individuals can approach a social dilemma with an
intrinsic motive to cooperate (e.g., prosocial value orientation), but payment may provide an
‚Äúextrinsic motivation‚Äù to behave less cooperatively (Frey & Goette, 1999; Gneezy &
Rustichini, 2000). Following this reasoning, we predict less cooperation when people receive
performance-based compared to hypothetical incentives (Hypothesis 5b). To date, prior
research has found inconclusive results about how incentives affect cooperation (e.g.,
Columbus et al., 2022; Engel, 2011; Johnson & Mislin, 2011; Romano et al., 2021; Sally,
1995; Zelmer, 2003). Evidence from online experiments showed that individuals behave
more prosocially while making hypothetical than incentivized decisions in dictator games, yet
no such difference was observed in public goods games or trust games (Amir et al., 2012).
Moreover, we predict that performance-based incentives will increase defection in one-shot
social dilemmas, but promote cooperation during iterated interactions (Hypothesis 5c). This is
because engaging in some cooperation, at least in early stages of the game, can benefit
oneself in the long-run across iterated interactions (Van Lange, Schippers, et al., 2011). In
addition, we will also explore whether rules about the type of incentive used for a game
payment (i.e., monetary/non-monetary; lottery/non-lottery payment) affects cooperation (see
SI for more information).
The Control Afforded to Participants: Symmetry
Social dilemmas can vary in the relative extent of control participants have in
determining their own and other‚Äôs outcomes in the dilemma, and to what extent their own
outcomes are affected by others‚Äô behavior (i.e., social power; Balliet et al., 2017). Rules can
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 15
be applied to affect the asymmetries in the control that individuals have over one another‚Äôs
outcome. For instance, members of organizations are often assigned different roles (e.g.,
leader and follower) to divide labor and achieve joint tasks; within family units, parents
typically manage significant financial responsibilities, whereas children are allotted a limited
allowance. Multiple sources of asymmetrical dependence have been studied in the social
dilemma literature; for example, group members were assigned unequal endowments to
contribute to the public good, group members received different benefits from a public good,
and group members were given specific roles determining how decisions are made, such as
leaders and followers within a group (Nieper et al., 2019; Van Lange et al., 2013). These
differences in resources, payoffs and roles are different operationalizations of power
(a)symmetry.
Halevy and colleagues (2011) proposed that asymmetrical dependence, power, and
hierarchy in a group, reduces conflict within a group and promotes voluntary cooperation of
individuals, since hierarchy is functionally adaptive and can enhance a group‚Äôs success.
Group members with lower power tend to cooperate with higher-powered members, since the
latter controls more resources and low-power members can pay less for cooperating relative
to their higher-power counterparts. This dynamic may be further complemented by high-
power members reciprocating this cooperation, as this may not only enhance their reputation
and maintain group stability but also strategically invest in a social structure where their
authority and influence are reinforced. This perspective also aligns with the morality-as-
cooperation theory, which posits that the display of dominance and submission are perceived
as morally good in human social life, signaling individuals‚Äô adherence to a hierarchical
structure (Curry et al., 2019). Thus, we predict higher level of cooperation in asymmetric,
compared to symmetric, groups (Hypothesis 6). Yet, empirical research has found
contradictory results: Asymmetric, compared to symmetric groups, have been found to

display (a) less cooperation (e.g., L. R. Anderson et al., 2008; Cherry et al., 2005; Fung & Au,
2014; Marwell & Ames, 1979, 1980), (b) greater cooperation (e.g., Bergstrom et al., 1986;
Chan et al., 1996), or (c) no impact on cooperation (e.g., Levati et al., 2007; Molho et al.,
2019; Warr, 1983).
Costs/Benefits Assigned to Actions: Sanctions
Social dilemmas exhibit variability in the extent and manner by which costs and
benefits are assigned to specific behaviors. Rules can be enacted that allow for the possibility
to sanction specific behaviors, including posing rewards and punishments, and potentially
regulate individuals in their pursuit of self-interest and solve the free rider problem. In society,
formal institutions have been developed to punish norm-violations in social dilemmas, such
as imposing fines for littering and not paying taxes. We have also observed examples of
rewarding cooperation in everyday life, such as providing public recognition for generous
donations to charity or for outstanding organizational citizenship behavior. Past research on
cooperation sometimes allowed people to punish or reward their fellow group members (Fehr
& G√§chter, 2002), or had a centralized institution that administered sanctions based on rules
(e.g., the lowest contributing members pay a fine; Yamagishi, 1986, 1988b).
Sanctions provide individuals with incentives that affect the outcome structure within
a social dilemma. The presence of sanctions can reduce the discrepancy between self-interest
and collective interest, which is expected to enhance cooperation. Previous research has
consistently found greater levels of cooperation in social dilemmas when people receive
rewards for cooperation and/or punishment for free-riding, compared to the absence of
sanctions (e.g., Balliet, Mulder, et al., 2011; Fehr & G√§chter, 2002; Mulder, 2008; Ostrom et
al., 1992; Parks, 2000; Rand et al., 2009; Yamagishi, 1986). We therefore predict that people
display more cooperation when there are sanctions, compared to no sanctions (Hypothesis 7a).
Furthermore, sanctions may be more effective in promoting cooperation when people play
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 17
with the same partners for several trials, because people can learn the group‚Äôs cooperative
norms and form expectations of others‚Äô cooperation accordingly (Balliet, Mulder, et al., 2011;
Fehr et al., 2002; Pruitt & Kimmel, 1977). We expect that sanctions will be more effective in
promoting cooperation during iterated, compared to one-shot, dilemmas (Hypothesis 7b).
Strategic Interaction: Stranger versus Partner Matching
Social dilemmas can vary in the nature and complexity of strategic interactions among
individuals involved. Rules can be created to determine how and with whom people make
decisions in social dilemmas. For example, organizations can initiate short-term or long-term
projects, form or dissolve project teams, which may influence staff members‚Äô anticipation
about possible future interactions with their co-workers. Past research on cooperation has
varied on how individuals are matched with their group members. In partner-matching,
individuals often interact with the same partner(s) for multiple rounds; while in stranger-
matching, individuals play with the same partner(s) only once, no matter whether there is one
or multiple rounds, each with different partner(s).
In stranger-matching social dilemmas, individuals are always better off by defecting
regardless of their partners‚Äô strategies (Dawes, 1980). However, under repeated interactions
with the same partner(s), individuals may experience benefits from mutual cooperation and/or
generate beliefs about their partners cooperative behavior in early stages of the game
(Andreoni, 1988; Kreps et al., 1982). In this setting, the more beneficial strategy is to
cooperate in earlier rounds, but to free-ride later in the game. According to interdependence
theory, repeated interaction with the same partner(s) may encourage individuals to consider
their long-term self-interest, which in turn promotes cooperation at the outset of the recurring
engagement (Kelley et al., 2003). We therefore predict that people display more cooperation
in partner matching, compared to stranger matching (Hypothesis 8). Interestingly, while some
prior research found higher cooperation in social dilemmas that had partner-matching

A Rule Unique to Experimental Social Dilemmas: Deception
In the implementation of social dilemma paradigms, there are certain rules of the
game that are unique to experiments, and do not necessarily generalize to social dilemmas
outside the lab. Nonetheless, the study of these rules can inform how experimental designs
affect cooperation. One such rule is the use of deception within studies, that refers to
researchers intentionally providing misinformation to participants about the study (Hertwig &
Ortmann, 2008). For instance, individuals may receive pre-programmed feedback about their
partner‚Äôs decisions in previous rounds of the social dilemma; and the experimenters can make
participants believe that they are interacting with ‚Äúreal‚Äù partners, while actually they are
interacting with computers or there is no interaction at all.
Experimental economists recommend avoiding the use of deception because it may
affect participant behavior via both first-hand (i.e., on deceived participants) and second-hand
exposure (e.g. through teaching, media reports, etc.; Hertwig & Ortmann, 2008). Yet,
psychologists have defended the use of deception on grounds that it has limited consequences
on attitudes (Christensen, 1988; Kimmel, 1998) and behaviors (Bonetti, 1998; Hertwig &
Ortmann, 2008; for a different position, see Hilbig et al., 2022). Previous evidence from
economic games has been inconclusive about the effect of deception on decision making and
behavior. Jamison et al. (2008) found that deception did not affect individual‚Äôs behavior in a
trust game at time one, but having experienced deception reduced return rates and
participants tended to not take the games seriously in a subsequent study. In contrast, Barrera
and Simpson (2012), using a similar design, examined the effect of direct and indirect
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 19
exposure to deception and found that deception reduced the reputation of psychologists but
did not affect the validity of the results. Here, we do not forward a directional prediction, but
will test whether there is a difference in cooperation, when deception is applied versus when
it is not used in a study.

Analytic Strategy
Following our pre-registration plan, we used a three-level mixed-effects meta-
regression to test hypotheses about how different structural variables are associated with the
cooperation estimates (i.e., logit-transformed cooperation rates). We selected a mixed-effects
model because it takes both fixed effects and random effects into consideration. Predictor
variables are included as fixed effects to the model to account for the between-study
heterogeneity (Raudenbush, 2009). The inclusion of random effects allows for inferences to
be made about the entire population of studies, and so beyond the studies included in the
meta-analysis (Hedges & Vevea, 1998). Importantly, 26% of the studies included in the meta-
analysis contributed more than one cooperation rate (i.e., 399 of 1527 studies) . To address
possible dependencies among these cooperation estimates, we conducted a three-level meta-
analysis with unique cooperation estimates nested within studies. Three sources of variance
were therefore modelled following this approach: (1) Level 1: sampling error, (2) Level 2:
variance between cooperation estimates, and (3) Level 3: variance between studies (Cheung,
2014; Van den Noortgate et al., 2013).
We tested our pre-registered hypotheses in meta-regression models with structural
variables, sample variables, and control variables included as predictors of the cooperation
estimates. We fitted the meta-regression model to our data using restricted maximum
likelihood estimation (REML), which corrects for negative bias in variance components and
overfitting (Raudenbush, 2009). Heterogeneity across cooperation estimates were assessed by

Study 2: How Do Structural Features Covary with Cooperation Within Studies?
We used an alternative meta-analytic approach to test our hypotheses, specifically
examining within-study effect sizes that estimate how a measured or manipulated variable
relates to cooperation (i.e., correlation coefficient and standardized mean difference). This
approach allow us to observe the effect of each structural feature on cooperation from studies
that most likely kept other aspects of the game structure constant and thus provides a
robustness test to the hypotheses. In Study 1, several structural features that were predicted to
affect cooperation did not have a statistically significant relation with cooperation. It is
possible that the heterogeneity between studies could mask any small effect size of a specific
structural feature. However, when structural features are manipulated or measured within
studies, this method reduces error variance and provides a more optimal setting to detect
small effect sizes. Thus, this additional approach can strengthen our conclusions about the
possible institutional solutions that (do not) promote cooperation.

General Discussion
A unique feature of human cooperation is the extent to which it occurs among non-
genetically related individuals, who have no history of interaction or knowledge of each
other‚Äôs reputation ‚Äì that is, impersonal cooperation. Institutions are one feature of society that
enable humans to achieve this kind of cooperation in large scale societies (Henrich et al.,
2010; Powers et al., 2021). Institutions involve setting the rules of the game, which can
fundamentally change the structure of the game, reduce the conflict of interests people
experience, and promote cooperation. Institutions can target different aspects that compose a
social dilemma, including the set of participants (e.g., group size), their positions (e.g.,
sequential decision making), the possible actions (e.g., communication), the potential
outcomes (e.g., payment), the level of control (e.g., asymmetry of dependence), the costs and
benefits imposed to different actions (e.g., sanctions), and whether interactions are repeated
for the same set of participants (or not) (e.g., matching design) (Ostrom et al., 1994). In the
present meta-analyses, we examined impersonal cooperation using social dilemma paradigms,
and analyzed how variation in the rules applied by the researchers to construct social
dilemmas were associated with variation in cooperation across treatments and studies.
Specifically, we conducted a meta-regression of nine structural features of social
dilemmas predicting 2,340 mean rates of cooperation obtained from 1,527 independent
studies (1958‚Äì2017) in Study 1. We also compared the conclusions of this approach with the
results from over 10 independent meta-analyses of studies that measured or manipulated each
of the structural variables within a study, involving a total of 909 effect sizes (Study 2).
Overall, we observed a medium amount of cooperation across prisoner‚Äôs dilemmas and
public goods dilemmas (i.e., 48.6%). A previous meta-analysis of social dilemmas found a
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 47
similar average rate of cooperation (i.e., 47%, Sally, 1995). These findings indeed confirm
that these situations are dilemmas, and clearly there is no skew in behavior towards either
complete cooperation or defection. In fact, there is a great amount of variation in cooperation
across the social dilemmas, which can be partly explained by the differences between studies
and between treatments within studies. Despite the temptation to defect in social dilemmas,
we found that several rules used to define structural features of the social dilemmas could
appeal to self-interest, mitigate the conflict of interests, and promote cooperation. We also
discovered that several structural features that were proposed to affect cooperation, in fact,
had no influence on cooperation.
We rely on results from both meta-regression and independent meta-analysis
approaches to test the effect of structural features on cooperation. The meta-regression
examines the variance in cooperation that one structural feature could explain while
concurrently accounting for other relevant structural features and control variables. This
approach may yield a more conservative analysis, yet it allows us to identify the most
influential structural features in affecting cooperation. The independent meta-analyses
aggregates studies that manipulated a specific structural feature and examined its effect on
cooperation. This approach is more able to detect smaller effect sizes that could have been
masked by heterogeneity across studies included in the meta-regression analysis. To interpret
the findings, structural features that demonstrate significant effects in both analytical
approaches are considered as the most effective structural solutions to promote cooperation;
structural features that are supported only within an independent meta-analysis (i.e., small to
medium effect sizes), but not in the meta-regression, are considered less effective structural
solutions; and structural features that do not exhibit a significant influence on cooperation in
each hypothesis found support from our two meta-analytic approaches.

Reducing Conflict of Interests in Social Dilemmas
Although each social dilemma contains a conflict of interest between the individual
and the collective, social dilemmas can vary in the degree of conflicting interests. In some
situations, there is a stronger temptation to defect and free-ride on other‚Äôs cooperation, but in
others self-interest is nearly aligned with the collective interest. Since the beginning of
research on social dilemmas, researchers found that reducing the degree of conflicting
interests can increase cooperation (Deutsch, 1949; Rapoport, 1967). This subsequently led to
attempts to identify solutions to resolving the free-rider problems by using rules to modify
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 49
social dilemmas in a way that reduces the conflict of interests and make cooperation more
aligned with self-interest (Kollock, 1998; Powers & Lehmann, 2013).
In the present studies, we operationalized the degree of conflicting interests between
studies with the K index (i.e., higher K index indicates less conflict of interest, Rapoport,
1967) and additionally by comparing step-level PGDs and continuous PGDs (Abele et al.,
2010). As predicted, there was less cooperation in social dilemmas characterized by stronger
conflicting interests (i.e., in studies with lower K index and within continuous versus step-
level PGDs, H1). We further analyzed studies that manipulated the degree of conflicting
interests within the study, and found that cooperation was higher in experimental conditions
with less, compared to higher, conflict of interests.
These findings provide support for the role of self-interest in predicting whether
people cooperate or defect in social dilemmas (Kelley & Thibaut, 1978), and suggest that a
fundamental route through which to increase cooperation is by reducing the conflict of
interests in a situation and making cooperation appeal to the self-interest of individuals.
While this can be easy to achieve in artificial laboratory studies, this is a formidable task to
groups, leaders, and policy makers, and decades of research has been conducted to
understand the institutions that can be created to shift the incentive structures and behaviors
of individuals facing social dilemmas. The present meta-analysis identifies which
institutional rules are more or less effective in promoting cooperation.
Effective Structural Solutions to Social Dilemmas: Communication and Sanctions
We found that the most effective structural solutions involved making certain
behaviors possible within the dilemma (i.e., allowing for communication) and assigning costs
and benefits to specific behaviors (i.e., rewards for cooperation and punishment for non-
cooperation). These conclusions were supported by findings from both Study 1 and 2.
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 50
Communication
We found that the presence of communication was positively associated with
cooperation and explained 4% of the variance in cooperation among PDs and PGDs (H4a).
This result was replicated in our meta-analysis using within-study effect sizes, which again
found a positive effect of the presence versus absence of communication (d = 0.46).
Communication was one of the earliest studied solutions to social dilemmas (Deutsch, 1958),
and has been replicated in experiments where communication among participants was
allowed before or during the social dilemma (e.g., Balliet, 2010; Bicchieri & Lev-on, 2007;
Ledyard, 1995; Sally, 1995; Shank et al., 2019). Communication allows people to make (non-
binding) commitments that they will cooperate (Kerr et al., 1997), which can promote trust
and allow for coordination. Bicchieri (2002) suggested that communication about the social
dilemma can focus individuals on prosocial norms (e.g., norm of promise keeping), which are
conducive to cooperation. Interestingly, communication about norms can help sustain
cooperation through not only the verification of prosocial norms, but also reminding
individuals of the potential reputational damage of non-cooperative behavior (Shank et al.,
2019).
Furthermore, we found that bi-directional communication is associated with higher
level of cooperation compared to uni-directional communication (H4b). In fact, merely
discussing a prosocial norm might not be enough to foster cooperation within a group, it is
also crucial that people form expectations about other‚Äôs cooperation (Bicchieri & Lev-on,
2007). Importantly, previous research found that perceived consensus can strengthen the
effect of a norm on behavioral intentions (Kusumi et al., 2017). Compared to uni-directional
communication, bi-directional communication allows people to both receive information and
respond to others. This can generate mutual expectations of cooperation and perceived
consensus of prosocial norms among individuals and therefore result in more cooperation.

We found that communication was more effective in promoting cooperation in
situations involving relatively stronger conflicting interests. Within social dilemmas that
contain more conflict of interests, people are less likely to cooperate and expect less
cooperation from others (Balliet & Van Lange, 2013), which may reflect a higher need for
mutual trust and coordination to address the free-rider problem. Previous research has found
that communication gives people the chance to make commitments and promises (Bicchieri
& Lev-on, 2007), and increases group members‚Äô experience of common fate (Bouas &
Komorita, 1996), consensus favoring cooperation (Dawes et al., 1977), and trusting and
trustworthiness (Ben-Ner & Putterman, 2009). Thus, the communication effect can become
stronger when people face higher degrees of conflict in social dilemmas.
Sanctions
Both game theory and interdependence theory suggest that sanctions (i.e., reward,
punishment) could be an effective promoter of cooperation in social dilemmas, because it
provides incentives for individuals to engage in cooperative behaviors in their pursuit of self-
interest. Indeed, we found that the presence of sanctions is associated with a higher level of
cooperation in social dilemmas, explaining approximately 2%~3% of the variance in
cooperation (H7a). Moreover, meta-analytic results analyzing studies that manipulated the
presence of sanctions within studies revealed that both punishment (d = 0.60) and reward (d =
0.46) are effective in enhancing cooperation.
Analyzing the effect of specific implementations of sanctions (e.g., sanctioning agent,
sanctioning effectiveness) goes beyond the scope of the current meta-analyses. However, we
additionally tested whether the mere presence of sanctions interacted with other rules to
promote cooperation. Specifically, we found that sanctions more strongly increase
cooperation when participants repeatedly interacted with the same partners, compared to one-
shot interactions with strangers (H7b). This result replicated the findings from a previous
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 52
meta-analysis, which found that both reward and punishment are more effective when these
sanctions are present over time during repeated interactions with the same group members
(Balliet et al., 2011). This finding may be explained by participants becoming more
cooperative by either directly experiencing punishment or reward (Skinner, 1953) and/or
observing others receiving rewards and punishments (Bandura, 1977). Partner matching may
also allow groups to use sanctions to develop trust that others will cooperate, as well as
expectations about group behavior and social norms (Balliet et al., 2011). Indeed, sanctions
have been found to be more effective during repeated interactions with the same partner (or
group), compared to repeated interactions with a different partner (or group), which suggests
that developing trust and norms within a stable group may play an important role in
determining the effectiveness of sanctioning institutions.
Communication versus Sanctions
The present study offers a unique opportunity to compare the magnitude of the effect
of communication and sanctions on cooperation. Importantly, we found that communication
and sanctions have approximately equivalent main effects on cooperation, either by
comparing the explained variance by each of these variables (4% vs. 2%~3%, respectively)
or by examining the magnitude of the effect of communication (d = 0.46), punishment (d =
0.60), and reward (d = 0.46). Though there are slight differences between the effect sizes, we
can conclude that communication and sanctions are both key institutional solutions and are
observed to have similar effectiveness in increasing cooperation in social dilemmas. Indeed,
previous research has identified these as some of the most promising solutions to deal with
social dilemmas experienced by organizations and communities. For example, Ostrom and
colleagues (1994) have provided both experimental findings and evidence from field studies
in different contexts and communities, which all converge to a single conclusion: a richer set
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 53
of institutional solutions that enable communication and/or allow for the sanctioning of non-
cooperation can generate better collective outcomes.
Though the present meta-analysis allows us to compare the effect of communication
and sanctions on cooperation, it remains unclear which solution is more efficient in promoting
cooperation. Prior research found that compared to communication, punishment did increase
contributions but had little net effect on efficiency because of its cost (Bochet et al., 2006).
However, both communication and sanctions can be costly to group members and therefore
poses a second-order social dilemma (Ostrom et al., 1994; Yamagishi, 1988a). Research has
also found that in the presence of communication, adding a (costly) punishment option did
not additionally increase cooperation that much (Bochet et al., 2006), and did not lead to as
long-lasting cooperation as communication without punishment (Janssen et al., 2010). Future
research could compare whether communication and sanctions are equally efficient
institutions at enhancing cooperation when people must pay an equivalent cost to either
communicate with group members or sanction group members. Besides, future field research
can measure the cost and efficiency of the implementation of various policies relating to
communication and sanctions to affect cooperation in social dilemmas in the field.
Less Effective Structural Solutions: Smaller Group Size and Partner Matching
We found less consistent support for other rules used to construct social dilemmas,
such as adjusting group size, and allowing for repeated interactions with the same partner. In
Study 1, we found that variation in these features of social dilemmas were unrelated with
variation between estimates of cooperation. However, in Study 2, the meta-analyses that
analyzed the within-study manipulations of these features of situations found that both
displayed the predicted influence on cooperation.
This discrepancy in the results could have occurred because these features of social
dilemmas have a weaker influence on cooperation than communication and sanctions. In the
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 54
meta-regression predicting the cooperation estimates, there are many differences between the
studies (and their treatments) which could affect cooperation, and it could be that this
variation in cooperation produces error that masks the small effects of these features of the
social dilemmas. However, when studies are designed to specifically test how a feature of the
situation relates to cooperation, then only one feature of the social dilemma differs between
experimental treatments (e.g., group size), and this method is ideal for testing small effect
sizes. Indeed, when considering the results of the meta-analyses, each of these effect sizes
were smaller than those observed for either communication or sanctions.
Group Size
Group size was predicted to have a negative effect on cooperation. In the meta-
analysis of studies that manipulated group size within a study (i.e., d values), we found less
cooperation in relatively larger, compared to smaller, groups (H2). Increased group sizes are
associated with lower self-efficacy (Kerr, 1989), lower sense of responsibility (Forsyth et al.,
2002), greater perceived anonymity (Van Vugt & Samuelson, 1999), and greater perceived
risk of encountering at least one defector (Barcelo & Capraro, 2015; Capraro, 2013), all of
which could account for a decrease in cooperation in larger groups. In the meta-regression,
however, we found that differences in group size between studies (and treatments) were not
associated with cooperation.
Meta-regression could have been a more suitable approach to examine the association
between group size and cooperation, relative to the meta-analysis using within-study effect
sizes. This is because group size can covary with the incentives for cooperation and thereby
alter the degree of conflicting interests across situations (Bonacich et al., 1976; Wu et al.,
2020). For example, Barcelo and Capraro (2015) examined the effect of group size on
cooperation in two economics games where the incentives to cooperate either increased or
decreased with expanding group size, and found that larger groups were more cooperative in
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 55
the public goods game (where individual gains increased with group size), but less
cooperative in the n-person prisoner‚Äôs dilemma (where individual gain remained constant as
the group size increased). Within the meta-regression analysis, we statistically controlled for
the conflict of interests within the social dilemma, which could be confounded with group
size. However, a general limitation of this entire body of evidence, is that most of the studies
included very small group sizes. Future research can better control for confounds when
manipulating group size, and also observe cooperation within larger groups.
Matching Design
People were more cooperative when they interacted with the same partner(s) for
multiple trials, compared to only once (d = 0.46, H8) . When the social dilemma changes
from stranger to partner matching, then people will weigh the benefit of exploiting others in a
single round against the cost of maintaining mutual cooperation in future rounds, and this can
help to align self-interest with cooperation (Andreoni, 1988). From the perspective of
interdependence theory, repeated interactions with the same partner may enhance concerns
for a partners‚Äô interests, afford goals that value future outcomes, and encourage strategic
considerations (Kelley et al., 2003). Indeed, previous evidence showed that compared to
stranger matching, people involved in partner matching tend to form higher expectations
about a partner‚Äôs cooperation and make greater contributions to the public good (Nihonsugi,
2016). Moreover, reputational concerns and social norms can develop over time and maintain
cooperation during repeated interaction with the same partner(s) (Axelrod, 1984; Nowak &
Sigmund, 2005; Ostrom, 2014).
Yet, the meta-regression results showed that repeated interaction with the same
partners was not associated with higher cooperation estimates. In the meta-regression,
matching design only captures whether people were playing with the same partner across

Non-Effective Structural Solutions to Social Dilemmas
Several structural features were found to have little, if any, impact on cooperation.
These included features that affect the symmetry of the social dilemma structure,
sequentiality of decision making, and payment associated with outcomes.
Sequentiality
The current meta-analysis did not find evidence supporting the hypothesis that there
would be more cooperation when people make their decisions simultaneously than
sequentially (H3). A game theoretical perspective would suggest that sequential moving may
decrease cooperation, since self-interested individuals tend to avoid being exploited by others
and reduce cooperation under low expectation about other‚Äôs cooperation. Furthermore,
simultaneous decision makers tend to perceive higher sense of groupness and engage in more
cooperation, compared to sequential movers (Abele & Ehrhart, 2005). However, previous
research has found that both free-riders and conditional cooperators exist among participants
in experimental games, and conditional cooperation can arise especially when participants are
inequity averse (Fehr & Schmidt, 1999; Fischbacher et al., 2001). Indeed, sequentiality
allows for the signaling and communication of the previous mover‚Äôs prosociality within each
round of interaction, which may elicit cooperative responses among those conditional
cooperators. It may also be possible to develop sequential reciprocity if participants engage in
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 57
dynamic interactions and are provided with feedback about other‚Äôs behavior. Future research
may explore whether complete versus incomplete information about what former mover(s)
have decided in a sequential game would play a role in the relation between sequential versus
simultaneous decisions and cooperation.
Symmetry
We did not find support that asymmetric groups would be associated with more
cooperation, compared to symmetric groups (H6). From the perspective of the functional
model of hierarchy, hierarchical differentiation can promote cooperation and performance
(Halevy et al., 2011). Yet, it also posited that hierarchy comes in different shapes and forms,
and interdependence among members, perceived legitimacy of the hierarchy (e.g., Gross et
al., 2016), and the misalignment of different bases of the hierarchy (e.g., power and status,
Anicich et al., 2016) may moderate the effect of hierarchy on group functioning and
performance (Halevy et al., 2011). In the present meta-analysis, hierarchical differentiation
was operationalized as asymmetries in different aspects of a social dilemma structure, such as
the initial endowment size, payoff matrix and role in the game. Possibly the different
operationalizations of power could each afford some distinct psychological processes to
affect behavior. For example, inequality in the initial endowment reflects the asymmetry in
control over resources (i.e., power), but may also evoke perceptions of fairness that can
impact cooperation. The reference point for fair contributions tend to be clearer under equal
endowments (e.g., everyone contribute 50%), compared to unequal endowments (e.g., equal
contributions in proportion or absolute amount). This lack of clarity may lead to biased
reasoning and self-serving perceptions of fairness, potentially hindering cooperation within
groups with asymmetric endowments. Future research may examine the different types of
asymmetries in social dilemmas and the specific and shared psychological processes by
which various types of asymmetry influence cooperation.

Payment
First, we found that compensation for participation was not associated with
cooperation in the meta-regression, and showed a small positive relation with cooperation in
the independent meta-analysis, which is not in line with our prediction (H5a). A potential
explanation is that compared to those who have not received a show-up fee, individuals who
receive such a fee may perceive they have already secured a gain, making them less risk-
averse, and thus be more inclined to cooperate. Second, we found that cooperation did not
vary between situations in which monetary/non-monetary payment were provided versus
hypothetical outcomes using the meta-regression approach (H5b). According to our meta-
analysis of studies that manipulated hypothetical versus real payments, there was a negligible
increase in cooperation among participants who received a game payment. We did not find
support for the hypothesis that performance-based payment would interact with matching
design to affect cooperation (H5c). Furthermore, among the incentivized games, we did not
observe differences in cooperation between different payment types, such as monetary versus
non-monetary payments and lottery versus non-lottery payment. These findings replicate
previous studies which found that cooperation did not vary between monetary and other
rewards (Zelmer, 2003), or between random incentivized and full payment treatments (Clot et
al., 2018). The evidence regarding the effect of real versus hypothetical payments on
cooperation was inconsistent with previous research (e.g., Locey et al., 2011; Vlaev, 2012).
These inconsistent findings underscore the need for future experiments to test theories and
hypotheses concerning the necessity of incentives to be used to study cooperation.
Methodological Implications
The current meta-analysis also investigated how several methodological features of
the social dilemma paradigms, including the sample characteristics, were associated with
cooperation.
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 59
Deception
We found that studies which used deception were associated with higher level of
cooperation, though deception only explained a tiny amount of variation in cooperation
across studies (0.4%). Generally, the use of deception in experimental games ‚Äì although
necessary to obtain control in certain experiments ‚Äì has been argued to affect how
participants behave during experiments (Bonetti, 1998; Jamison et al., 2008; Levitt & List,
2007). A potential explanation is that participants may become suspicious about the
experiment due to either direct or indirect exposure to deception (Ortmann & Hertwig, 2002),
and therefore take the experiment less seriously (Jamison et al., 2008) and tend to behave in a
socially desirable way (i.e., making cooperative decisions). The current analyses only
considered whether deception was used in the experiment, but not whether deception was
used within the participant pool. Therefore, our study is unable to examine whether the effect
of deception was due to its direct use within the study or indirectly through exposure to
deception in previous studies. An important direction for future research is to better
understand the consequences of the use of deception within experiments, both within studies
and participant pools.
Sample Characteristics
We found no support that female (vs. male) and psychology (vs. economic) students
would engage in more (less) cooperation in social dilemmas ‚Äì as these sample characteristics
were not associated with differences in the cooperation between studies, and these effects
were neither detected by meta-analyzing within-study effect sizes. Indeed, prior meta-
analyses found no difference in cooperation between men and women (Balliet et al., 2011;
Spadaro, Jin, & Balliet, 2023). We additionally compared student sample with non-student
sample, and found no difference in the amount of cooperation observed across samples (see
SI). This finding is in line with a recent study which found that behaviors are similar across
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 60
student and non-student samples, and that generalizable inferences about human behavior can
be made from standard student-based experiments (Snowberg & Yariv, 2021). Though there
is mixed evidence about the relation between age and cooperation (e.g., Romano, Bortolotti,
et al., 2021), we detected a positive association between age and cooperation, albeit with a
relatively small effect size, while meta-analyzing within-study effect sizes. We further
identify that sample characteristics, including age, gender and academic discipline, were
relevant predictors of the cooperation estimates using the adapted random forests algorithm
for meta-analysis (i.e., MetaForest), a method which can account for possible non-linear
relations (see SI). These findings suggest that sample features may not have a simple linear
relation with cooperation, and future research is encouraged to explore possible non-linear
relations.
Control Variables
We controlled for several additional methodological differences between the social
dilemmas (i.e., experimental setting, trial of cooperation, choice type), whether the
paper/study had been published or not, and the year of data collection. Importantly, we found
no differences in cooperation between lab and online studies. This replicates previous work
which has found similar patterns of cooperation between the lab and online studies (Horton et
al., 2011), and suggest online social dilemmas experiments can be an equally valid method to
study cooperation (Arechar et al., 2018). Besides, we found the amount of cooperation
observed in a study did not differ between whether the study was published or not, which
suggests this finding may not be affected by publication bias. The level of cooperation
between studies did not differ according to whether the data were observed during the first
trial or across all trials of the social dilemma, but we did observe more cooperation in
continuous-choice social dilemmas, compared to when participants made dichotomous
choices to either cooperate or defect. We did not put forward an a priori hypothesis about the
INSTITUTIONS AND COOPERATION 61
effect of choice type on cooperation. And prior research on the trust game has observed a
different result: First mover‚Äôs in the trust game made less investments (i.e., cooperation)
within continuous-choice trust games, compared to dichotomous-choice trust games (Schniter
et al., 2013). Future research can explore the potential effect of choice type in social
dilemmas to understand which type of the action space affords greater cooperation.
Lastly, we found that the level of cooperation observed across studies displays an
increase over the past several decades. Recently, Yuan, Spadaro and colleagues (2022)
applied cross-temporal meta-analytic techniques to social dilemmas studies conducted in the
United States and found the level of cooperation has increased in American society in the last
60 years. The same increase in cooperation was found across the last several decades of
research within studies conducted in China (Yuan, Wu, et al., 2024). One possible
explanation of this trend in cooperation is the rise in urbanization and relational mobility
within American and Chinese societies, which could be associated with higher trust and
cooperation among strangers (Thomson et al., 2018).
Strengths and Limitations
There are a few strengths and limitations of the present studies worth mentioning.
First, we used a total sample of 2,340 cooperation estimates obtained from 1,527 studies
comprising 229,528 participants in the meta-regression. This large scale meta-analytic dataset
increased the precision of the population cooperation estimation (Cohn & Decker, 2003) and
lowered the risk of overfitting while conducting the meta-regression analysis (Higgins &
Thompson, 2004). However, we also observed large heterogeneity among the estimates of
cooperation. This was reflected in the wide range of the prediction intervals, which suggest
there is a broad range of possible true outcomes of cooperation within any single study, and
questions the value of the overall estimated amount of cooperation (IntHout et al., 2016).

Second, the present studies applied complementary analytic approaches to replicate
our findings using different techniques, including meta-regression modeling in Study 1 and
traditional meta-analytic methods in Study 2. We used both forced entry and machine-
learning based exploratory methods (see MetaForest analysis in the SI) to build multiple
meta-regression models. Our forced entry models were based on predefined and theory-led
decisions, which included sample, methodological variables and paper meta-data as controls
while examining the association between structural features of social dilemmas and
cooperation. However, due to highly standardized social dilemma paradigms used in past
research, some values were frequently used for parameter setting in the experiments while
others were less commonly used. Indeed, we observed little variation in several features of
social dilemmas, such as sequentiality (91% simultaneous decision-making), symmetry (92%
symmetric games), communication (90% without communication opportunities) and sanction
influences. To address this limitation, we additionally conducted meta-analyses that analyzed
within-study manipulations of the structural features to test the hypotheses. This approach is
capable of detecting smaller effect sizes and also allows us to compare the magnitude of
standardized effect sizes.
Third, we should acknowledge that the current study did not capture cross-societal
variation in cooperation across studies or individual differences in response to structural
influences. Some previous research has found that individuals and societies differ in
impersonal cooperation (e.g., Herrmann et al., 2008; Romano, Sutter, et al., 2021; Thielmann
et al., 2020). Moreover, features of the situation may vary in their impact on cooperation
across individuals (e.g. personality) and societies (e.g., culture). For example, punishment
was found to be positively associated with cooperation in some societies but not others
(G√§chter & Herrmann, 2009). Recently, Spadaro, Graf, et al. (2022) performed a large-scale

meta-analysis on 1,506 studies using PDs and PGDs conducted across 70 societies (k = 2,271)
to examine the cross-societal variation in impersonal cooperation. They found little, if any,
cross-societal variation in cooperation after controlling for study characteristics, including
several structural features analyzed in the present study (e.g., sanction, communication,
matching design). Their finding suggested that cooperation between strangers may be an
essential and general characteristic of large-scale modern societies, and that the variation of
cooperation in experimental social dilemmas can be better explained by the rules of the game,
as opposed to the society or culture in which the study was conducted.
Concluding Remarks
For decades, researchers have been observing how different structural features affect
behavior within highly standardized, internally valid models of social dilemmas. The
variation in the rules applied by researchers to create social dilemmas can inform the efforts
of policy makers, managers, and community organizations about the possible institutional
solutions to promote cooperation. Ostrom‚Äôs institutional analysis framework (2009) identifies
possible aspects of social dilemmas in which rules can be designed to alter the number of
participants, including their positions and level of control over outcomes, the possible
behaviors, the costs and benefits of their behaviors, and whether strategic interactions are
possible. Of the nine rules about structural changes that were examined in this meta-analysis,
we found that communication and sanctions were the most effective solutions to promote
cooperation, while smaller group size and partner matching were less effective in promoting
cooperation. We found no evidence to support the hypotheses that rules about the symmetry
of the dilemma, sequentiality of decisions and payment affect cooperation.
We found that cooperation was indeed lower in social dilemmas that involved a
stronger conflict of interests, and several of these rules can be wielded to fundamentally alter
the social dilemma in way to align self-interest with collective interests, such as sanctions and

partner matching. Yet, some rules that were hypothesized to encourage cooperation through
the rational pursuit of self-interest, such as sequential decisions, had no influence on
cooperation. Importantly, rules about structural changes can also affect psychological
processes beyond a rational pursuit of self-interest. Rules can be used to establish trust that
others will cooperate, encourage people to assign greater value to collective outcomes, that
facilitate the learning of social norms, and which can influence how biases in decision
making affect behavior. In fact, evidence exists that communication can affect cooperation
through each of these cognitive and motivational processes. Policy makers or managers of
organizations who create rules to manage social dilemmas can benefit from the knowledge
acquired from decades of research on cooperation, and attend to the many possible rules and
corresponding psychological processes that affect cooperation. In turn, scientists can observe
the impact of institutional rules developed to promote cooperation, measure the efficacy and
efficiency of the many possible rule configurations in social dilemmas, and sketch out the key
psychological processes through which these rules affect cooperation.
