ABSTRACT
Cooperation is essential for human societies, but not all individuals cooperate to the same
degree. This is typically attributed to individual motives - for example, to be prosocial or to
avoid risks. Here, we investigate whether cooperative behavior can, in addition, reflect what
people pay attention to and whether cooperation may, therefore, be influenced
by manipulations that direct attention. We first analyze the attentional patterns of subjects
playing one-shot Prisonerâ€™s Dilemma games and find that choices indeed systematically relate
to attention to specific social outcomes, as well as to individual eye movement patterns
reflecting attentional strategies. To test for the causal impact of attention independently
of subjectsâ€™ prosocial and risk attitudes, we manipulated the task display and found that
cooperation is enhanced when displays facilitate attention to othersâ€™ outcomes. Machine
learning classifiers trained on these attentional patterns confirm that attentional strategies
measured using eye-tracking can accurately predict cooperation out-of-sample. Our findings
demonstrate that theories of cooperation need to incorporate attention and that attentional
interventions can improve cooperative outcomes.
INTRODUCTION
Cooperation â€“ the ability of individuals to incur a personal cost to achieve a group benefit â€“ is
1,2 3
a hallmark of human social behavior and is essential for the functioning of our societies .
Understanding why people engage in cooperation rather than behaving selfishly, and how we
can foster cooperation, is important for many pressing global challenges, including
combatting climate change, curbing infectious diseases, and addressing problems associated
with aging populations.
Natural selection implies competition, but cooperation is observed in both animal and human
groups . Evolutionary biology offers several potential explanations for the emergence of
5,6
cooperation, including kin selection, reciprocity, byproduct benefits, and group selection .
To understand and improve cooperative outcomes in human behavior, it is crucial to pin down
the specific motivations and decision mechanisms underlying cooperative choices .
The existing literature suggests several possible motives underlying cooperation. First,
cooperation may depend on the outcomes of the decisions. This is suggested by lab studies
using economic games such as the Prisonersâ€™ Dilemma, where two individuals choose to
either cooperate or defect in the presence of a trade-off between individual and group
outcomes. These games represent many real-world situations where an individual has the
incentive to free-ride and take advantage of others - for instance, group projects, the use of
public goods such as parks and transportation, or the division of household chores. A vast
behavioral literature exploring individual and group behavior in various possible designs,
settings, and conditions of these games has developed in economics, psychology, and
sociology since the early 1960s . In these studies, most individuals do not cooperate or defect
categorically but choose systematically whether to cooperate in line with the relative size of
9â€“15
the different payoffs .
Second, individual differences in cooperation may reflect individual beliefs and preferences.
16â€“19
Most prominent in this respect may be social or other-regarding preferences : People
often cooperate with strangers due to their concern for other peopleâ€™s wellbeing , but could
21â€“23
also do so due to other motives such as â€œwarm glowâ€ or empathy . Non-social preferences
related to specific circumstances may also influence cooperation. For example, cooperation
can break down due to risk aversion: One might refuse to vaccinate due to personal fear of
potential health issues. If another person then suspects that such an individual will not
reciprocate and could therefore put their personal outcome (e.g., health) at risk, they might
also choose not to engage in cooperation .
Finally, in addition to incentives and individual preferences, cooperation may also relate to
attention and sampling of the information on which the choices are based. Studies using
25â€“32
choice process data, such as gaze duration and saccades , have revealed that subjects do
not pay equal attention to all pieces of information but vary considerably in their eye
movement patterns . This suggests that individuals shift their overt attention in line with
simplified information sampling strategies, often not using all available information to the
34,35
same degree . This may partially reflect goal-directed attention guided by preferences and
choice strategies. For example, in social dilemmas, individuals with strong other-regarding
preferences exhibit gaze patterns favoring othersâ€™ payoffs . Moreover, individuals who tend
to play closer to equilibrium strategies â€“ or who use a higher level of strategic sophistication
â€“ pay attention to both their own and their opponentâ€™s payoffs, whereas individuals who fail
to play the equilibrium strategies only sample their payoffs . Finally, other studies suggest
that altruistic individuals attend to both playersâ€™ payoffs, whereas selfish ones often only
sample their own payoff .
Attention is not only guided by behavioral goals but can also be driven exogenously by
39â€“41
characteristics of the environment and the choice-relevant stimuli , such as the
42 43

cooperative behavior have mainly examined goal-directed attention and have by and large
not considered how behavior may relate to how attention can be captured exogenously, for
instance by changing the salience and location of the payoff information on the screen. This
contrasts with the literature on choices in non-social contexts, where it has been consistently
shown that value-based choices are strongly affected by exogenous attention

of an item leads to a stronger behavioral preference towards the item , and corresponding
exogenous attention manipulations during moral decisions have shown that directing the

Here, we integrate these separate perspectives and investigate how attentional information
sampling subserving cooperative decision-making depends on both individualsâ€™ preferences
and exogenous manipulation of information presentation. To disentangle the effects of
outcomes, preferences, and attentional mechanisms on cooperative decisions, we use one-
shot Prisonerâ€™s dilemma (PD) games combined with computational choice models and eye-
tracking. Such PD games involve four distinct payoffs: reward (R) for mutual cooperation,
temptation (T) for one-sided defection, punishment for mutual defection (P), and suckerâ€™s
payoff (S) for one-sided cooperation. In a one-shot PD game, typically T > R > P > S, so the
dominant strategy for a player is to always defect, as this strategy guarantees a higher payoff
(T or P) independent of the opponentâ€™s choice. If both players defect (as they should do in
Nash equilibrium if they are rational and optimal), they both receive a punishment payoff P,
a worse outcome than they could have obtained if they had cooperated (R).
In our experiment, subjects played 192 trials of this game with a real opponent seated in a
separate room and did not receive feedback on the opponentâ€™s choice after each game. To
capture the effects of outcomes and distinct motives on cooperative choices in different
individuals, we systematically varied the four payoff values. Specifically, we generated 96
unique PD games, by varying the four payoffs between 0 and 40, with a step size of 5 Swiss
estimate individual other-regarding preferences, risk attitudes, and subjective prior beliefs
about potential cooperation by the opponent. The one-shot games we used circumvented
other dynamic factors that could also influence cooperation, such as belief updating or direct

METHODS
Subjects
We recruited 88 healthy volunteers (48 females, aged 18 to 35 years old, mean: 24.6, std: 3.1)
from the subject pool of the Department of Economics at the University of Zurich to take part
in the eye-tracking experiment. We determined the target sample size (N = 84) needed to
estimate a significant correlation (significance level of 0.05, power 80%) between an
individualâ€™s eye tracking measures and behavioral measures such as cooperation rate,
assuming a weak relationship with a Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.3.
In parallel, another set of 88 subjects recruited from the subject pool of the Department of
Economics at the University of Zurich participated in a simultaneous fMRI session and acted
as partners in the task. All subjects were right-handed, medication-free, and with normal or
corrected-to-normal eyesight (using contact lenses). All subjects provided written informed
consent. The study was approved by the ethics committee of the Canton of Zurich. The study
was not preregistered.
Task
trial, two subjects (main subject, playing columns, and their opponent playing rows) chose
between two options labelled â€œAâ€ and â€œBâ€ (corresponding to cooperation or defection, with
labelling counterbalanced across subjects). The payoffs were determined by the choices of
both players, leading to four possible outcomes. If both players cooperated, they both earned
the reward payoff (R). If they both defected, they both earned the punishment payoff (P < R).
If one player cooperated but the other one defected, the player who defected earned the
temptation payoff (T > R) while the player who cooperated received the suckerâ€™s payoff (S <
P). We did not use the words â€œcooperationâ€ or â€œdefectionâ€ anywhere in the instructions or
display to avoid influencing the subjectâ€™s choices. Instead, subjects chose between a left and
a right column, labelled A and B, and were told that the other player chose between the top
and bottom row, also labelled A and B. The meaning of the letters (cooperation/defection)
remained constant for each subject and was counterbalanced across subjects. We
manipulated the position of the defect or cooperate columns and rows within-subject. The
position of rows and columns was randomly determined for each trial and counterbalanced
throughout the experiment.
At the end of the experiment, one trial was randomly selected, and the decision of the second
player was shown to the subjects. This trial was then paid according to the choices of both
subjects, with one point corresponding to 1 Swiss franc (around 1 USD at the time of the
experiment). The subjects were also paid a 20 CHF show-up fee.
Trial presentation and timing
We implemented the task in Matlab 2018b, using the Psychophysics Toolbox 3 extension .
On each trial, subjects were presented with a new payoff matrix, displayed as four squares
containing the potential payoffs of the subject (â€œyouâ€) and the second subject (â€œotherâ€)
represented as numbers and green (for â€œyouâ€) and orange (for the â€œotherâ€) rectangles. The
had a maximum of 15 seconds to choose either A or B by pressing the left (for the left column)
or right (for the right column) arrows on the keyboard. As soon as they chose one option, a
yellow square framed the column selected for 1.5s, and the payoffs disappeared for 1.5s
the other player until they completed all trials.
Trial construction
We systematically varied the four possible payoffs (reward R, temptation T, suckerâ€™s S, and
Prisonerâ€™s Dilemma games where payoffs could take values between 0 and 40 points with an
increment of 5, and where the following rules were respected: T > R, P > S, R > P, T > S, and
2R > T + S. This procedure yielded 96 distinct games. Each subject played each game twice (96
games in random order in the first half, and the same 96 games in random order in the second
half).
Experimental session
We brought the eye-tracking and fMRI subjects to the lab separately to make sure they
remained anonymous. Both subjects were informed of the existence of another subject
playing the same game in an adjacent room, and that one of their mutual choices would be
implemented at the end of the experiment. The eye-tracking subject entered the lab after the
fMRI subject had started the experiment and sat in a separate room. At the end of their
session, eye-tracking subjects waited until the fMRI session was over. Both subjects received
simultaneous feedback on one randomly selected trial.
After completing a short comprehension quiz and two practice trials, eye-tracking subjects
completed two blocks of 96 trials of the task each. At the end of the experiment, subjects
filled out a short demographic questionnaire, stating their age, sex, nationality, field of
studies, religious and political orientation, monthly disposable income, family affluence, and
community size.

Statistical analysis
We analyzed the effects of payoffs, options position, and gaze patterns using generalized
mixed effect models implemented in R (R Core Team 2017), using the lme4 package. We
analyzed trial-by-trial choices (cooperation vs defection), and binary eye metrics (first gaze to
â€œyouâ€ vs â€œotherâ€, first gaze to payoffs, etc.) using mixed-effects logistic regressions (glmer,
probit link function) including random intercepts and slopes for all independent variables for
each subject. We analyzed the effects of option position on fitted model parameters using
fixed effects linear regression, and the effects of positions and payoffs on gaze duration and
gaze frequency using mixed-effect linear models including random intercepts and slopes for
each subject.
Out-of-sample predictions of cooperation
We used the trial-specific gaze patterns of each subject to obtain out-of-sample predictions
of cooperation. We used random tree classifiers (fitctree implemented in MATLAB) trained
on features representing the fixation of each AOI at different time points and predicting the
binary outcome (cooperate vs defect) on individual trials. Specifically, we either dummy-
coded the fixation to an AOI on a specific time point (first, second, third, or last fixation) or
used the value of the sampled payoff on each fixation to create 32 possible features (8 AOIs
x 4 fixations). We used the outcome of each trial (cooperate or defect) as labels. Using a leave-
one-subject-out cross-validation procedure, we trained the classifiers using different
combinations of features (either all or some fixations, or all or some AOIs) on all but one
subject and predicted the trial-by-trial cooperation for the left-out subject. We then averaged
the predicted cooperation, either across games for each subject, or across subjects for each
game, and compared these out-of-sample predictions to the real subject- or game-specific
cooperation rates using mean-squared-error (MSE). We additionally trained simpler
classifiers, using as features the relative dwell time of each AOI on each trial, or the values of
the four different payoffs on each trial, to compare the performance of classifiers trained on
sequences versus summary variables.
Since our dataset was misbalanced (23% of cooperation trials and 77% of defection trials), we
additionally computed the chance level-cooperation rate by shuffling the training-set label 50
times on each cross-validation iteration and predicted the cooperation on the left-out trials
using the classifiers trained on these permutations. We estimated the chance to predict a
cooperation rate at around 0.2.
We compared the game- and subject-based goodness of fit (MSE) of classifiers trained on one
fixation only (first, second, or last) or on a combination of these fixations (first-to-second and
last, first-to-third and last) using all AOIs and either dummy variables or variables containing
the normalized payoff being sampled during the fixation (value of the AOIâ€™s payoff, divided
by the sum of all payoffs of the trial). We additionally compared classifiers trained using data
from one AOI only, using normalized payoffs and all fixations (first to third and last).

DISCUSSION
Our results demonstrate that cooperative behavior is not only affected by the potential
outcomes of social interactions and individual preferences but also by attentional
mechanisms (at least when outcomes are viewed on a computer screen as here). Attention
to othersâ€™ payoffs was associated with increased cooperation rates, on both the individual
and game levels. Exogenous manipulations of the payoff presentation, orthogonal to the
manipulation of payoff values, changed subjects' gaze patterns, leading to changes in
cooperation rates. Subjectsâ€™ attention at the beginning of a trial was naturally attracted to the
top-left part of the screen and to higher payoffs. Changes in the option presentation therefore
affected which payoff information was sampled by the subjects, leading them to specific
sampling sequences (importantly, increasing the chance to sample payoffs of the opponent).
Sequences of fixations to the different payoffs, combined with the payoff values, predicted
trial-by-trial cooperation rates, showing the importance of information sampling patterns in
cooperative decisions.
Our study is the first to systematically evaluate cooperation decisions using a wide range of
payoffs within the same subject, allowing us to simultaneously estimate beliefs, risk, and
other-regarding preferences using one-shot Prisonerâ€™s Dilemma games. Previous studies have
shown that cooperation in such games was influenced by the different payoff values, with
simplified indices derived from payoffs used to predict cooperation across games and showing
9â€“15
the importance of different motives in cooperation decisions . Here we used a different
approach, explaining individual rather than group behavior using utility models based on
standard economic choice assumptions such as risk preferences (stemming from diminishing
59 16
marginal utility) , other-regarding preferences , and expected utility calculation using prior
beliefs. Using an extensive set of models, we found that individualsâ€™ behavior was best
captured by a combination of other-regarding preferences and beliefs about the opponentâ€™s
choices, while risk preferences (on average) did not play an important role. In our study,
however, outcomes were deterministic and only depended on both subjectsâ€™ choices. Studies
using stochastic outcomes could be conducted to evaluate the link between risk preferences
and cooperative behavior in settings closer to real-world decisions.
37,38
Previous studies have linked cooperative behavior to information sampling patterns ,
showing that gaze directed at othersâ€™ payoffs is typically associated with cooperation. These
studies, focusing on goal-directed (endogenous) attentional mechanisms, highlight individual
differences in cooperation that can be associated with different sampling strategies,
reflecting individualsâ€™ levels of pro-sociality , rationality (playing according to game-theoretic
predictions), and strategic sophistication . Supporting these findings, we found that subjects
who attended to the opponentâ€™s payoffs â€“ and in particular the opponentsâ€™ reward â€“
cooperated more, while subjects attending primarily to their payoffs often defected. These
60,61
effects may reflect that people with the ability to mentalize and employ theory-of-mind
may focus attention on othersâ€™ outcomes to determine whether to cooperate. In addition to
these subject-level effects, we found that within-trial gaze sequences that included the
opponentâ€™s payoff were also associated with an increased cooperation rate for those trials.
To disentangle the informational and preferential components of these attentional effects,
future studies could use an asymmetric payoff matrix that orthogonally manipulates rewards
for the subject and their opponent, creating an additional incentive to sample own or otherâ€™s
payoffs.
We found that exogenous manipulations affecting bottom-up attention impacted
cooperation. Simple permutations of option positions on the screen affected individual
cooperation rates, independently from choice outcomes or individual preferences. We found
that these manipulations did not significantly affect the estimations of risk and social
preferences, but instead changed the assumed prior beliefs about cooperation. These results
provide new insights for the important debate on the question of whether attention (affected
for instance by gaze manipulation) can change the way preferences are constructed or
47,62
whether it purely affects the choice process itself . Previous studies focusing on the effects
of goal-directed attention have shown an effect of attention on the subjective values
26,62,63
attributed to attended options , which may partly explain behaviors such as preference
64â€“66 67
reversal or instability .
Studies exogenously driving attention to some choice options in non-social domains found
47,68,69
that such manipulations can bias choices . Extending such exogenous manipulations to
social decisions, we found that stimulus-driven attention affected information sampling and
choices, but not estimated social preferences. These findings suggest that even though our
display manipulations changed attention towards the otherâ€™s payoffs, they did not seem to
change the perception of inequality (captured by the social preference parameter of the
model) but instead appeared to increase prosocial tendencies overall (captured by the
cooperation belief parameter). These findings suggest that new models may be needed that
explicitly address the interplay between these factors that influence subjective valuation
processes underlying prosocial behavior.
Examining potential causes of such bottom-up attentional effects, we found that a habitual
37,55
information sampling pattern , attributed to the left-to-right and up-to-down Western
56,57
writing conventions , may partially underlie these effects. Simple alterations of choice
interfaces, such as changing the position of the options presented therefore influence
53,70,71
information sampling and choices by interacting with this natural pattern. Neural
evidence suggests that initial information processing plays an important role in social
decisions . Here we find that manipulating the position of the options on the screen changed
cooperation rates but did not increase the selection of the top-left option. Instead, after a
first fixation on higher payoffs placed on the upper part of the screen, more complex
mechanisms oriented subjectsâ€™ attention toward specific gaze sequences.
Previous studies of binary choices largely focused on the effects of the first and last fixations
26,47,73
as well as the relative dwell time of each option on decisions across multiple domains .
Cooperation decisions in the Prisonerâ€™s Dilemma, however, are more complex, as four
different payoffs are represented for two different subjects, and the outcomes are conditional
on othersâ€™ decisions. Recent studies of multi-attribute decisions in non-social decisions have
extended findings that the first fixation and the relative dwell time of different options are
related to choices , and have shown that gaze allocations to options and attributes have
interactive influences on choices .
Studies on multi-attribute decisions in social dilemmas have focused on the frequencies of
transitions between fixations of different payoffs and found that these reflected distinct social
36,38 37
preferences in dictator games , or strategic sophistication in strategic games . However,
these studies did not evaluate the temporal structure of information sampling, and the
potential differential effects of fixations occurring in different temporal order. Our results
showed that the first and last fixations or overall dwell times were not sufficient to accurately
predict cooperation and that options sampled second had a high effect on behavior.
Going further than this overall dwell time, our analysis highlighted the importance of specific
sampling sequences and showed that the order of the fixations of different payoffs was key
to predicting cooperation. Using machine learning, we were able to accurately predict trial-
by-trial cooperation from four-fixation (three first and last) gaze sequences, with higher
accuracy than using the first and last fixations only, showing the importance of studying gaze
sequences. Scanpaths have been used in vision research to compare information-sampling
patterns across individuals or to improve users' experience in human-computer
76 77
interactions , and they have recently been applied to predict choices in economic games .
Here we showed that studying such paths in the context of cooperative decisions improves
predictions of behavior and allows us to explain the effects of exogenous manipulations of
attention on choices, by showing how positioning options at different locations on the screen
modified key information sampling patterns, not limited to the first information subjects
sampled. Extending our results to different games would allow us to test whether the gaze
patterns we measured here generalize across games and individuals.
Limitations
Modifying gaze behavior may affect the content of the information subjects consider for their
decision, or the way this information is processed. More work is needed to uncover the
specific computational mechanisms of individual differences in gaze strategies and how
exactly they influence choices. Further studies could manipulate the temporal presentation
49 78
of options , or the size and saliency of the different payoffs and response options, to
evaluate whether such stronger manipulations could further increase cooperation. Testing
our protocol with populations of non-western origin with different reading conventions, such
as right-to-left Hebrew or Arabic, or top-to-bottom Chinese or Japanese readers could
additionally provide more insight into how different habitual reading patterns combined with
manipulations of information presentation may affect social choices.
Finally, we only tested a simple manipulation of option presentation in a Prisonerâ€™s Dilemma
and found that we could increase average cooperation rates. Our results demonstrate that
social behavior can be exogenously influenced toward more pro-social choices via clearly
measurable attentional mechanisms. Previous studies have suggested the general
79â€“81
importance of choice architecture for nudging behavioral outcomes . Our findings provide
a more mechanistic, attention-based account that could have an impact on the design of real-
world interventions, such as healthcare measures. One example could be new public health
policies, with printed information materials drawing individualsâ€™ attention toward the specific
outcomes of their decisions for others may lead to an increase in cooperation. Another
potential application is in the charitable giving domain where it has been shown that drawing
82â€“84
attention to specific aspects of donations can increase charitable behavior .
