Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 2
Abstract
In a typical probabilistic cuing experiment, participants are asked to find a visual
target among a series of distractors. Although participants are not informed about this,
the target appears more frequently in one region of the display, resulting in faster search
times for targets located in this region. This bias is thought to depend on a habit-like
attentional control mechanism, unconstrained by the availability of working memory
(WM) resources. However, the only study that has explored this feature in the past
suffers from methodological shortcomings that leave the results open to alternative
explanations. In three experiments we aimed to confirm whether probabilistic cuing is
unaffected by visual, spatial, and spatiotemporal WM load. For each experiment, one
group of participants performed the visual search task during the retention interval of a
WM task (high-load group), whereas other group of participants (no-load group) carried
out the visual search task after the WM task. We hypothesized that the probabilistic
cuing effect would be larger for the no-load group compared to the high-load group.
This hypothesis was confirmed in one experiment, but exploratory analyses suggest that
the results can be highly dependent on the analytic approach, casting doubts on its
robustness. Overall, our results provide partial support for the hypothesis that
probabilistic cuing is not affected by a secondary task. However, given that some
analyses reveal an effect of WM load, we conclude that it might be premature to rule
out the possibility that the expression of this attentional bias requires WM resources.
Keywords: probabilistic cuing, attention, implicit cognition, working memory
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 3
Visual statistical learning can improve attentional selection, allowing us to look
for the object we want to find in a more efficient way (Chun & Turk-Browne, 2008;
Goujon et al., 2015). For example, when we want to close a program in our Windows
laptop, we will look for the cross on the upper right side of the program, given that this
is its more probable location. One of the tasks that has been used to study this process is
location probability learning or probabilistic cuing (Jiang & Sisk, 2019). In the typical
task used to explore this effect (e.g., Jiang et al., 2013, 2014), participants search for a
visual target among several distractors and respond to its orientation. The target appears
more often in one region of the display (i.e., the rich quadrant) than in the others (i.e.,
the sparse quadrants). Participants are not informed about the probability distribution of
the target position. However, response times (RT) eventually become faster for trials in
which the target appears in the rich quadrant compared to the sparse quadrants.
Jiang and Sisk (2019) have proposed that the attentional bias produced by
probabilistic cuing is not goal-driven or stimulus-driven. Instead, it could be
characterized as an attentional habit. As such, it shares certain characteristics with motor
habits (Dickinson, 1985; Luque et al., 2020): It is gradual (i.e., it develops after
extensive experience), implicit (i.e., participants are not aware about the spatial
distribution of the target), persistent (once acquired, it is difficult to unlearn, even when
the bias is no longer advantageous), viewer-centered (i.e., the rich region is coded
relative to the participants perspective), transferable only to other serial search tasks,
and insensitive to working memory load (i.e., taxing cognitive resources with a
secondary task does not impede the learning of the bias). In contrast to this view,
previous studies from our laboratory have concluded that probabilistic cuing might not
possess some of these features (Gim√©nez-Fern√°ndez et al., 2020; Vadillo et al., 2020).
In particular, we found no support for the hypotheses that probabilistic cuing is gradual,
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 4
implicit or persistent. The aim of the present series of experiments is to address whether
probabilistic cuing is independent of working memory (WM) load.
So far, this question has only been explored by Won and Jiang (2015). These
authors asked participants to perform a probabilistic cuing task during the retention
interval of a WM task, manipulating the amount of cognitive load demanded by this
secondary task. For half of the trials, the encoding and the probe displays of the WM
task had to be ignored (no-load condition). For the other half (high-load condition),
participants had to decide which stimulus had varied between the encoding and the
probe display (Experiment 1) or whether the encoding display and the probe display
were identical (Experiments 2 and 3). In three experiments, they found that the
magnitude of the probabilistic cuing effect did not diminish under high WM load.
Hence, Won and Jiang (2015) concluded that the bias producing the probabilistic cuing
effect was not dependent on WM resources‚Äîconsistent with the conclusion that it is the
result of an automatic attentional habit.
As we will show, there are reasons to remain skeptical about Won and Jiang
(2015) main conclusions. Firstly, the (in)dependence of WM resources has been also
explored in a similar implicit learning paradigm known as contextual cuing, with less
than conclusive results. In a typical contextual cuing experiment, participants carry out a
visual search task that is superficially identical to the probabilistic cuing task, except
that in this case some of the displays are repeated throughout the task and other displays
are completely new. Eventually, reaction times become faster for repeated compared to
new displays. Several studies found that contextual cuing was not observed if
participants were asked to concurrently perform a WM task (Chen et al., 2019;
Manginelli et al., 2012). However, not all the studies have replicated these results
(Annac et al., 2013, Experiment 2D; Manginelli et al., 2013, Experiment 1) and there
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 5
are several attempts to explain why WM load sometimes does and sometimes does not
impair contextual cuing. For instance, it has been argued that WM load might impair the
expression of contextual cuing but not learning itself (Goujon et al., 2015; Pollmann,
2019). It has also been claimed that contextual cuing might be impaired or not
depending on the type of WM task. There is some consensus that WM tasks with a
strong spatial component impair the expression of contextual cuing more effectively
than other WM tasks (Manginelli et al., 2013; Travis et al., 2013). Given that both,
contextual cuing and probabilistic cuing are thought to share similar mechanisms (Jiang
& Sisk, 2019), the results observed in contextual cuing suggest that probabilistic cuing
may be dependent on WM resources, at least under some conditions.
Furthermore, there are theoretical reasons to cast doubts on the independence of
probabilistic cuing of working memory resources. Working memory allows us to
temporary store and manipulate information and is fundamental to accomplish complex
cognitive tasks such as language comprehension, reasoning, and learning (Baddeley,
1992). Also, numerous studies have found that working memory is intimately related to
selective attention (e.g., Awh et al., 1998; Hollingworth & Luck, 2009; Olivers et al.,
2006). Importantly, Won and Jiang (2015) hypothesized that the probabilistic cuing
effect might be independent of working memory resources because this effect is
considered the outcome of an implicit learning mechanism. However, in a recent study,
we found that, overall, participants seem to be aware of the regularities involved in this
task. Specifically, more than 60% of participants were able to identify the rich quadrant
when asked right after finishing the task, suggesting that the probabilistic cuing effect
may not be entirely unconscious (Gim√©nez-Fern√°ndez et al., 2020). Therefore, during a
probabilistic cuing task, participants may need working memory resources to update the
information about the spatial distribution of the target on a trial-by-trial basis, increasing
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 6
search efficiency. If this hypothesis is correct, the amount of visual, spatial, or
spatiotemporal load imposed by the WM task might determine the extent to which
probabilistic cuing is impaired. In summary, we think that probabilistic cuing may be a
goal-driven attentional bias produced by explicit learning of the spatial distribution of
the target and, as such, it may be dependent of working memory resources.
There are also methodological reasons for taking Won and Jiang‚Äôs (2015) results
with caution. First, each experiment was carried out with a sample of 18 participants.
This sample size is sufficiently large to detect the basic probabilistic cuing effect but
may be inadequate to explore potential differences between WM load conditions. Won
and Jiang (2015) interpreted that WM load does not influence probabilistic cuing
because the interaction between quadrant (rich vs. sparse) and the WM load in
Experiments 2 and 3 did not achieve statistical significance. In Experiment 1, they
found a significant interaction that indicated larger probabilistic cuing under a high WM
load. However, considering such small sample sizes, we think that there are good
reasons to consider this evidence inconclusive. A simple means to illustrate this point is
to quantify how much support these data lend to the hypothesis that probabilistic cuing
was unaffected by WM load. This can be easily done with Bayesian analysis. With the
information reported by Won and Jiang we can estimate that the negative interaction
between quadrant and WM load detected in Experiment 1, F(1, 17) = 5.27, corresponds
to a BF of 1.92 in favor of the alternative hypothesis that cuing is modulated by WM
load (modelled as a Cauchy distribution scaled at .707) over the null hypothesis. This
evidence is insufficient to discriminate between the null and the alternative hypotheses.
For Experiment 2, Won and Jiang did not report sufficient information to conduct a
similar analysis, but for Experiment 3, they reported that the cuing effect was not
significantly different in the high-load and no-load conditions, t(17) = 0.53, which
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 7
corresponds to a BF = 0.27. This can be considered moderate evidence in favor of the
null hypothesis (Lee & Wagenmakers, 2013), but in any case, far from conclusive.
These complementary Bayesian analyses suggest that it would be desirable to put these
hypotheses to the test with larger samples.
Setting statistical considerations aside, we think that the procedure employed by
Won and Jiang (2005) can be improved. In Experiment 1, the rich quadrant was the
same for both the high-load and the no-load conditions. Even under the assumption that
a high WM load can interfere with probabilistic cuing, one would expect that any
learning taking place in the no-load condition could generalize to performance in the
high-load trials. This possibility makes the result hard to interpret, given that the
probabilistic cuing effect observed in the high-load condition could be due to the
information learnt during the no-load condition. In Experiments 2 and 3, there were two
different rich quadrants for each participant, one for each WM load condition. However,
this might not suffice to eliminate carry-over effects between both WM load conditions.
If the two rich quadrants happened to be adjacent to each other, for instance, then search
times in the rich quadrant of the high-load condition could easily benefit from any
learning about the rich quadrant in the no-load condition. Ideally, this hypothesis should
be tested with a procedure that prevents any carry-over effect between both WM load
conditions.
As a secondary aim, the present series of experiments explored whether WM
reduces the expression of probabilistic cuing or affects learning itself. As noted above,
research conducted with contextual cuing tasks suggests that working memory load
might not affect learning itself, but the processes involved in translating this learning to
an observable response (Goujon et al., 2015; Pollmann, 2019). This conclusion is based
on evidence that although WM load sometimes seems to abolish contextual cuing,
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 8
significant levels of cuing are nevertheless found as soon as WM load is removed from
the task. An ideal experiment pursuing this idea in the probabilistic cuing paradigm
should complement the original design used by Won and Jiang (2015) with a final
testing stage free of working memory load that could potentially reveal evidence of
latent learning. If working memory load affects only the expression of learning, robust
evidence of probabilistic cuing should be observed in a testing stage without WM load,
even if the effect seems to be absent during the training under WM load. That is,
participants who conduct the task under high WM load may be learning the bias but not
expressing it until the WM load is removed. On the contrary, if WM load affects
learning itself, then removing the secondary WM task should not reveal any evidence of
probabilistic cuing. Given that Won and Jiang (2015) did not include a testing stage,
this is the first study to explore this question.
In sum, our aim in the present series of experiments was to explore whether
probabilistic cuing is unaffected by WM load once the above-mentioned problems are
addressed. Additionally, we explored for the first time if WM load affects learning
and/or performance in a probabilistic cuing task. We carried out three experiments in
which participants had to perform a probabilistic cuing task, but with some important
changes with respect to Won and Jiang‚Äôs (2015) work. First, we obtained data from a
sample of participants that allows us to obtain stronger evidence for the influence of
WM on probabilistic cuing. Second, to avoid carry-over effects between the rich
quadrant in the high-load condition and the no-load condition, working memory load
was manipulated between-subjects. Third, to ensure that both groups were differently
loaded and following Annac et al. (2013), half of the participants (high-load group)
carried out the visual search task between the encoding and the probe display of a WM
task, whereas the other half of the participants (no-load group) carried out the visual
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 9
search task after the probe display of the WM task (i.e., the search task was executed
without a concurrent WM load). Fourth, in order to explore whether WM load affects
learning or the expression of learning we added, at the end of the learning stage, a
testing stage without the concurrent WM task, where the target was evenly distributed
across quadrants.
As in Won and Jiang (2015), the nature of the secondary WM task was visual in
Experiment 1, spatial in Experiment 2, and spatiotemporal in Experiment 3 to explore
whether different modalities could differentially affect probabilistic cuing. Even though
Won and Jiang did not find any interference of WM load on probabilistic cuing, our
main prediction was that both the learning and the expression of probabilistic cuing
would be impaired by the secondary task in all the experiments. We speculated that
during the probabilistic cuing task participants need to rehearse information about the
location of the targets. The encoding display of the WM task also needs to be rehearsed,
therefore, these two tasks will compete for WM resources and will interfere with each
other. Note that this does not necessarily mean that all three tasks would have the same
impact on probabilistic cuing. Given that the probabilistic cuing effect is driven by
regularities in the spatial distribution of targets, we hypothesized that WM tasks that
require participants to store and retrieve spatial information would have a greater impact
on probabilistic cuing than other WM tasks. This led us to think that the secondary tasks
of Experiments 2 and 3 would interfere more strongly with probabilistic cuing.

Stimuli
Each trial began with a WM encoding display, consisting of four squares
presented in four different colors randomly chosen amongst red, green, blue, yellow,
magenta and cyan. The four squares were arranged in the same spatial configuration as
the arrow keys of a standard keyboard. Each side of the square was 3 cm and the
separation between squares was 1 cm. In the probe display, a randomly chosen square

Visual search displays consisted of 15 L-shaped distractors (which could be

At the beginning of the experiment, participants were informed that they would
have to carry out two tasks concurrently. When a visual search display was presented,

Planned Analyses
Search Times. Data from participants not able to conclude the task due to

To analyze participants‚Äô performance during the learning stage, we used a
nonlinear mixed-effects approach (Brooks et al., 2010; Chun & Jiang, 2003).
Specifically, we fitted a power function to data from each condition from each
participant (RT = ix ) where i denotes the intercept of the function, x denotes the search
block and s denotes the slope of the function. That is, s represents the rate at which
reaction times decline progressively over blocks in each condition. If probabilistic cuing
benefits visual search, this decline should be steeper for the rich than for the sparse
condition. Consequently, the difference between the parameter s in conditions rich and
sparse can be taken as a measure of probabilistic cuing. For each subject we calculated
the difference between the s parameter for the sparse condition minus the s parameter
for the rich condition during the learning stage. We tested whether the basic
probabilistic cuing effect was found in each group comparing this difference score
against zero with a one-sample t-test and a Bayesian t-test for repeated measures,
separately for both groups. Then, we compared these difference scores in both groups
with a frequentist and Bayesian t-test for independent samples. Note that this is the

Discussion
The main aim of this study was to test whether the probabilistic cuing effect is
independent of WM resources. Importantly, in an effort to contribute to the transparency
of science and the replicability of psychological research, this study was conducted as a
Registered Report. This implies that the rationale, hypotheses, methods, and analyses
Probabilistic Cuing and Working Memory 37
plan of the three experiments were put forward before data collection began. The
experimental protocol (i.e., the Introduction and Methods of the present article) was
approved by the journal editor and reviewers before any results were available. The
main advantage of this publication format is that the reader can rest assured that the
results reported in the present article are completely free from publication bias, selective
reporting, HARKing, and questionable research practices (e.g., Nosek et al., 2018).
Goal-driven selective attention and WM are assumed to be intimately related
(Awh et al., 2006; Gazzaley & Nobre, 2012). Thus, the question of whether
probabilistic cuing is independent from working memory resources is crucial for any
attempt to find out if the effect relies on goal-driven or habitual processes. In the current
experiments, one group of participants performed a visual search task during the

WM diminished (but did not abolish) the probabilistic cuing bias during the learning

probabilistic cuing is indeed highly resilient to WM load. At the same time, in

automatic attentional habit (Jiang & Sisk, 2018; Jiang, 2019).
In the preregistered protocol, we anticipated that any effect of WM load would
probably be smaller in the first experiment. Our results provide partial support for this
hypothesis, as we found weak signs of dual-task interference in Experiments 2 and 3,
but clearly not in Experiment 1. The difficulty of the task may have been responsible for
this result since in Experiment 2 we found the lowest accuracy in the WM task
compared to the other two experiments, along with most reliable impact of WM load on
the expression of probabilistic cuing.
It is also possible that only a particular component of WM is relevant for the
successful development and expression of probabilistic cuing and that not all the WM
tasks used in this series of experiments are equally effective at taxing that component.
The secondary task used in Experiment 2 might have been particularly effective at
draining any cognitive resource required for the processing of spatial representations.
Note, in any case, that the conclusion that only certain types of secondary tasks interfere
with probabilistic cuing is based on a cross-experimental comparison. Future research
should compare the impact of different types of WM load within a single experiment.

In the three experiments, the high-load group showed systematically lower
performance in the WM task compared to the no-load group. That is, the visual search
task interfered with the WM task when performed during the retention interval. This
result was expected, since it is a well-established result that performing a secondary task
during the retention interval impairs WM performance (Smyth, 1996; Awh et al., 1998;
Makovski et al., 2006; Mercer, 2018).
Unlike Won and Jiang (2015), our experiments included a testing stage without
any secondary task at the end of the experiment. This allowed us to test the (yet
unexplored) possibility that the attentional bias that underlies the probabilistic cuing
effect could be latently learned under WM load, even if its expression is completely or
partially compromised by the secondary task. Our results did not show a clear effect of
group (that is, WM load) on reaction times during the test stage, suggesting that by the
time they began that stage, both groups had already reached the same learning of the
biased distribution of targets. The safest conclusion that can be drawn from our results
is that WM load does not prevent the acquisition of probabilistic cuing and, if anything,
it might hinder the expression of the learned bias. This result dovetails with previous
research on contextual cuing of visual search, a related visual statistical learning effect
(Goujon et al., 2015; Pollmann, 2019; but see Vicente-Conesa et al., 2022).

Regardless of the role played by WM resources, another argument for claiming
that probabilistic cuing is an automatic attentional habit is that participants seem to
show little or no awareness of the experimental manipulation that drives their
performance in the task (Jiang, 2018; Jiang et al., 2015; Jiang & Sisk, 2019). Although
our experiments were not primarily aimed at addressing this question, they did include a
brief awareness test at the end of the task for exploratory purposes. We did not find
convincing evidence of awareness about the biased distribution of the target and the
correlation between the performance of participants during the test stage and their
estimates of the frequency for the rich quadrant was nonsignificant either. The poor
evidence of awareness in our experiments was not completely unexpected, though, since
participants performed the awareness test right after the testing stage, once the bias in
the spatial distribution of targets had already disappeared. Ideally, awareness about the
experimental manipulation should be evaluated as close as possible to the moment
where the manipulation is taking place (Shanks & St. John, 1994). In a previous
experiment using the same paradigm we showed that participants performed better in
the awareness test if it was conducted right after the training stage as compared to a
condition in which, as in the present experiments, it was conducted after the test stage
(Gim√©nez-Fern√°ndez et al., 2020; Vadillo et al., 2020). The absence of correlation
between probabilistic cuing and awareness was not unexpected either, because these
dependent measures are notoriously unreliable (Vadillo et al., 2020, 2021).
