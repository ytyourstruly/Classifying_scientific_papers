Abstract
Social evaluations are a pervasive feature of human thinking. Studies on selective trust, selective social
learning, and helping ‚Äì that is, in cooperative contexts ‚Äì have shown that children become competent
social decision-makers in their preschool years. Choosing partners based on relevant attributes is
equally important for success in competitive situations, however. To test predictions from a dual-
process account of children‚Äôs development of reasoning abilities, we devised two online experiments
for 4- to 7-year-old children. In Experiment 1, children were introduced to a weak and to a strong
model and had the opportunity to apply this information strategically in competitive and cooperative
domain-relevant games (requiring strength) and in a domain-irrelevant game (requiring object
labelling knowledge). Children recruited the optimal partner in both contexts from around 5 years of
age and generalized partner expertise to novel domains. Experiment 2 was designed to shed light on
the reasoning process underlying partner recruitment choices. Children were introduced to a weak
but smart and a strong but ignorant model and had the opportunity to apply this information in games
of different domain-context combinations. Contrary to our expectations, even the oldest children
performed poorly. Our findings are at odds with previous research showing that 4-year-olds rationally
recruited optimal partners for cooperative tasks. Children may have struggled with the unfamiliar first-
person perspective, and the difficulty of the task when they had to consider multiple agent
characteristics at once.
KEY WORDS
Social evaluation ‚Äì Partner choice ‚Äì Strategy ‚Äì Cooperation ‚Äì Competition
Children gain valuable knowledge from observing and communicating with the people in their
surroundings (Csibra & Gergely, 2009; Harris, 2012; Harris et al., 2018). From around three to four
years of age, children begin to be selective about whom they attend to, imitate, and trust when making
use of socially-disseminated knowledge (for reviews on selective trust, see Mills, 2013). Selective trust
has often been studied by means of the ‚Äútwo-informant‚Äù paradigm, developed by Koenig et al. (2004).
From these studies, we know that preschoolers tune into epistemic cues (e.g., Birch et al., 2008; Jaswal
& Neely, 2006; Koenig et al., 2004; Koenig & Harris, 2005; Pasquini et al., 2007) as well as social cues
(Bascandziev & Harris, 2014; Bernard et al., 2016; Corriveau & Harris, 2009; Elashi & Mills, 2014; Jaswal
& Kondrad, 2016; MacDonald et al., 2013). Recently, the focus has shifted from identifying cues that
mediate selectivity, to theorizing about the inferential processes likely to underlie selectivity (Fusaro
et al., 2011; Hermes et al., 2015; Sobel & Kushnir, 2013). Three candidate processes have been put
forward (outlined in Fusaro et al., 2011): (1) behaviour-matching, (2) global impression formation and
(3) trait-reasoning.
In behaviour-matching, a child assumes that a model shows behavioural consistency over
time. Thus, someone who has demonstrated accuracy in object-labelling in the past will provide
accurate labels in the future. An inference based on behaviour-matching is relatively narrow. In global
impression formation, in contrast, a child conceives a model as positive/negative in undifferentiated
ways (also referred to as ‚Äúhalo/pitchfork‚Äù effects), leading to wider competence predictions than
warranted. For example, a child might infer that someone who is strong is also an accurate object-
labeller. Trait-reasoning, finally, requires a more complex inference process than behaviour-matching
and global impression formation. A child who engages in trait reasoning succeeds in a two-component
process (Liu et al., 2007). The first component involves mapping observed behaviour to a trait. The
second component involves mapping the assigned trait to future behaviour. There is evidence to
support the idea that children engage in global impression formation in certain situations and trait-
reasoning in others (Bascandziev & Harris, 2014; Fusaro et al., 2011). For example, Bascandziev and
Harris (2014) found that preschoolers preferentially learned from people who rate highly in terms of
attractiveness, although attractiveness has no obvious relation as to whether or not a person is
knowledgeable. There is also, however, evidence that young children understand that people differ in
their abilities in different domains of expertise (e.g., Lane & Harris, 2015; Lutz & Keil, 2002; Sobel &
Kushnir, 2013). For example, given the choice between adults and peers, children consult peers for
toy-related information and adults on the topic of food (VanderBorght & Jaswal, 2009).
Hermes and colleagues recently suggested a dual-process account (Hermes et al., 2018) to
reconcile findings showing that children‚Äôs social selectivity sometimes relies on simple and sometimes
on more complex inferences. They theorize that early in development, children‚Äôs trust decisions are
based on simple decision heuristics (‚ÄòType-I‚Äô processes, Kahneman, 2003), such as a general trust
heuristic and a trust-the-better heuristic. As children grow older, they acquire a sounder concept of
knowledge, a better understanding of how different domains relate to one another, and more general
cognitive resources, including better executive functions and Theory of Mind abilities. In cases where
a conclusive answer is possible, such as in in degree of competence contrasts, halo-effects are
observed. In cases where such generalizations are insufficient, such as in domain of competence
contrasts, more sophisticated reasoning processes are required (Type-II processes). A number of
findings are compatible with the dual process account (for a review, see Hermes et al., 2018). For
example, Hermes et al. (2016) found that the same children who chose the more competent of two
models for domain-unrelated tasks showed context-specific partner choice when confronted with two
models who had different domain expertise. Furthermore, older children and children with better
executive function abilities have been shown to be able to resist a general trust bias and disregard
advice from formerly unreliable models (Heyman et al., 2013; Jaswal et al., 2014; Vanderbilt et al.,
2011).
One way to test predictions from the dual-process account is to examine whether the Type I
and Type II strategies used in the cooperative context are also applied in other contexts that require
similar problem-solving strategies. Choosing partners based on relevant attributes is equally
important for success in competitive situations. If children operate with rational conceptual capacities,
they should be able to apply these capacities in flexible, domain-general ways, allowing them to
choose a given partner in one context but reject the same partner in another. We designed two
experiments to investigate how 4- to 7-year-old children recruit co-action partners in competitive and
cooperative contexts when they are provided with different sets of information about potential
partners. Competitive tasks pose more complex perspective-taking demands compared to
cooperative tasks (Perner et al., 2005; Priewasser et al., 2013) because, in a cooperative context, the
perspective of the child and the recruited team member align, whereas in a competitive context the
child has to keep in mind that their goal and the goal of the recruited character are in conflict.
In Experiment 1, children met two models who differed in terms of physical strength. We
predicted that that younger children would make fewer correct choices in the competitive context,
while older children should be equally successful in both contexts. We also predicted that younger
children would be more likely to generalize about competence and would be more likely to do so in
the cooperative as compared to the competitive context. In Experiment 2, children met two models
who each had a strength and a weakness: one character was strong but ignorant, the other was weak
but knowledgeable. We monitored the partner recruitment choices of each participant in a series of
competitive and cooperative strength and knowledge games. In this design, only those participants
who engage in rational trait reasoning (Type-II process) should be successful: they should choose the
strong/ignorant partner for cooperative strength and competitive knowledge games and the
weak/knowledgeable partner for competitive strength and cooperative knowledge games. We
predicted that younger children should do less well than older ones, and struggle particularly in the
competitive context, while older children should be equally successful in both contexts.

Design and materials
In Experiment 1, children were given information about two models who differed from one another
in terms of physical strength: Mr. Blue was physically strong and Mr. Green was weak. Children then
played a series of competitive and cooperative strength games in which their avatar had to play with
or against one of the models. The optimal strategy was to choose the weak model as an opponent and
the strong model as a teammate. In addition to these strength games, each child played a knowledge
game. In this novel domain, they lacked information about the partners‚Äô expertise. If the children
engage in global impression formation, they should, for instance, recruit the physically strong model
details regarding experimental stages and familiarization are described in subsection 1.1. of the ESM.
All video stimuli were created using the Vyond animation software. An overview of the stimuli
can be found in the ESM (subsection 1.2). The experiments were run in Labvanced (Finger et al., 2017).
The child met with an experimenter via virtual conferencing software ‚Äì usually BigBlueButton (BBB)
and occasionally Zoom, when BBB failed. Three female experimenters collected the data. We used a
repeated-measure within-subject design for the strength games (the ‚Äúdegree-of-competence‚Äù test
trials) and counterbalanced the order in which we presented the context (cooperation vs
competition). The knowledge game (‚Äúgeneralization‚Äù test trial) was implemented as a between-
subject factor. Children who had received a competition strength game first, experienced a
competition knowledge game and vice versa. The stimuli for adults were identical to those used in the
child study, although bridging text was added so that the participants could navigate the experiment
alone.

Analyses
All statistical analyses were carried out in R (R Core Team, 2021, version 4.1.1). We fitted a total of six
models. Three Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM, Baayen, 2008) were used to analyse the
degree-of-competence test trials. Two Generalized Linear Models (GLM, Baayen, 2008) and one
GLMM were used to analyse the generalization test trials. Four of these analyses (Models 1 through
preregistered analyses (Models 1B and 3B) were run to check for a potential effect of experimenter
ID (given that three experimenters collected the child data). In the subsections that follow, we
concisely describe the structure of each model but for detailed information concerning model
structure, sample size, variable standardization, model diagnostics (stability, collinearity), and to view
the output tables, see subsection 1.3 of the ESM. In the ESM, we also elaborate on comprehension
checks, participants‚Äô answers to follow-up questions, and exploratory analyses with these qualitative
data.
We constructed a GLMM, Model 1, to test whether context, age, or their interaction had an
effect on children‚Äôs partner recruitment choices in the degree-of-competence test trials. The binary
response variable was optimality of partner recruitment choice. Model 1 additionally comprised the
fixed effects of gender, condition presentation order and trial number. Participant ID was included as
a random effect and the random slopes of context and trial number were included in the model. In
addition, we fitted Model 1B to assess and control for a potential effect of experimenter ID, which
was fitted as a random effect. Models 1 and 1B thus differed only in terms of their random effects
components (and therefore also with regard to their random slope structures, see ESM). To establish
whether context had an effect on adult partner recruitment choices, we fitted Model 2. The binary
response variable of this GLMM was optimality of partner choice. Model 2 comprised a main effect of
context in addition to the fixed effects of gender, order and trial number. Participant ID was included
as a random effect and the random slopes of context and trial number were included in the model.
We constructed a GLM, Model 3, to test whether either an interaction between context and
age, or a main effect of context or age, had an effect on children‚Äôs partner choice in the generalization
test trial. The binary response variable was whether recruitment choice constituted a generalization;
to generalize in the competition knowledge game was to choose the physically weak partner and to
generalize in the cooperation knowledge game was to choose the strong partner. Model 3 comprised
a two-way interaction between context and age, in addition to their main effects and the fixed effects
of gender and the intercept-BLUPs (Best Linear Unbiased Predictors) generated in Model 1. These
BLUPs were a proxy measure for how successfully each child had performed in the degree-of-
competence test trials. To control for a potential effect of experimenter ID, we constructed another
GLMM, Model 3B. Models 3 and 3B differed in terms of their random effects components (and
therefore also with regard to their random slope structures, see ESM) and the BLUPs were taken from
Model 1B rather than Model 1. We fitted a GLM, Model 4, to test whether context had an effect on
adult generalization behaviour. Model 4 comprised the main effect of context, in addition to the fixed
effects of gender and the BLUPs generated in Model 2.

Experiment 2 was designed to determine if participants use sophisticated trait reasoning
(Type-II process) or a simpler heuristic such as global impression formation (Type-I process) to choose
between co-action partners in competitive and cooperative games. Children performed at chance
level, indicating that their partner choice wasn‚Äôt based on rational trait reasoning. Adults overall
recruited rationally in both contexts, with almost ceiling performance in the cooperative context and
somewhat reduced rational choice in the competitive context. Considering that none of the adults
expressed confusion regarding partner competence and requirements of optimal choice in the two
co-action contexts, we think the effect of context isn‚Äôt indicative of the competitive scenario
presenting a harder task than the cooperative scenario, for adults. More likely, a few students weren‚Äôt
fully concentrated during some of their partner selections, which led to chance performance after just
one wrong choice. To gain clarity, though, differences of adults‚Äô social selectivity in cooperative and
competitive tasks should be followed-up on in future studies.
In light of the fact that the older children in Experiment 1 performed well, the poor
performance of the older children who took part in Experiment 2 raises questions. Ample studies have
demonstrated that from four-years of age, children are capable of flexibly switching between models
that show expertise in different domains in the cooperative context (e.g., Hermes et al., 2016) and in
a very recent study, 5-6-year-olds avoided competent players in domain-relevant competitive tasks
(Grueneisen et al., 2023). Yet in the present experiment, not even the oldest children (seven-year-
olds) performed well in the cooperative context. One possible explanation is that children struggled
with the amount of information they had to consider simultaneously during the test trials. Despite
being able to recall single pieces of information (hence their good performance during comprehension
and advice questions, see ESM), they may have been overwhelmed by the amount of information they
had to consider during the test trials: When recruiting their co-action partner, they had to consider
two competence attributes per partner, one even being of a negative quality, i.e., incompetence, and
use these bits of information appropriately in light of the current context. Previous studies that
presented information of two characteristics per model used only one competence attribute in
combination with one social identity characteristic (familiarity/accent) when asking children to
endorse a model (Corriveau et al., 2013; Corriveau & Harris, 2009). An interesting line for future
research will be to explore the developmental trajectory of integrating several model characteristics
to make optimal decisions in selective trust scenarios.
General Discussion
We found that children from around five years of age succeeded in switching between choosing and
avoiding the same partner depending on task context, thus showing a new level of flexibility in
children‚Äôs social decision-making competence. At the same time, the majority of these children saw
fit to recruit co-action partners in a novel task based on the partners‚Äô competence in an unrelated
domain ‚Äì a pattern indicative of global impression formation rather than sophisticated trait reasoning.
In the relevant contrasts to differentiate between these two possibilities, children performed at
chance irrespective of age and context. These results stand in contrast to our predictions and are at
odds with previous research that found that 4-year-olds rationally recruit optimal partners for
cooperative tasks.
The children's unexpectedly poor performance may be explained by the relatively unfamiliar
first-person perspective, which may have masked children's abilities to strategically recruit between
the two models, as well as by the complexity of our task (see previous discussion). In addition, we
were operating with a minimal incentive structure ‚Äì children‚Äôs only rewards were passively viewing
their virtual character win, and an animated prize ceremony where they were awarded virtual
trophies, at the end of the experiment. Thus, children had nothing to miss out on, and nothing really
to gain by making their choices. During in person testing, it is common to use incentive systems such
as collecting marbles or stickers to motivate children to choose wisely (e.g., Hermes et al., 2019). In
the absence of such incentives, some children may simply have chosen at random, despite having
understood all aspects of the task.
A final and more general point worth mentioning, is that statistically controlling for the effect
of experimenter ID affected the results of both experiments. For practical reasons, the use of multiple
experimenters is common practice in the field of developmental psychology. But despite awareness
that experimenters can bias results (Gallo & Dale, 1968), it is not common to see data divvied up by
experimenter ID in publications. Yet, information about number of experimenter identities and how
this detail has been controlled for in statistical analyses would help readers to assess the robustness
of findings. We decided to include experimenter ID in the random effects structure of each GLMM in
light of a recent publication that indicated it is possible to include a factor with just three levels as a
random effect (Gomes, 2022). We were surprised to find that even in ‚Äòremote‚Äô research where one
might think the experimenter could have little influence, there may still be the danger of unaccounted
for experimenter bias. Importantly though, with the current study we can‚Äôt be sure to which degree
and in which way experimenter ID affected results, or whether the difference in model results merely
constitutes a chance finding. In light of improved statistical methods, this may be a question to be
explored more systematically with appropriate a priori considerations in future studies.
In summary, we found that from around age 5, children‚Äôs social decision-making skills are
flexible enough to successfully navigate easy cooperative and competitive tasks. Our study also
showed that there are limits to children's rational selective partner choice abilities; children seem to
struggle when they have to consider multiple agent characteristics at once. We suggest future studies
investigate the effect of remote as opposed to in person testing, the effect increasing incentives has
on optimal decision-making, and the developmental trajectory of coping with an increasing amount
of information when choosing partners based on social evaluation judgements.
