Abstract
In this manuscript, we provide a discourse of issues and resolutions that we should be conscious
of when doing research in the unconscious using backward masking of faces. First, we revisit
subjects that are contributing for understanding the unconscious as a concept. These involve
historical episodes and early episodes of controversial experimentation. Subsequently, we
revisit and discuss topical concepts, such as the metrics and the statistical analyses applied for
assessing perception during backward masking. We proceed to discussing novel and
developing issues relating to masked visual processing and contemporary psychophysics. We
empirically illustrate how these issues bias experimental research. We present and empirically
illustrate resolutions for these issues, such as the application of signal detection theory metrics,
Bayesian analysis and advances in the psychophysics of masked presentations using faces. We
assess whether and the extent to which we are truly conscious of established and known, and
novel and developing issues and resolutions. We make use of backward masking as a rally
point to emphasize the importance of conscious scholarly and methodological awareness for
undertaking and advancing research in the unconscious.

The Early History and Controversies
The concept of the unconscious was clinically first discoursed by â€“ the now considered
notoriously cliche and largely extraneous to contemporary psychology â€“ Sigmund Freud. It
was introduced as part of psychoanalysis and involved extensively criticised concepts, such as
a topographical arrangement of the human psyche, a theoretical-observational distinction of
evolutionary-inherited human mechanisms for repression, and the proposition that the human
condition involves an involuntary apoptotic function that is expressed via the workings of a
death instinct (for a comprehensive review, see Webster, 1995).
These concepts were characterized as unscientific during the 1960â€™s and 1970â€™s by
among others (see Burnham, 1967) Karl Popper â€“ in more publications that are commonly
associated with this thesis (Popper, 1963) â€“ for being irrefutable, untestable and unfalsifiable
(Popper, 1950; 1952; 1959; 1960). Psychoanalytic authors added to the ordeal by claiming that
these criticisms reflected personal psychoanalytically explainable issues relating to the
criticizing authors (GrÃ¼nbaum, 1979). This response was largely considered a confirmation of
the original criticisms (see Frosh, 1989; Erdelyi, 1992).
At the same time in history, infamy, scepticism and controversy were stirred from an
additional front. In 1957, James Vicary claimed that he could boost sales of specific products
in cinema theatres by presenting very brief unconscious messages (03 seconds; 18 ms) at
random intervals during movies, such as â€œDrink Coca-Colaâ€ and â€œEat Pop-Cornâ€. He claimed
that he was able to boost Coca-Cola and pop-corn sales in cinema theatres by 18.1% and 57.5%
respectively using unconscious messages (Pratkanis, 1992; see also Nelson, 2008). His claims
caused public infatuation and stirred scientific disbelief (see particularly Rogers, 1992).
Vicary, under the pressure of the entire psychological community to publish his
findings, eventually admitted in 1962 that his claims were an elaborate publicity hoax (see
Nelson, 2008). This simply added insult to injury as regards the reliability of claims that
supported unconscious processing. Since then, a multitude of empirical publications attempted
to re-instate the validity of research into the unconscious (Karremans, Stroebe & Claus, 2006;
van den Bussche, Van den Noortgate & Reynvoet, 2009; Brooks et al., 2012; Meneguzzo,
Tsakiris, Schioth, Stein & Brooks, 2014; van der Ploeg, Brosschot, Versluis & Verkuil, 2017)
but a great deal of damage was already done (see Stafford, 2014). Unconsciousness became
something of a taboo subject in experimental psychology.
Contemporary arguments for unconscious processing are still recovering â€“ typically via
silent distancing (see Hassin, Uleman & Bargh, 2004) â€“ from these early episodes of
controversy (see also Uleman et al., 2005). Nevertheless, these early episodes are erroneously
considered extraneous for understanding the argumentative polarity of attitudes that
characterise contemporary research (Kihlstrom & Barnhardt & Tataryn, 1992;. Kihlstrom,
Mulvaney, Tobias & Tobis, 2000; Tallis, 2002; Winkielman & Berridge, 2004; Kihlstrom,
2008; Ffytche, 2011; Talvitie, 2018; Shanks, Malejka & Vadillo, 2021). Instead, our opinion
is that they describe very well the origins of why cotemporary research is so divided between
believers and disbelievers in unconscious processing, and why contemporary researchers were
required to go to great lengths to restore some quantum of faith in conducting research on the
unconscious (see Merikle & Cheesman, 1987; Bar & Biederman, 1998; Pessoa & Adolphs,
2010; Elgendi, Kumar, Barbic, Howard, Abbott & Cichocki, 2018).

Survival and Refutations
History eventually showed that a controversial lineage was not reason enough to
abandon a promising empirical research area (Shevrin, & Dickman, 1980; Kihlstrom,
Barnhardt & Tataryn, 1992; Western, 1999; Hassin, Uleman & Bargh, 2004; Scherer, 2005).
The general public was and is very interested in the subject (Elgendi, Kumar, Barbic, Howard,
Abbott & Cichocki, 2018; Song, Wei & Ke, 2019; Osman, 2021), research and publications on
unconsciousness continued (Haggard, Kaelen, Saroglou, Klein, & Rowatt, 2019; Soto, Sheikh
& Rosenthal, 2019; Albarrak, Metatla & Roudaut, 2020; Tsikandilakis et al., 2021;
Tsikandilakis et al., 2022a) â€“ as did an equal sum of rebuffs for unconscious findings (Reber,
1989; Scherer, 2004; Stafford, 2014; Stein, 2019) â€“ and the promise that the outcomes of
experimental research in this area can shed light to fundamental questions concerning human
cognition held against the siege of incoming repudiations (Ekstrom, 2004; Norman, 2010;
Kihlstrom, 2018; Tsikandilakis, Bali, Madan, Derfuss, Chapman & Groeger, 2021).
Nevertheless, many psychologists believe that validity was and is missing from
experimental research into the unconscious (Gawronski, LeBel & Peters, 2007; Wang & Minor,
2008; Bargh, 2011; Greenwald & Banaji, 2017; Wang et al., 2017). Outcomes for unconscious
processing have been criticized for several reasons as regards their validity (Breitmeyer &
Ogmen, 2000; Gawronski, LeBel & Peters, 2007; Strick et al., 2011). These involve the metrics
(Macmillan & Creelman, 2004) and analyses (Kouider & Faivre, 2017) applied for assessing
unconsciousness, the statistical and mathematical definition for presenting stimuli that are
unconscious (Dienes, 2014; 2015; 2016), and previously unaddressed issues, such as visual
processing and psychophysics (see for example Breitmeyer, 2015), related specifically to
backward masking of faces (see particularly Brooks et al., 2012; Meneguzzo, Tsakiris, Schioth,
Stein & Brooks, 2014; van der Ploeg, Brosschot, Versluis & Verkuil, 2017).
Backward Masking using Faces
Backward masking is a widely used method for testing unconscious processing. It is an
area in which our group has multiple publications and expertise (see for example Tsikandilakis
& Chapman, 2018; Tsikandilakis, Chapman & Peirce, 2018; Tsikandilakis, Bali, Derrfuss &
Chapman, 2019; Tsikandilakis, Bali, Derrfuss & Chapman, 2020a; Tsikandilakis et al., 2021;
see also Tsikandilakis et al., 2022a) and, therefore, will be the focus for examining issues and
resolutions for unconscious research in the current manuscript. Backward masking refers to the
presentation of a brief target stimulus, such as, typically. but not exclusively. emotional faces
(see Axelrod, Bar, M., & Rees, 2015), for 6 to 50 ms that is subsequently followed by a noise
image. The aim of the noise image is to disallow conscious perception of the target stimulus.

responses (known as skin conductance responses or SCR) and heart rate responses (see
Cacioppo, Tassinary & Berntson, 2007; pp. 72-119), and participant self-reports, typically, but
not exclusively, for valence and arousal (see Macknik & Martinez-Conde, 2007) are recorded.

Issues and Resolutions
Two important issues in backward masking using faces are using hit rates as a metric
for perception and significance testing for assessing unconsciousness (Enns & Di Lollo 2000;
Kim & Blake, 2005). Hit rates are the percentage of correct answers in response to visually
suppressed stimuli. The issue with hit rates is that they are affected by response bias. For
example, some participants might respond conservatively, that is, they withhold a response for
stimuli awareness unless they are absolutely certain. Others might respond liberally, that is,
they might respond seeing a stimulus even if they are quite uncertain. Significance testing is a
statistical method that using, in this case, a method called a one-sample t-test, can allow us to
conduct a comparison between the participantsâ€™ detection performance and chance (e.g., 50%).
This method can provide evidence, given a margin of error typically set at 5%, for whether we
can fail to reject or whether we can reject the null hypothesis that the participantsâ€™ detection
performance was not significantly different to chance. This is subsequently erroneously
interpreted to signify that the participants were unconscious or conscious of the presented
stimuli respectively (Stanislaw & Todorov, 1999; Erdelyi, 2004; see also Dienes, 2014; 2015;
2016).
As a theoretical illustration, let us assume that we are presenting, using backward
masking, fearful, sad and neutral faces for 16.67 ms. After every trial we ask â€œDid you see a
face? (Y/N)â€. When using hit-rates we cannot control for the possibility that the participants
can reply using either conservative response strategies and criteria, such as replying that they
saw a face only when certain beyond a shadow of a doubt that a face was presented, or liberal
response strategies and criteria, such as replying that they saw a face even when quite uncertain
that a face was presented. Many researchers might not feel moved by these possibilities and
proceed to test the resulting data. They could use a one-sample t-test and, for the purposes of
this hypothetical scenario, report that participantsâ€™ detection performance was not significantly
different to chance.
That would mean that using a biased metric (hit-rates), we have failed to reject the null
hypothesis that the participantsâ€™ detection performance was not significantly different to
chance. We could claim that we have evidence for the presentation of unconscious faces (see
Brooks et al., 2012; Meneguzzo, Tsakiris, Schioth, Stein & Brooks, 2014; van der Ploeg,
Brosschot, Versluis & Verkuil, 2017). In reality, we employed a biased metric for perception
which we used to apply a statistical analysis. In this statistical analysis, we misinterpreted
failing to reject the null hypothesis, that there are no significant differences between the
participantsâ€™ performance and chance, with evidence for the null hypothesis, that the
participantsâ€™ performance was proximate to or at-chance (Dienes, 2015).

five most recent non-clinical meta-analyses in this area show explicitly that this was required.

We failed to achieve unbiased evidence for unconscious presentation for fearful faces.
The answer as to why could be deferred to evolutionary psychology (Ã–hman & Mineka, 2001;
Schultz & Helmstetter, 2010; Sevenster, Beckers, & Kindt, 2014; Weike, Schupp & Hamm,
2007) supported incidentally in this case by the findings in our own previous work
(Tsikandilakis, Bali, Derrfuss & Chapman, 2020b; 2020c; Tsikandilakis et al., 2021;
Tsikandilakis et al., 2021). The perception of fearful faces is more acute because it arguably
confers increased evolutionary survival value. Fearful expressions signify via an interpersonal
proxy the unseen presence of an imminent threat in our immediate environment (Ã–hman,
2009).
Therefore, presented for the same brief duration (i.e., 16.67 ms), fearful faces were
perceived more accurately than less evolutionary important stimulus types (i.e., sad and neutral
faces; see Hedger, Adams & Garner, 2015). This is a viable argument. Our team has provided
empirical evidence that could be interpreted to support this argument in multiple publications
(Tsikandilakis, Bali, Derrfuss & Chapman, 2020a; 2020b; Tsikandilakis et al., 2021;
Tsikandilakis et al., 2021), other research groups have provided empirical evidence that could
be interpreted to support this interpretation in multiple publications (Etkin, Klemenhagen,
Dudman, Rogan, Hen, Kandel & Hirsch 2004; Stein, Peelen, Funk & Seidl, 2010; Wieser &
Keil, 2014; Hedger, Adams & Garner, 2015; Wang et al., 2017; Stein, Utz, & Van Opstal,
2020). Nevertheless, this thesis cannot be directly proven empirically (Workman & Reader,
2021). It can only be inferred as a theoretical interpretation from supporting empirical findings
(for a comprehensive review concerning the limitations of evolutionary interpretations, see
Hagen, 2005).
There is something that can be illustrated directly (Gray, Adams, Hedger, Newton &
Garner, 2013). The careful reader will have noticed that depending on the occasion, throughout
our manuscript, we presented the standard deviation for luminosity either as a fixed value or
an overall aggregate. We did so because in the case of pattern masks the same image was
repeated and, therefore, the same standard deviation applied for lumens values. In the case of
masked faces, different faces of the same and different emotional type involved differences in
the distribution of luminosity (see Sarantakos, 1998). That occurred because the distribution of
luminosity is a high-level visual component that varies for each face and contributes to the
structure of the presented face and contributes to the structure of emotional facial
characteristics (see De Gardelle & Kouider, 2010; Izmailov, Chernorizov & Polyansky, 2022;
see Figures 5 & 6).
We cannot change that the standard deviation of facial images will differ and will be
processed differently by the optical nerve, the optical thalamus and the occipital cortex (see
Sarantakos, 1998; see also De Gardelle & Kouider, 2010). If we do change them, the result will
contributes to the structure of the presented face that confers emotional information (Wyart &
Tallon-Baudry, 2009; Spillmann & Werner, 2012; Izmailov, Chernorizov & Polyansky, 2022).
That means that even if we achieve â€œEquivalence of Contrastâ€ of the mean luminescence
between faces and masks, we cannot achieve â€œEquivalence of Contrastâ€ of the distribution of
luminescence between faces and masks without changing faces to non-facial patterns.
We could not bring fearful faces to Bayesian evidence for the null because they have
properties related to the distribution and structure of facial characteristics that convey emotion
that make them potentially more perceptible than other stimulus types under conditions of
visual suppression (see Tsikandilakis & Chapman, 2018). We do not wish to enter an
Aristotelian â€“ or worse, yet Kafkian â€“ loop concerning whether the higher-level diversity
characteristics of fearful faces are due to their evolutionary significance or whether
evolutionary significance is a conceptual interpretation of high-level visual characteristics (see
Murphy & Zajonc, 1993).
Fearful faces â€“ as do other kinds of emotional faces (Kihlstrom, Mulvaney, Tobias &
Tobis, 2000) â€“ have distinct high-level characteristics that make them more perceptible than
other emotional types, such as sad and neutral faces (Liddell et al., 2005). Inconveniently, there
is very little we can do about this issue except employ our previously applied method for
individual unconsciousness (see Tsikandilakis & Chapman, 2018; Tsikandilakis, Chapman &
Peirce, 2018; Tsikandilakis, Bali, Derrfuss & Chapman, 2019; Tsikandilakis, Bali, Derrfuss &

Summary of Conclusions
In this manuscript, we revisited part of the history of the concept of the unconscious,
the issues and resolutions of contemporary research using practical illustrations and offered
ready-to-use open-source resources to address issues that have been characterized as
problematic in backward masking using faces. We discussed the current need for this
reminding and discussed that applied awareness and further methodological developments are
of the essence. The overarching aim of the current manuscript was to make the reader conscious
of issues related to unconsciousness research, offer potential resolutions, and most critically,
to remind us that applying the methodological advances, and advancing the scientific
methodology in topical research are seminal.
