22 Abstract
23 A fundamental objective in Auditory Sciences is understanding how people learn to generalize
24 auditory category knowledge in new situations. How we generalize to novel scenarios speaks
25 to the nature of emergent category representations and generalization mechanisms in handling
26 perceptual variabilities and novelty. The dual learning system (DLS) models propose that
27 auditory category learning involves an explicit, hypothesis-testing learning system, which is
28 optimal for learning rule-based (RB) categories, and an implicit, procedural-based learning
29 system, which is optimal for learning categories requiring pre-decisional information
30 integration (II) across acoustic dimensions. Although DLS describes distinct mechanisms of
31 two types of category learning, it is yet clear the nature of acquired representations and how
32 we transfer them to new contexts. Here, we conducted three experiments to examine
33 differences between II and RB category representations by examining what acoustic and
34 perceptual variabilities affect learners‚Äô generalization success. Learners can generalize to
35 different sets of untrained sounds after only eight training blocks for both II and RB categories.
36 The category structures and novel contexts differentially modulated the generalization success.
37 The II learners significantly decreased generalization performances when categorizing new
38 items derived from an untrained perceptual area and in a context with more distributed samples.
39 In contrast, RB learners‚Äô generalizations are resistant to changes in perceptual regions but are
40 sensitive to changes in sound dispersity. Representational similarity modeling revealed that the
41 generalization in the more dispersed sampling context was accomplished differently by II and
42 RB learners. II learners increased representations of perceptual similarity and decision distance
43 to compensate for the decreased transfer of category representations, whereas the RB learners
44 used a more computational cost strategy by default, computing the decision-bound distance to
45 guide generalization decisions. These results suggest that distinct representations emerged after
46 learning the two types of category structures and using different strategies and flexible
47 mechanisms in resolving generalization challenges when facing novel perceptual variability in
48 new contexts. These findings provide new evidence for dissociated representations of auditory
49 categories and reveal novel generalization mechanisms in resolving different variabilities to
50 maintain perceptual constancy.
52 Keywords: auditory category learning, generalization, representation nature, dual learning
53 system, information integration, rule-based learning

55 Introduction
56 Category learning ability allows us to learn to group items based on shared features or common
57 regularities across individual examples. The grouping knowledge acquired after learning is
58 considered abstract category representations guiding us to categorize items in new scenarios
59 (Kruschke, 1996; Smith and Minda, 1998, 2002; Love et al., 2004; Wills et al., 2006).
60 Successful generalization of learned category knowledge requires coping with various
61 variabilities of within- and between-category exemplars. Failure to do so would affect critical
62 decisions in our lives and even survival (e.g., under-generalization: missing detecting early
63 signatures of serious diseases, or over-generalization: categorizing poison mushrooms as
64 eatable). Therefore, category learning requires forming mental representations to support an
65 appropriate level of generalization. However, we are not yet fully understand the nature of
66 these category representations and the differences in representational characteristics when
67 learning different category structures.
68 Category representations are formed in the process of interacting with category
69 members, where the structure and distributional patterns of the category members affect how
70 we learn and the nature of acquired category representations (Ashby and Maddox, 2005, 2011;
71 Chandrasekaran et al., 2014a, 2014b). Previous behavioral and neuroimaging studies suggest
72 that at least two distinct learning systems are involved in category learning across visual (e.g.,
73 Ashby and Maddox, 2005, 2011; Smith and Grossman, 2008) and auditory modalities
74 (Chandrasekaran et al., 2014a, 2014b; Feng et al., 2021b). An explicit, hypothesis-testing
75 learning system is considered to be optimal for learning rule-based (RB) categories, where
76 those categories can be categorized based on verbalized rules. In contrast, an implicit,
77 procedural-learning-based system is hypothesized to be optimal for learning category
78 structures requiring information integration (II) across multiple dimensions before making a
79 categorization decision (Ashby and Maddox, 2005; Ashby and Valentin, 2005).
80 Previous studies have demonstrated various factors that differentially affect the learning
81 of II and RB category structures. For example, feedback types (instant vs. delayed; with vs.
82 without feedback) affect learning II categories more than RB‚Äôs (Maddox et al., 2003; Maddox
83 and Ing, 2005); category-response mapping is more critical and specific for II category learning
84 than that of RB (Ashby et al., 2003; Maddox et al., 2004b, 2007, 2010). In contrast, RB
85 category learning is more affected by executive-function-related factors, e.g., concurrent
86 working memory task (Waldron and Ashby, 2001; Filoteo et al., 2010), stress (Ell et al., 2011),
87 feedback processing interference (Maddox et al., 2004a), number of categories (i.e., limitation
88 of working memory) (Maddox et al., 2004c). These findings support the dual learning system
3 / 43
89 (DLS) model that distinct neural and cognitive systems are involved in learning II and RB
90 categories.
91 Different category learning tasks recruiting distinct learning systems provides a helpful
92 framework to reason that distinct natures of category representations emerged after learning
93 (due to engaging different learning processes), and distinct mechanisms support learners to
94 generalize acquired representations to categorize new items that are different from training
95 samples in various levels of similarity. However, these predictions have not been
96 systematically tested to date, as we know, and there is no detailed description of the
97 representation nature and generalization mechanisms. In the present study, we aim to test these
98 predictions and reveal the abstraction levels of category representations of II and RB categories
99 by modeling learners‚Äô categorization responses with representational similarity models and
100 examining how well learners generalize the learned category knowledge to novel contexts.
101 Generalization and categorization require accessing our learned category knowledge to
102 guide the judgment of within- and between-category members. Types and degrees of sampling
103 variabilities affect how well we generalize the learned category knowledge. To resolve
104 generalization challenges imposed by stimulus novelty and variabilities, we must form
105 category representations abstracted from individual exemplars-specific features at appropriate
106 levels (Raviv et al., 2022). The abstract form of category representations was previously
107 considered prototypes or rules. Prototype-based theories propose that category membership is
108 determined by the distance or similarity between members and a category prototype that is
109 abstracted from individual training samples (Posner and Keele, 1968; Reed, 1972; Rosch, 1973;
110 Homa et al., 1981; Smith and Minda, 2000). Rule or regularity derived from the hypothesis-
111 testing-based learning process is another abstract form of category representations. A rule may
112 refer to a dimensional boundary (e.g., Category A consists of larger items, while Category B
113 consists of smaller ones; a decision is based on the dimension of size) or complex combinations
114 of features (simultaneous presentation of features a and b belongs to Category A, while the
115 presentation of features a and c belongs to Category B) (Erickson and Kruschke, 1998; for
116 other instantiations of RB models, please see Ashby and Maddox, 1992; Maddox and Ashby,
117 1993). RB and prototype-based hypotheses would predict that surface variations in a new
118 context are less likely to affect the generalization of these abstract representations.
119 Successful generalization may also depend on exemplar-specific representations
120 acquired from the perceptual properties of training samples (Erickson and Kruschke, 1998,
121 2002). Exemplar-based models assume that categorizing new stimuli are based on the
122 similarities between new items and previously-experienced individual exemplars in a
4 / 43
123 psychological space (Medin and Schaffer, 1978; Estes, 1986; Nosofsky, 1986, 2011).
124 Exemplar- or feature-specific category representations was emerged and considered dominant
125 in learning categories with a II structure. For example, in the visual domains, II category
126 learners performed significantly less accurately when categorizing new items sampled from
127 untrained locations in the stimulus space (Casale et al., 2012). Similarly, changes in stimulus
128 dimensions significantly affected generalization performances even though the dimensions are
129 irrelevant to categorization (Filoteo et al., 2010; Grimm and Maddox, 2013). Rosedahl et al.
130 (2018) reported a more striking finding: learners exposed to items presented in one visual field
131 failed to categorize the same items in another visual field, suggesting visual-field-dependent
132 representations emerged after learning II categories. However, other studies revealed a
133 different pattern that II category learners can generalize learned representations to categorize
134 items from untrained perceptual locations in the visual domain (Nosofsky et al., 2005; Seger et
135 al., 2015). These mixed findings suggest that the abstractness of II category representations are
136 required further investigation.
137 Here we designed novel generalization paradigms to examine the nature and
138 abstractness of representations that emerged after learning auditory categories with II and RB
139 structures. We generated well-controlled non-speech auditory category structures with the
140 same perceptual dimensions: spectral and temporal modulations. These dimensions are
141 fundamentals of complex acoustic signals like music and speech, where their representations
142 are robustly associated with the activations in the auditory cortex (Sch√∂nwiesner and Zatorre,
143 2009). We sampled sounds from two-category II and RB structures separately in the two-
144 dimensional space, where the RB structure prefers categorization using an explicit decisional
145 criterion on the spectral-modulation dimension (high vs. low), and the II structure that was
146 created by rotating the RB structure by 45¬∞ anticlockwise requires integrating information from
147 the two dimensions. Importantly, we devised four generalization tests (see Fig. 1 for a graphical
148 illustration) by sampling new sounds that were different from the training samples in different
149 degrees to probe the abstractness of the learned category knowledge. The four tests consist of
150 a baseline generalization test without inducing new variations, but the items were not exposed
151 in the training sessions; a ‚Äúfundamental frequency (F0)‚Äù test where a change in acoustic
152 dimension (i.e., F0) irrelevant to the categorization was induced; a ‚Äúnew dispersity (ND)‚Äù test
153 where the dispersity of the generalization sounds increased compared to the training samples
154 while the sampling range in the perceptual space remains unchanged; and a ‚Äúnew location (NL)‚Äù
155 test where the generalization sounds were sampled from an untrained region of the perceptual
156 space. Successful generalization to the four tests requires learners to form and transfer
5 / 43
157 representations with different abstraction levels, ranging from generalizing category
158 knowledge across items with surface acoustic differences (F0 test) to items with different
159 distributional patterns (i.e., ND test: sound dispersity; NL test: perceptual areas).
160 According to the DLS model, learning RB categories is dominated by an explicit
161 learning system associated with working memory and executive hypothesis-testing functions
162 (Chandrasekaran et al., 2014a, 2014b; Feng et al., 2021b). Thus, DLS predicts that RB category
163 representations are abstracted from individual training samples and the associated features. RB
164 learners can generalize the learned category knowledge to untrained items with different
165 variabilities. If this is the case, we would predict that RB learners perform equally well across
166 the four generalization tests. In contrast, II auditory category learning is hypothesized to be
167 dominated by an implicit learning system that maps sounds onto categories via corticostriatal
168 systems (Chandrasekaran et al., 2014a, 2014b; Feng et al., 2021b). This implicit sound-to-
169 category mapping learning is not consciously penetrable and requires accessing and integrating
170 information from multiple dimensions (Ashby et al., 2003; Maddox et al., 2007; Smith et al.,
171 2015; Rosedahl et al., 2018). Therefore, the DLS model predicts that II category knowledge
172 tightly pertains to sample-specific features and the weighting of the features in perceptual space.
173 If this hypothesis is correct, we would predict that II learners will decrease generalization
174 performances in F0, ND, and NL tests compared to the baseline test. The degree of
175 generalization decrease depends on the abstractness of II representations. If the emergent II
176 representations are strictly specific to exemplar features, any acoustic changes will affect
177 generalization performance equally. However, if the representations are not strictly exemplar-
178 specific, II learners can resist certain degrees of acoustic and perceptual changes. Thus, the
179 generalization performance will be as a function of the four tests (e.g., baseline > F0 > ND >
180 NL).
181 We conducted three auditory category learning experiments to test the DLS model and
182 the above predictions, focusing on the nature of II and RB category representations and
183 generalization mechanisms. Across three experiments, we also examined whether
184 categorization-based training (in Exp. 2.1 vs. observation-based training in Exp. 1) and
185 providing feedback (Exp. 2.2) could facilitate category learning and generalization and alter
186 the nature of emergent representations. Numerous studies showed that category training
187 procedures modulate learning outcomes (e.g., Ashby et al., 1999, 2002; Goudbeek et al., 2008,
188 2009; Levering and Kurtz, 2015). Compared to observational training, categorization training
189 could facilitate category learning by learning to attend to discriminative features among
190 categories while ignoring within-category item variabilities (Hsu and Griffiths, 2010; Levering
6 / 43
191 and Kurtz, 2015). In addition, providing immediate feedback was considered a prerequisite for
192 learning II categories (Maddox et al., 2003; Maddox and Ing, 2005). Specifically, feedback
193 facilitates performances in learning auditory and speech categories (Goudbeek et al., 2009).
194 Participants were constrained to use a sub-optimal strategy to learn categories without feedback
195 (Ashby et al., 1999). We examined to which extent the two factors augment the differences
196 between II and RB category representations. In addition to categorization accuracies averaged
197 across trials, we modeled trial-by-trial categorization responses with representational similarity
198 analysis (RSA) (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) for each generalization test to reveal the nature of
199 emergent representations of each category structure. With pre-defined representational models,
200 RSA enables us to reveal how different informational content besides category knowledge
201 emerged in generalization when facing different changes.

225 Stimulus Construction
226 Ripple sounds were generated by modulating a white noise stimulus, varying along spectral
227 and temporal modulation dimensions (duration = 500 ms, digital sampling rate = 44.1 kHz).
228 The spectral-modulation dimension ranges from 0.1 to 2 cyc/oct. The temporal-modulation
229 dimension ranges from 4 to 10 Hz (Fig. 1). We selected these modulation ranges since they are
230 strongly represented in the human auditory cortex (Sch√∂nwiesner and Zatorre, 2009) and reflect
231 the complexity of natural spectro-temporal variations in speech (Elliott and Theunissen, 2009).
232 All sounds were synthesized with in-house scripts with MATLAB (Mathworks, Natick, MA,
233 USA), and the RMS amplitude was normalized to 80 dB.
234 Two sets of ripple sounds were generated for category training based on predefined
235 two-categories II and RB structures, respectively. We created two bivariate distributions in a
236 normalized two-dimensional space along the positive diagonal to generate sounds of the II
237 categories. This normalized space ranges from 0 to 1 along spectral and temporal modulation
238 dimensions. The centers of two II categories are in the locations of [temp. = 0.39, spec. = 0.61]
239 and [temp. = 0.61, spec. = 0.39]. The standard deviation (SD) is 0.15 for the longer axis and
240 0.05 for the shorter axis with an orthogonal design. The optimal category bound is along the
241 positive diagonal (Fig. 1A upper panel, red dashed lines). We randomly sampled 480 ripple
242 sounds for the II category structure. Values along each dimension were logarithmically mapped
243 onto temporal and spectral modulation dimensions. The RB category sounds were generated
244 by rotating the II samples by 45¬∞ anticlockwise, and the centers of the two categories were
245 shifted to [temp. = 0.55, spec. = 0.35] and [temp. = 0.55, spec. = 0.65] (Fig. 1A lower panel).
246 The optimal category bound for the RB sounds perpendicular to the spectral modulation
247 dimension at 0.45 cyc/oct (Fig. 1A lower panel, red dashed lines).

284 Training and generalization procedures
285 We conducted three experiments by manipulating the training procedure to examine whether
286 categorization and providing feedback could facilitate category learning and generalization. In
287 Exp. 1, participants were asked to learn to associate category labels with sounds via
288 observational training (OT) procedure (Fig. 2A, left panel). For each training trial, a fixation
289 cross was presented for 200 ms, followed by a category label (i.e., ‚ÄúÁ±ªÂà´ 1‚Äù or ‚ÄúÁ±ªÂà´ 2‚Äù)
290 displayed on the screen. One second after the category label appeared, a sound was presented
291 for 500 ms. Participants were instructed to learn the associations between sounds and category
292 labels without making any categorization response. After each trial, a jittered inter-trial interval
293 (randomly sampling between 1 and 2 seconds) was added to minimize the effects of temporal
294 prediction. Each participant completed eight blocks of OT. Each training block consists of 60
295 trials.
296 Participants were asked to complete the four generalization tests twice, one
297 immediately following training and another one week after (Fig. 2B). The CT was always
298 performed first to minimize the influence of the other tests. The order of the other three tests
299 (i.e., F0, ND, and NL) was counterbalanced across participants. Each generalization test
300 consists of two blocks (60 trials/block). The training and testing were conducted in a sound-
301 attenuated booth using E-Prime software (Psychology Software Tools, Inc., Sharpsburg, PA,
302 USA). The sounds were presented over headphones (Sennheiser HD280 pro) at a comfortable
303 listening level.

326 Representation modeling
327 Although categorization accuracy provides an overall performance for each generalization test,
328 it does not tell us how sound-by-sound variabilities in response patterns and category
329 confusions link with category knowledge acquired in training. Therefore, we employed a
330 representational similarity analysis (RSA) approach (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008; Feng et al.,
331 2021c) to examine the degree of category representations applied to each test and whether there
332 are additional sampling-related information emerged in aid of generalization. We calculated
333 representation similarities between three pre-defined representation models and categorization
334 response confusions across learner groups and experiments.

343 We constructed three representation models (see Fig. 3A) based on binary category
344 labels, bound-based decision distance, and center-based perceptual similarity from the training
345 samples to uncover the nature of emergent representations in generalization. These models
346 reflect three types of category-related information that the learners could acquire during
347 learning. These models are operationally defined by representational dissimilarity matrices
348 (RDMs) to fit the behavioral response RDMs derived from trial-by-trial categorization
349 response patterns (Fig. 3B).
350 The category model (and the corresponding RDM) represents abstract category labels
351 and is designed to have only two values: 0 if two sounds are from the same category or 1 if
352 they are from different categories. The bound-based decision distance model was created based
353 on the Euclidean distance between each sound and the optimal category bound in the
354 normalized spectral-temporal-modulation space (Fig. 3A). The center-based perceptual
355 similarity model was created based on the Euclidean distance between the center of each
356 category and every sound in the 2-dimensional space. The following equation was used to

679 Discussion
680 Learners‚Äô ability to generalize to novel testing situations directly speaks to the nature of
681 representations acquired in previous category training and the mechanisms supporting cross-
682 situation transfer of category knowledge. Here, with this experimental logic, we designed four
683 generalization tests with different types of novel items and conducted three category learning
684 experiments with different training procedures to probe the differences in representation nature
685 between II and RB categories. Across three experiments, we found distinct characteristics of
686 the newly-acquired II and RB category representations indicated by generalization patterns and
687 representation modeling. II and RB learners transferred category knowledge to the four tests to
688 different extents. The II learners significantly decreased generalization performances and
689 category representations in the ND and NL tests but not in the F0 test compared to the baseline
24 / 43
690 test, which suggests that the generalization of II category knowledge is not affected by surface
691 acoustic changes in an irrelevant F0 dimension but less successful in categorizing new items
692 sampled from an untrained perceptual area (i.e., the NL test) and in a context with more
693 disperse samples (i.e., the ND test). In contrast, RB learners‚Äô generalizations are resistant to
694 changes in perceptual regions (i.e., the NL test) and interference of the F0 change (especially
695 for the representations after stabilization, i.e., one week after training), but they are sensitive to
696 changes in increasing dispersity of exemplars (i.e., the ND test). Representational similarity
697 modeling further revealed that generalization in the ND test was accomplished by II and RB
698 learners with different strategies. II learners enhanced representations of perceptual similarity
699 and decision distance to compensate for the decreased transfer of category representations,
700 whereas RB learners used a more computational cost strategy by default, computing the
701 decision-bound distance to guide categorization decisions. These findings provide new insights
702 into understanding the relationships between category learning, the nature of category
703 representations, and how different learning and representation systems operate differently to
704 achieve successful generalization.
705 We aim to examine the representation nature of II and RB categories, especially the
706 abstractness of the category representations utilized in generalization. We manipulated three
707 factors in generalization, ranging from low-level surface acoustics to higher-level exemplar
708 dispersity and untrained perceptual location. Based on DLS models, especially in the visual
709 domains, II representations are tightly linked to the exemplar-dependent or feature-specific
710 information (e.g., visual field), and any changes in the low-level information affect
711 generalization performances (Ashby et al., 2003; Maddox et al., 2004b, 2005, 2007; Rosedahl
712 et al., 2018). However, across the three experiments, we demonstrate that II learners can resist
713 changes in F0 for generalization tests performed immediately and one week after training,
714 suggesting that auditory II category representations are not tightly linked to low-level
715 dimensions irrelevant to discriminating category members. Changes in F0 analog changes in
716 talker (e.g., Bradlow and Bent, 2008; Xie et al., 2021). This finding is consistent with previous
717 auditory and speech category learning studies that showed talker-independent category
718 representations emerged at both behavioral and neural levels during training (Bradlow and Bent,
719 2008; Feng et al., 2019, 2021c; Johnson and Sjerps, 2021; Xie et al., 2021). In speech
720 perception, native listeners can maintain perceptual constancy while facing various types of
721 acoustic and perceptual variabilities (Bradlow and Bent, 2008; Feng et al., 2018, 2021a),
722 suggesting exceptional generalization ability to overcome the ‚Äúlack-of-invariance‚Äù challenge.
25 / 43
723 In comparison, RB learners‚Äô generalization performances were not consistently affected
724 by F0 changes. The generalization performances in the immediate F0 test were significantly
725 less accurate than the control test, especially for Exp. 1 with observational training. However,
726 the generalization was insensitive to F0 changes one week after training across the three
727 experiments. These findings suggest that RB learners may undergo a transformation or
728 abstraction process within a week after training to stabilize the category representations to
729 overcome the variability induced by an irrelevant acoustic dimension. One speculation is that
730 this post-training abstraction or stabilization process is specific for RB categories, where the
731 F0 information could be fastly integrated into the previously-acquired categorization rules so
732 that the irrelevant acoustic information is eliminated before the decision (e.g., application of
733 new filtering rules). Adopting a new rule to filter irrelevant information while maintaining
734 attention to the relevant dimensions or features may require time and the support of executive
735 systems (Diekelmann and Born, 2010; Genzel et al., 2014). The distinct patterns between II
736 and RB learners in the F0 test suggest that the category structure we learn plays an important
737 role in how the auditory perception system copes with acoustic variabilities.
738 One of the critical tests for the abstractness of emerged representations is to test the
739 generalization to new sounds sampled from an untrained area of the stimulus space. We found
740 that significantly reduced generalization performances and decreased category model fittings
741 to new sounds outside the range of training samples (i.e., in the new location test) for II learners
742 across the three experiments. In contrast, increased generalization performances and enhanced
743 category representations for the RB learners were found in the same test. This dissociated
744 pattern suggests that II representations are tightly restricted to the perceptual areas where the
745 training samples are covered. The trained locations in the stimulus space may be weighted
746 differently than untrained areas, so the categorization performances are compromised when the
747 category knowledge is transferred from trained locations to untrained locations. These results
748 are consistent with previous findings from studies of visual category learning. For example,
749 Smith and colleagues (2015) asked two groups of participants to learn RB and II categories,
750 respectively, and required them to categorize new stimuli sampled from an untrained region of
751 the stimulus space. They found that RB learners‚Äô generalization was nearly seamless, whereas
752 II learners failed to categorize the new items. Moreover, II representations are often linked with
753 exemplar-associated sensorimotor features. Changes in the visual field have significant
754 interferences of II categorization but not RB (Rosedahl et al., 2018), suggesting visual-field-
755 dependent representations of II categories. Similarly, changing the response key to categorize
756 items only affects the II categorization but not RB (Ashby et al., 2003), suggesting that visual
26 / 43
757 II categories are tightly associated with their motor correspondences. Comparing these findings
758 from visual domains, our findings in auditory category learning indicate that II auditory
759 category representations are only tightly linked to the perceptual properties of the dimensions
760 relevant to categorization instead of any other irrelevant dimensions (e.g., F0) inherent from
761 the training samples. This representation mechanism of II auditory categories may help
762 listeners precisely generalize category knowledge to novel items and scenarios with redundant
763 and irrelevant acoustic information.
764 Another critical test of the representation nature is to examine the generalization
765 performances to new sounds with distinct distributional patterns from the training samples. We
766 found that reduced categorization accuracy and decreased category representations when
767 learners generalized to a set of new sounds with a more dispersed distribution (i.e., the ND test)
768 compared to the training samples for both II and RB learners. These findings indicate that both
769 II and RB category representations are sensitive to the within-category sound distributional
770 pattern. DLS models predict that learning II and RB categories recruit distinct learning
771 strategies and neurocognitive systems (Ashby and Maddox, 2005, 2011; Chandrasekaran et al.,
772 2014a, 2014b). However, DLS models do not have detailed descriptions and predictions about
773 whether the category representations are associated with the within-category distributional
774 statists of the training samples. DLS model may predict that the generalization of II category
775 representations is affected by changes in sound dispersity because II category representations
776 are hypothesized to link with sample-specific features. This reasoning is supported by recent
777 findings that II but not RB categories with a probabilistic distribution are learned worse than
778 categories with a deterministic distribution (Roark and Holt, 2018), suggesting II category
779 representations are formed based on or along with distributional information. Any changes in
780 the stimulus distributional pattern would affect generalization performances. Even though the
781 overall coverage in the perceptual space is the same for the training and generalization samples,
782 the distributional patterns within each category are different. The training samples are normally
783 distributed in the space (more samples in the middle of the distribution), whereas the
784 generalization samples in the ND test are more distributed. Thus, our results suggest that
785 forming II category representations is a learning process that considers all types of variabilities
786 in the perceptual space. Any changes in sample distribution or the locations in the perceptual
787 space, whether within or outside the coverage of training samples, would affect the transfer of
788 the II category knowledge.
789 A perceptual weighting mechanism may explain the II generalization decreases in the
790 ND and NL tests (Niv et al., 2015). Successful learning of II auditory categories requires
27 / 43
791 integrating information from the spectral- and temporal-modulation dimensions. The
792 integration ratio of the two dimensions is subject to the distributions of training samples. The
793 distribution of the training samples provides statistical information about the category
794 probabilities for the learners to weigh the two dimensions. Learners may gradually adjust the
795 integration ratio or weights of the two dimensions as they expose to more samples. Higher
796 perceptual weights would be assigned to the perceptual areas more frequently encountering
797 samples. Transferring this weighting knowledge from trained areas to untrained areas (e.g., in
798 the NL test) or from more trained areas to less trained areas (e.g., in the ND test) in the stimulus
799 space would be less successful because these weighting representations are acquired with the
800 procedure-based learning system and tapped into the auditory cortex. In addition, sounds from
801 untrained perceptual areas may not be weighted the same way in the stimulus space as the
802 trained sounds, which may lead to incorrect categorization. This speculation is consistent with
803 our recent neuroimaging findings. Feng and colleagues (2021) examined the neural encodings
804 of II and RB auditory categories during categorization training. They found that auditory and
805 speech perception-related regions in the superior temporal cortex gradually increased the
806 weightings of perceptual information for II sounds. In contrast, the same regions gradually
807 decreased the perceptual representations for RB sounds. This putative perceptual weighting
808 mechanism of II representations requires further examination.
809 For the RB categories, DLS models may predict that changes in perceptual locations or
810 sample distributions in generalization would not affect categorization performances. However,
811 we found that categorizing RB sounds in generalization was affected by changes in
812 distributional patterns but not in locations of the perceptual space. These unexpected findings
813 are inconsistent with the prediction of DLS models and findings from recent studies (Maddox
814 et al., 2005; Roark and Holt, 2018). RB category representations have been considered an
815 abstract form of rules associated with executive functions and working memory capacity
816 (Waldron and Ashby, 2001; Zeithamova and Maddox, 2006; DeCaro et al., 2008; Reetzke et
817 al., 2016). Sample dispersity is not directly related to the execution of these functions. One
818 possibility of our findings is that the categorization rules are derived from the statistical
819 distribution of the training samples. Learners abstract the regularities of acoustic and perceptual
820 information from the distributional patterns of the samples via hypothesis-testing processes.
821 Transferring the sample-specific regularities to a new sample set with a significantly different
822 dispersity in the perceptual space would affect the categorization performances. Another
823 possibility may relate to the RB generalization mechanism. Dispersity of the samples relates to
824 the accuracy in computing the sound-to-boundary decision distance. More distributed sounds
28 / 43
825 within each category (i.e., the ND test) may cause a less accurate estimation of the decision
826 distance between each sound and a putative category bound. Therefore, the less accurate
827 bound-distance estimation may affect the categorization decision in the ND test compared to
828 the control test. This possibility assumes that the decision-distance estimation based on a
829 category bound is a necessary categorization process during generalization. This assumption is
830 supported by our representational similarity modeling results showing that the decision-
831 distance model significantly correlates with trial-by-trial category response confusions (see Fig.
832 S2 in supplementary). The model fits did not significantly change between generalization tests
833 (see Fig. 8), suggesting that the estimation of decision distance is a default step in
834 generalization. Future studies are needed to investigate the two possibilities systematically.
835 Regarding the training procedure, we did not find any significant differences in
836 generalization accuracy or representational model fit across the three experiments. In addition,
837 the training procedure did not affect the differences between II and RB in generalization
838 performances. These findings suggest that categorization tasks and providing immediate
839 feedback compared to the observation training may not additionally facilitate learning and
840 generalization accuracies for both category structures. In contrast to the generalization
841 accuracy and representation modeling, we found that categorization training with feedback (i.e.,
842 Exp. 2.2) facilitated learners‚Äô categorization response time across generalization tests (Fig. S3
843 in supplementary), while this facilitation is not a tradeoff of categorization accuracies. These
844 findings indicate that categorization training with feedback may facilitate learners‚Äô
845 categorization decision processes, such as more efficiency in accumulating information for
846 category decisions compared to the observation training. Providing feedback and presenting
847 category labels may play a similar role for RB‚Äôs hypothesis-testing and II‚Äôs associative learning
848 processes, except that feedback-based training recruits reinforcement learning mechanisms to
849 learn sound-to-category mappings (Ashby and Maddox, 2005). The feedback-dependent
850 reinforcement learning may facilitate the formation of many-to-one corticostriatal projections
851 from the auditory and prefrontal cortex to the striatum regions (Chandrasekaran et al., 2014a,
852 2014b; Maddox and Chandrasekaran, 2014; Feng et al., 2021b). The reinforcement-based
853 mechanism may facilitate the retrieval of the category knowledge acquired during feedback-
854 based categorization training, thus enhancing the decision processing in categorization. More
855 investigations are needed to test this possibility.
857 Summary and conclusion
29 / 43
858 We examined the nature of II and RB auditory category representations by accessing the ability
859 of learners to generalize new category knowledge to novel testing situations. Four
860 generalization tests with various novel items were designed, and three category learning
861 experiments with different training procedures were conducted to compare the characteristics
862 of newly acquired II and RB category knowledge. II learners demonstrated decreased
863 generalization performance and category representation when generalizing to untrained
864 perceptual areas and in contexts with more dispersed samples; on the other hand, RB learners
865 showed resistance to changes in perceptual regions but sensitivity to increasing dispersity of
866 sounds. II and RB learners used different strategies for generalization when facing the more
867 variable context, with II learners enhancing representations of perceptual similarity and
868 decision distance while RB learners employed a more computational cost strategy to guide
869 categorization decisions. These findings shed light on the relationship between category
870 learning and representation nature and the varying strategies used by different learning and
871 representation systems to achieve successful generalization.
