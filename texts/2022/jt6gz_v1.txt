Abstract
Our social environment influences our affective states and decisions, which was quite evident
during COVID-19 pandemic-imposed quarantine. However, little is understood about recovery
from social deprivation, especially for those with high trait anxiety. To understand this, we
gathered ~24000 momentary assessments of affect and social interactions over three days of
isolation followed by seven days of recovery from 116 young adults returning to a university
campus. Quarantine induced a greater desire for social interactions and post-quarantine,
individuals attempted to make up for this in different ways. While individuals with high trait
anxiety experienced greater stress, negative affect, and lower positive affect post-quarantine,
they benefited more in the company of people ‚Äúclose‚Äù to them, and experienced greater reduction
in negative affect from positive interactions. In contrast, low trait anxious individuals
experienced less stress and, after quarantine, engaged more with distant companions, leading to
increased positive affect. As an objective measure, we also studied how affective states impacted
decision-making through a virtual patch-foraging task played during and after quarantine.
Affective states reported in the last 24 hours significantly impacted participants' near-optimal
strategies to maximize rewards in the task. Participants with high fluctuation in affective states
tended to over-harvest, while those with high average negative affect tended to under-harvest.
Our results highlight how social deprivation impacts affective states as a function of trait anxiety
and social interactions and their effects on reward-based decision-making.
Sig nificance statement
The COVID-19 pandemic imposed quarantine has been indicated to affect subjective well-being
and decision-making, but the underlying processes during such social deprivation has not been
explicitly studied. This study used momentary assessment to reveal the impact of the social
environment on affective states during and after quarantine. Our analysis revealed the differential
role of social interactions in regulating momentary experience for high and low trait anxiety
individuals. A virtual patch-foraging task played during these constraints uncovered the role of
affective states in decision-making. Our findings highlight the factors governing recovery from
social isolation and lend causal value to our inferences regarding how social environment
impacts momentary experiences, advancing future research aimed at supporting individuals'
well-being during times of crisis.
Introduction
Humans are social animals and have evolved to thrive in environments that foster social
connections and interactions (1‚Äì4). While social interactions have a profound impact on
individual behavior, mood (5, 6), and decision-making (7‚Äì9), influencing both physical and
mental health, as well as overall well-being (10‚Äì15), lack of it can lead to subjective feelings of
loneliness and isolation. Chronic social isolation can even increase the risk of depression and
anxiety. Loneliness is also a significant source of psychological stress that is associated with a
range of negative mental health outcomes, including reduced positive emotions, depression and
suicidal behaviour (16‚Äì20). And these conditions were exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic
that disrupted daily routines, led to social isolation due to quarantine regimens and increased
loneliness across all age groups (21‚Äì23). Fear of contracting the virus, disruptions in daily
routines, lack of social interactions, and a high level of uncertainty about the future and
educational prospects contributed to increased levels of anxiety and mental health issues in the
population (24‚Äì28). This study aims to investigate the impact of quarantine-related constraints
and social deprivation on the emotional well-being of young adults. Specifically, we sought to
answer two key questions: how did social isolation impact the affective states of the participants
during quarantine? And, if so, how did the removal of constraints post quarantine reset their
affective states?
The conventional cross-sectional sampling approach consisting of one sample during quarantine
and one sample post quarantine, could suffer from retrospective bias and neglect temporal
relationships in the affective states. Moreover, these samples may not be able to account for the
dynamic changes in the social environment (29). To this end, we used experience sampling, also
known as ecological momentary assessment (EMA), with the help of smartphone technology.
Furthermore, mental health conditions at the outset, even before quarantine, can profoundly
impact the quarantine and post quarantine experience. We particularly focused on trait anxiety,
assessed using the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory for Adults (30), that profoundly impacts the
affective states. High trait anxious individuals frequently go through negative emotional
experiences and show increased risk of developing depression, anxiety, and related disorders
(31‚Äì34). Here we had the opportunity to examine how trait anxious individuals not only handled
the social isolation phase but also how they recovered from it. We hypothesized that even though
individuals with high trait anxiety would experience more negative affect than those with low
trait anxiety, both during quarantine and after quarantine, high anxious individuals may benefit
more from the resumption of social interactions. Quarantine imposition and its subsequent
removal lends some causal value to the inferences.
We were also interested in examining how momentary affective states impacted decision-making
in real-life contexts. A growing body of research has examined the impact of affect, emotions,
and mood on decision-making (35‚Äì39). To this end, we used a virtual patch-foraging game,
during and after quarantine, to understand the impact of affective states on decision making (40,
41). Foraging is a naturalistic decision-making process where individuals must balance
exploration for new resources with exploitation of known resources (42, 43). This
exploration-exploitation dilemma has been extensively studied in the context of patch foraging,
where exploiting a known area with preferred food or rewards is typically energy-efficient, but
exploring new areas becomes more efficient when resources become scarce or unreliable. To
remain efficient, foraging requires an adaptive strategy that balances the exploration-exploitation
trade-off. Internal states such as mood-related arousal and valence can impact this trade-off (44,
45). Positive-going mood states have been shown to favour exploratory behaviour (46), while
negative-going mood states potentially result in overexploitation (44). Here, we investigated
whether self-reported momentary affect via EMA surveys prior to game play impacted foraging
decisions.
To address these questions, we recruited young adults who were quarantined on the institute
campus as per the established protocols. Once they tested negative for the COVID-19 virus, they
were allowed to leave and reside in their respective hostels. We monitored their mood and social
interactions during the 3-day quarantine period and the subsequent 7-day post-quarantine period.
During this period, we collected survey data on momentary affect, social interactions, and
stressful and positive events experienced 4 times a day. Participants played the foraging game
once in quarantine and once post release.
Results
Participants
The current study was conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic on college students who
returned to campus after a long lockdown. Upon arrival, they were quarantined for 3-4 days
before being released if they tested negative for COVID virus. Overall, after applying the
exclusion criteria, we investigated the effects of trait anxiety and quarantine on 87 young adults
who reported their mood and social interactions over 21000 momentary assessments over a
10-day period, including the days in quarantine.

Trait anxiety, social situations and momentary affect

Leave-one-subject-out cross-validation
After determining the factors that influenced positive and negative affect, we sought to evaluate
their ability to predict an individual's momentary affect. To this end, we created linear regression
models that included various predictor variables and their combinations as fixed effects. Using
these, we attempted to predict the level of positive or negative affect (between 0-4). We used the
leave-one-subject-out cross-validation method to evaluate the models' predictive performance,
for positive and negative affect separately. First, trait anxiety and pleasantness from positive
events were found to be notable predictors of positive affect. While trait anxiety increased the
accuracy to 33.22% (sensitivity = 0.21, specificity = 0.80), pleasantness from positive events led
to a prediction accuracy of 34.58% (sensitivity = 0.22, specificity = 0.81), see Fig. 3a. The full
model including frequency of close and distant interactions, social context, and unpleasantness,
increased the prediction accuracy to 37.73% (sensitivity = 0.26, specificity = 0.82), even though
these predictors by themselves had a limited effect on positive affect. AIC values decreased from
5376.81 for the trait anxiety model to 5061.55 for the pleasantness model. It further went down
to 4857.25 for the models including trait anxiety, pleasantness, and multiple predictors,
respectively. Additionally, we performed 1000 shuffles-based control experiment for the
prediction of positive affect and found that the accuracy of the full model decreased to 32.18%
(sensitivity = 0.20, specificity = 0.80), indicating that the unshuffled classifier was perhaps
picking up on relevant structures in the data.
Similarly, trait anxiety was a significant predictor of negative affect, with an accuracy of 41.89%
(sensitivity = 0.26, specificity = 0.84), as shown in Fig. 3a. When combined with unpleasantness
of stressful events, the prediction of negative affect improved with an accuracy of 48.02%
(sensitivity = 0.32, specificity = 0.85). However, the full model, which included multiple
predictors such as close and distant interactions, social context, and pleasantness, did not lead to
a drastic improvement in predicting negative affect as compared to the model with the
combination of trait anxiety and unpleasantness: accuracy increased to 48.12% (sensitivity =
0.32, specificity = 0.85). AIC values for the models were 5252.97, 4871.72, and 4874.44 for the
models including trait anxiety, trait anxiety and unpleasantness, and the full model, respectively.
Models that used shuffled samples could only predict negative affect with 24.77% accuracy
(sensitivity = 0.20, specificity = 0.80). Overall, the classifier analysis also reinforced the earlier
results that highlighted the impact of trait anxiety and the social context on momentary
well-being.

Discussion
The present findings illustrate the impact of the environment on the social interactions of
individuals, highlighting the role of social factors in shaping how individuals engage and interact
with others. In this study, we examined 24000 momentary social and affective experiences for 10
consecutive days in 116 young adults, who spent 3-4 days in quarantine and 7 days outside. The
quarantine period significantly impacted their social interactions and led to social deprivation,
which was reflected in the increased need for social interaction. However, after quarantine, there
was a decrease in the desire to socialize. There were fewer interactions with close companions,
while interactions with distant acquaintances increased.
The study provides ecologically valid evidence of expression of trait anxiety in daily lives of
young adults in three important ways. First, the findings replicate and expand on previous EMA
research demonstrating the negative impact of high trait anxiety levels on individuals‚Äô quality of
momentary experience (53). Individuals with high trait anxiety experienced high levels of
negative affect and low levels of positive affect. Further they reported higher stressful events in
comparison to individuals with low trait anxiety. These results corroborate prior research linking
higher levels of trait anxiety with greater exposure and reactivity to social stressors (54‚Äì57). It is
interesting to note that work-related events were the most prevalent source of stress for
individuals with both low and high trait anxiety, but this was not the case for other stressors (see
Second, this study provides novel evidence regarding the impact of trait anxiety on social
interactions, a finding that expands upon our current understanding of the relationship between
trait anxiety and social behaviour. Previous research has shown that individuals with high levels
of trait anxiety may experience negative outcomes in social relationships, such as reduced social
support and perceived social connectedness. However, they have suggested little to no effect of
trait anxiety on social interaction frequency (53, 58) and social network size or density (59‚Äì66).
Our study revealed that individuals with high trait anxiety had significantly fewer interactions
with distant companions compared to individuals with low trait anxiety. These findings are
relevant and highlight the importance of considering the impact of trait anxiety on social
interactions and well-being. Future research may further explore the relationship between trait
anxiety and social behavior, and how interventions aimed at reducing trait anxiety may positively
impact social interactions and ultimately, mental health outcomes.
Third, the current findings expand the literature highlighting the role of social and interpersonal
processes in emotion regulation of trait anxious individuals (67), emphasizing the importance of
pleasant events and experiences in this process as well. Our study reveals that individuals with
higher trait anxiety heavily rely on their close companions to regulate their distress, evident from
the significant positive affect derived from interactions with close companions depicted in Fig.
2f. This trend strongly aligns with previous studies (47, 53, 68), underscoring the influence of
trait anxiety on individuals' responses to social partners and feelings of connection. Additionally,
our findings highlight the significant role of positive events in effectively alleviating negative
affect for individuals with high trait anxiety, as they experienced a more pronounced reduction in
negative affect associated with the pleasantness of positive events. These insights shed light on
the impact of trait anxiety on momentary experiences, as well as the experiences of positive
events and social interactions in everyday life.
Furthermore, in contrast to the traditional emphasis on understanding and reducing negative
mood states, the current study examines both positive and negative aspects of an individual's
well-being holistically by allowing participants to report both stressful and pleasant daily events
in order to assess their effects on the present moment. Work-related events were a source of both
stress and positive experiences, emphasizing the significance of addressing both aspects to
maintain mental well-being during quarantine.
We also examined the influence of momentary affective states on explore-exploit decision
making by using a patch foraging task. Patch foraging offers an ecologically valid way of
assessing the impact of affective states on decision making (42, 44). While participants were near
optimal in their strategy to maximize reward rate, by engaging in near-optimal number of
harvests per patch, as per the Marginal Value Theorem, their affective states in the last 24 hours
significantly impacted gameplay (see Fig. 4d). Negative affective states reduced the number of
harvests, perhaps because of reduced motivation to engage. Increased fluctuations in affective
states led to overharvesting consistent with earlier work (44, 69). These findings suggest that
momentary affect influenced decision making in ecologically valid settings. The scalability of
this approach offers promising applications in mental health research, as it allows for the
objective tracking of individuals' mental health over time using the decision-making patterns
within the game as a reliable indicator (70).
The present study is not without its limitations. The generalisability of this study is somewhat
limited by the demography of the study participants and social environment: our study
population was primarily post-graduate students aged 22 to 26. Assuming a large sample size
and diversity among participants, it may be reasonable to generalize the findings to other young
adults. In terms of the social environment, the research was carried out during the COVID-19
pandemic, which was a one-time event. The current study aimed to investigate the effects of
quarantine as causal manipulation of social interactions during the COVID-19 pandemic. While
the study examined mood and affective states during and after quarantine, it lacks the data from
the participants in normal everyday life from before quarantine. Moreover, the participants were
students coming back to campus and happy to be back with their friends. The aforementioned
limitations underscore the need for future research to address these issues and to explore the
effects of quarantine on causal manipulation in a more comprehensive and balanced manner.
Methods
This study was carried out during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2021, when the cases were on the
decline following its second wave in India. Participants were randomly and independently
recruited via email from students returning to college residence in 7 batches over a 4-month
period after the lockdown (see Fig. 1a). All participants provided informed consent and filled an
online baseline survey administered through Qualtrics. The baseline surveys consisted of Trait
anxiety survey (Y2) from the State and Trait Anxiety Inventory for Adults (30), 50-item IPIP
(International Personality Item Pool) version of the Big Five markers (71), General Self-efficacy
scale (72), Perceived Stress Scale (73), along with other questions assessing sleep quality and
feelings of loneliness in general. In total, we had 116 participants; 79 of them identified as males,
36 as females, and 1 preferred not to disclose their gender. Upon arrival, they were quarantined
for 3-4 days by the institute, and if they tested negative for COVID-19 at the end of quarantine,
they were allowed to move back to their residence hostels1. During the study period we used a
smartphone-based ecological momentary assessment (EMA) to examine the effect of trait
anxiety on mood and interaction dynamics of young adults during and after quarantine in this
study. The study was approved by the IIT Kanpur Institute Human Ethics Committee.

Data collection. Ethica app (Ethica Data Services, Inc.'s proprietary application) was used to
send 50 automated in-app surveys over a 10-day period for ecological momentary assessment
(EMA). The app provided a dashboard where participants could view their pending surveys and
used push notifications to encourage participants to complete the surveys. Participants responded
to four routine surveys (see Fig. 1c for prompt timings) and one sleep quality related survey, a
total of 5 surveys, everyday. Each routine survey was active for 90 minutes. An hour after the
initial notification, the app sent a reminder to the participants if the survey was still pending. The
sleep survey was sent daily at 6 AM and could be completed any time within the following 24
hours. Prior to the study, a training video was shared with participants familiarizing them with
the study procedures and the survey app. They were also instructed on survey frequency and
duration and were asked to enable push notifications for the Ethica app in order to receive survey
notifications on time. Participants were advised to avoid completing the surveys when under the
influence of alcohol. To maximize compliance in the study, a reminder notification to complete
the survey was provided 30 minutes before the deadline. We also provided the participants with a
24/7 technical support number in case they had any questions or concerns. Except participants of
B1, others were compensated with Rs. 30 for filling the baseline survey with the expectation that
it would increase their compliance during the study. Participants in the study could earn up to Rs.
740 as a reward, which depended on completing baseline surveys, EMA surveys and playing
foraging tasks. Participants were paid a fraction of Rs. 290 for answering EMA surveys
depending on their response rate of answering EMA surveys (Rs. 290 * response rate) and an
additional completion bonus of Rs. 150 (90% > response rate >= 70%) or Rs. 250 (response rate
> 90%). Playing the game once as per instructions (i.e., to play the game within the quarantine or
non-quarantine setting) earned them Rs. 70.

Statistical analyses. All data analyses were conducted in R. All statistical tests were two-sided,
and P < 0.05 were considered statistically significant unless otherwise mentioned. The lmerTest
package was employed to construct linear mixed effects models and evaluate the significance of
model effects using 5000 bootstrap samples (74, 75). The moderation-mediation based
regressions were implemented using the bruceR package, which employs other packages such as
mediate, interaction, and the PROCESS macro (76‚Äì78). A Monte Carlo approach with 5,000
samples was used to assess the significance of indirect effects (79) in mediation analyses. The
momentary assessment data was nested within participants, and intercepts could vary between
them. Moment-level predictors were mean-centered separately across participants (80). For
illustrative purposes, conditional effects are depicted for extreme values (¬±1 standard deviation)
of trait-anxiety. Figures were produced using Python (version 3.8.11) with the help of Matplotlib
(version 3.5.1) (81) and seaborn (version 0.12.2) (82).

Model validation. We confirmed the applicability of the models through Monte Carlo
simulations of the indirect effects and leave-one-person-out cross-validation (LOOCV). We
tested the statistical significance of each predictor variable in the final moderation-mediation
models of positive and negative affect in a manner similar to mixed stepwise variable selection
(see model equations in the Supplementary Information: Leave-one-out-cross-validation
models). For cross-validation, one participant was set aside as a test set, while the rest comprised
the training set. For this test-training set combination, accuracy, sensitivity, specificity were
calculated. This procedure was repeated 87 times (87 being the total number of participants),
leaving out a different participant from the training set each time. The model's metrics were then
computed by averaging over all repetitions. Finally, this process was repeated for each model,
and their aggregate metrics were used to compare them. However, the dependent variable
consisted of ordinal affect ratings ranging from 0-4, whereas the regression models above
predicted continuous variables. To obtain a new predicted ordinal variable ranging from 0-4, we
rounded the predicted values to the nearest integer. This new predicted variable was used to
compute the aforementioned model metrics.
Foraging game
Paradigm. To understand how momentary affect influences decision-making, we administered a
modified version of the computer-based patch foraging game (see Fig. 4a) (40‚Äì42, 83).
Participants were advised to play the game on their computers in the full-screen mode. They
were explicitly told that the berry collection in the game is directly related to the monetary bonus
they would receive at the end of the study, and thus, were instructed to maximize berry collection
in the game. In every trial (see schematic in Fig. 4a), participants could ‚Äòstay‚Äô in a patch to
harvest berries from it or ‚Äòleave‚Äô to move to the next patch. To either harvest the current bush or
move to the next one, participants had to hold the mouse cursor over a hollow fixation square
which appeared at the center of the orange path and wait for 1 sec for it to get filled with green
color to make any decision. If participants moved the pointer out of the square before it turned
green, the square would become hollow again and they had to move the cursor to the fixation
square again to make any decision. Once the fixation square turned entirely green, they could
move the cursor to the current patch or to a hollow square placed horizontally on the opposite
side of the patch to leave the current patch. If the participant chose to stay and harvest again,
reward associated with the berries harvested was displayed near the patch 500 ms after the cursor
reached the patch. If the participant chose to leave for the next patch by selecting the hollow
square after the fixation was successful, the patches moved downwards, mimicking the forward
movement of the participant. Travel time to the next patch was set to 3 sec or 10 sec depending
on the block.
